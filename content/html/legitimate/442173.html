The Nature of Justice

June 4, 2012

Written by legaleagle7

Comments Off on The Nature of Justice


Themis!

This image apparently is the Angel of justice, and it has been shown in many departments of law. However, I am opposed to this picture because the sword is the symbol of violence. Social studies  show such  people  commit crimes.     Themis is armed with a sword and scales. Lady Justice (Latin: Justitia , the Roman goddess of Justice , the equivalent of the Greek goddess Nike ) is an allegorical personification of the moral force in judicial systems. [ 1 ] Modern penal systems do not agree with punishment. They suggest reform and rehabilitation. So we have to educate people, not punish them. The sword belonged to Nemesis,  who was responsible for revenge and retribution, but the periods of  revenge and barbarity were abolished a long time ago.

1- I object to the blindfold because justice has the ability to recognize who is guilty and who is not. Also, if Themis looks into the faces of people, she will understand better than if she is blindfolded.

2- I object to the blindfold because according to Wikipedia there are some coins   from Roman times that show that Justitia was not blindfolded.

3- Object to the blindfold, because atop the old bialy court house inLondon, a statue of lady justice stands without a blindfold, the court house brochures explain that “This is because lady justice was originally not blindfolded and because her “maidenly form” is supposed to guarantee her  impartiality which renders the blindfold redundant !!

4 I object to the fact that Lady  Justice is a goddess. Oranos is her father, so she represents honesty , purity, and equality. It is  not fair that she is blindfolded.

Besides, she is credited with wisdom and powerful foresight or prophecy, knowing unknown secrets, so that such specifics  discharge her from bias.

Now , the question is  this: For how long do we have to watch a silly goddess with a ridiculous blindfold? and for how long  do we  have to suffer the arbitrary creations of sculptors ? so let us start to challenge them.

Saying Goodbye to the Old Year – Welcoming in the New Year

January 1, 2012

Written by Alex

Comments Off on Saying Goodbye to the Old Year – Welcoming in the New Year


So, we did it again – a third party.

And this time without any help at all from KONO Italia. No more parties in that place! No more classes in that place!

If you were happy with that place, I’m sorry – but I will not go back there – not for English class, not for Parties, not for KONO pizza cones. They don’t know it, I hope, but their forgetting our party was the end of our time together. They said goodbye to us by forgetting us. I’m a nice, gentle, understanding person – but I have a huge, huge memory. And I will not forget that they forgot a party that they and I discussed a hundred times.

So we left them on December 30 and if I have anything at all to say about it I will not be going back. They might try to apologize but I don’t want to hear it. With the attitude they have in that place, they’ll be closed in 6 months.

So December 30 we had our party elsewhere. It wasn’t the same, but it wasn’t uninteresting. New friendships were made, I’m sure, and that’s the important point. Yi Xiao got her Wilson R. Thornley Award. That was the main goal of the party. We took care of that at Cafe Depot on the corner of Mackay and Demaisonneuve. We spent a happy hour there. At 8 o’clock they closed and we upped our tents and went over to the Chinese restaurant next door. I don’t know the name of the place. I call it “nouilles” or “noodles” but it must have a name. No matter. I recommended the “aigre-piquante” soup with chicken. I always take that soup. It’s number 12: large bowl for me, thank you. It came out to be about 8 dollars. It is spicy and sour. That’s not the same as “sweet and sour” mind you: spicy and sour. It’s so good I wish the bowls, which are already meal size, were twice as large. This isn’t a restaurant review, but I strongly recommend if you have a little gnaw somewhere in your stomach, stop by “Noodles” and have Number 12.  Large pieces of luscious white chicken in a dark, spicy, sour sauce-soup. All sorts of mushrooms and other unknowns floating around in it. I think there are bean sprouts, some sort of dark fungus or mushroom, noodles of course – all piping hot, dark, and delicious. That’s Number 12.

So we relocated over there. Some of us had the soup. Some of us had chicken and noodles. We had a dinner party – not a KONO pizza cone in sight. And we chatted and laughed and tried very successfully to forget KONOs. I took some pictures in the restaurant, and here they are. No videos this time, I’m afraid.

Enjoy the photos and think very hard about January 28. That will be a Saturday. Remember we alternate from Friday (December 30) to Saturday. Think very hard about the next party. Where should it be and what should it be? It seems to me that it should have Chinese New Year as the theme, since the Chinese New Year comes at that time. Red Envelopes anyone?

Keep blogging!!

Cheers,

Alex

Home is Where the Art Is – give me a taste of my own neighborhood in your next film, please

December 24, 2011

Written by Alex

Comments Off on Home is Where the Art Is – give me a taste of my own neighborhood in your next film, please


People like their own backyards, as in “what movies were made on my street?” I’m no exception to that rule so I often look at the credits at the end to find out where the movie was made: Italy (nah, too far away); France (same); USA (same), so what’s wrong with Montreal as a location? One of my favorite made-in-Quebec movies is Beastly. The acting is good; the story is fun; the morality is right on.

This is the sum of the story - beautiful but dumb or ugly and smart - which would you prefer?

The acting seems very good, very professional. The lines as delivered are very natural. When X says Y, you can believe him. There are very few moments when you squirm and say to yourself, “S/He’s reading that.” So I give it a 9/10 for acting. It’s like pressing your ear up against a wall to hear your neighbor’s sighs and shouts.

Then there is the matter of the fun of the story. This good-looking guy is too good looking. His problem is that he knows it. He brags about knowing nothing but looking good. He runs for a political position in his high school (was the front of that school taken in Montreal? Probably.) He trashes the concept of expertise at anything. He brags, for instance, that he knows nothing about and cares not at all about environmental issues. He insults, following his father’s line, people who are fat or ugly. He tells them to their faces. Then he decides to take on a marginal girl who is “known” as a witch. He insults her looks at first. She takes it. Then he pretends to be interested in her at a moment when his girl friend has dropped him. When the girl friend comes back (being herself, like him, interested in the beautiful appearance of things), he drops the witch and tells her it was all a joke. At this point, she decides it’s pay-back time: she puts a spell on him. She turns him into a tattooed, scarred freak show. I applauded her revenge because the guy deserved it. Of course this Beauty and the Beast story has a catch: if someone can say “I love you” while he’s so ugly, then the curse will be lifted. So he sets about trying to get love while in this freak-show condition. The story is great. It moves along at a nice pace. It hangs together. You don’t find yourself asking questions like, “Who’s that guy? Or where are we now.”

So, finally, there is the matter of the morality in this film. You want this fool to suffer. He’s made others suffer for his ridiculous “Beauty-is-Everything” attitude. You want him to get a taste of life on the other side. When he remembers that he was attracted to a certain girl in the school, he first wants to contact her again so she will know what he’s going through and so he won’t be so alone in his new skin. He follows her around. He sees her every move. His feelings for her grow stronger and stronger. When her father makes a huge mistake by murdering a guy, the girl is threatened. The Beast decides she needs his help. He spirits her away to his sanctuary so she will be safe. He slowly learns how to make her happy. He stops giving her expensive gifts that she sniffs at. He learns from his own live-in servant to give the girl what she really wants: he builds her a green house; he writes her long romantic letters; he shares his secret places; he takes her to his father’s romantic country house. All of this is going in the right direction. We start to feel he’s getting the message. He even trashes his former opinions and philosophy. He’s learning that it’s what’s on the inside that counts. But there is the time element he’s racing against. He has to get the magic “I love you” words before a year is up. If he fails, the curse will never be undone. And the girl is going on a long trip at just the point where she’s about to say the magic words. Will she? Won’t she? Finally, she does. There is a match between the rhythm of the story and the morality that is really quite intense. In short, he has learned his lesson at last, the curse is lifted. He returns to his cute, curly haired self. We come full circle with a kiss and the words. The whole film is like a good dinner with a fine ice cream dessert at the end.

In sum, the acting in this movie is good; the story is interesting; the morality is sure and solid. That’s why it’s one of my favorite made-in-Montreal movies. If you’re like me and your backyard is best – then remember that home is where the art is, go rent or see this movie.

Tours of the Shaughnessy Village

December 22, 2011

Written by Alex

Comments Off on Tours of the Shaughnessy Village


Tour 1

The area around Concordia University is called The Shaughnessy Village. It is the area from Guy Street to Atwater Street and from Sherbrooke Street to René-Levesque. It is the most densely populated area of Quebec because of the large number of students at Concordia. It was named the Shaughnessy Village because the area used to belong to a very rich man named Thomas Shaughnessy. He was the President of Canadian Pacific Railroad. He lived from 1854 to 1923. His very large, beautiful house, built in 1874, still exists on René-Levesque. It is now a part of the Canadian Centre for Architecture.

(Beginning of Tour 1) 1 In 1992 Valerie Fabrikant, a professor at Concordia University, massacred 4 people on the 9th floor of the Hall Building. He was tried and put in prison.

2 MacKay Street is the street just to the west of the Hall Building. For many years Concordia has wanted to stop the traffic on MacKay Street between Demaisonneuve and Sherbrooke and to turn that part of MacKay into a no-car zone, that is, a green space with grass and trees. This project has not gone any further than the little plots of vegetables beside the Hall Building.

3 The Café-Dépôt on the corner of Demaisonneuve and MacKay Street is a café where there is a Meetup Group from 2:30 to 5:00 on Saturday. The people in the group want to practice French.  There are many other Meetup groups in Montreal. They meet to discuss all sorts of subjects: pets, movies, religions, babies, etc. Some of them meet to practice languages.

4 St. James Church is an Anglican church on St. Catherine Street. There are many activities in the church throughout the week:  free dinners for poor people, musical concerts, the Toastmasters club (learning to speak in public), Alcoholics Anonymous (for people who drink too much). On Wednesdays from 10:00 to 2:00, the church opens its second-hand shop (sometimes called a “bazaar” or a “nearly new shop”) in the basement. A Christmas in the Blazing Sun

Written by chocolatetransformer

Comments Off on A Christmas in the Blazing Sun


It was a very hot day. Mr. Turner’s little classroom was flooded with so much heat that droplets of sweat tickled uncontrollably down his back. A burning ray of sunshine the shape of a square wiggled across his desk and rippled over the fingers of a student at a nearby desk, making the student lean back and yawn as the clock showed three in the afternoon. However, the clock was almost ready to ring at the end of school and, like for all kids, the thought of soon being able to play outside pulled them with strong, warm arms and excited them with muscles, and warm, moist clasps.

Having noticed the rise in energy and participation, Mr. Turner, sitting at his desk, chewing on the end of his pencil,  and stroking and tapping the edge of the moving square of sunlight,  decided that, as final activity of the day, the children would read out loud the poems he had asked them to write the day before.

A girl in the back of the class pulling on her dress to straighten it, a boy on the right side, near where the ray of sunlight poured through the window, and a girl pulling a pencil out of her curly hair, raised their hands. As they rolled their rhyming and sentimental sonnets out of their mouths, Mr. Turner was fairly satisfied and delighted.

Soon, another student and then another raised a hand as the atmosphere turned more relaxing, partly because Mr. Turner praised their works and partly because heat naturally makes the human body and mind feel more peaceful.

As the activity reached an end, Mr. Turner asked the class if there was anyone else willing to share a poem.

Marie, who was sitting in the corner of the room, timidly raised her hand. In fact, she was one of those shy students who worked hard, but who barely ever spoke in front of the class. Everyone was considerably surprised that she would want to read her poem out loud. For that reason, all the children listened carefully.

Marie took the poem in her hands and began to speak out the verses. She read them fluidly, in a sincere and soft voice. The melody in her tone, mixed with the warmth of the air, filled the classroom with the flow of her melancholic poetry.

When she finished, all the students applauded.  Her performance was in fact remarkable, but it was hard to tell whether the children were applauding Marie or the end of the class.

As for Mr. Turner, he was truly impressed by her poem and, most importantly, by the heartfelt way with which she read it. After giving Marie some praise, he announced the end of class. All at once, the children started to hurry out of the small classroom.

As they went by, they accidentally pulled off the dirty straw curtain that served as a door, but none of them seemed to realize it as they jumped into the blazing sun.

Running into dilapidated streets of the African village, they raised a cloud of dirt behind them. Mr. Turner watched them run with a smile on his face. As a British teacher who had committed himself to education in such places, he was proud of his career. He felt pretty cheerful that day.

As a matter of fact, in just two days, it would be Christmas. Though there was no Christmas atmosphere at all in that village, surrounded by the desert, where people couldn’t care less about Christian holidays, he still wanted to celebrate Christmas somehow. Actually, he had thought about giving small gifts to every one of his students for Christmas, in two days, when he would also teach them about western festival traditions.  As he thought about his holiday plans, he noticed that all the students had gone outside, except for Marie.

She was still at her three-leggèd desk, taking her time to put the little bit of school materiel she had back into her bundle. Then, as she slowly walked to the door, she picked up the fallen curtain. So Mr. Turner walked over to her.

“Thank you, Marie,” he said gently as she handed him the curtain. “You know, I really loved your poem. It was wonderful!”

“Thank you, Sir,” Marie said timidly.

Then she watched silently as Mr. Turner managed to hang the curtain back onto the rusty nails above the entrance.

“Sir…,” she began with a tiny voice, “I want to say to you that I think you are a very good teacher.”

“Oh, well thank you very much!” Mr. Turner exclaimed, flattered, but mostly surprised by her sudden compliment. “I have tell you that you are a very good student and an extraordinary little poet. And I’m sure that your poems will be even better next time!”

“Well… I… I won’t come back to school anymore,” replied Marie, looking down. “Before I leave, I want to say goodbye to you, Sir…. Eh….I’m getting married in two days.”

She paused for a second. Then, she added,

“And…a married woman should… have the responsibility to stay at home. And I will be a good wife.”

She smiled at Mr. Turner.
But it was not joy that he saw on her face.
And he had one less Christmas present to prepare.

Introduction to Beijing

December 5, 2011

Written by akafell

Comments Off on Introduction to Beijing


Beijing,  also known as Peking,  is the capital of the People’s Republic of China and one of the most populous cities in the world. Can you imagine how many people live in Beijing? The population is over 19 million. It is more than half the population of Canada.

Beijing is the political, cultural, educational center of China, and home to the headquarters of most of China’s largest state-owned companies, such as China Petrol, China Oil, China Telecom, China Insurance, etc.

Beijing, located in northern China, borders Hebei Province to the north, west, south, east, and Tianjin to the southeast.
The area of Beijing is 16,801.25 sq km. Beijing is divided into 14 urban and suburban districts and two rural counties. It is a major transportation hub, with dozens of railways, roads and motorways passing through the city, and the destination of many international flights to China.”Beijing” means literally “northern capital”. Beijing is one of the Four Great Ancient Capitals of China. It has been the heart of China’s history for centuries. Beijing was the capital of the Yuan Ming and the Qing dynasties.

At the historical heart of Beijing lies the Forbidden City, the huge palace compound that was the home of the emperors of the Ming and Qing dynasty.

The Summer Palace lies in the western part of the city. It is a UNESCO（United Nations Educational, Scientific，and Cultural Organization) World Heritage Site, containing a comprehensive collection of imperial gardens and palaces that served as the summer retreat for the Qing emperors.

Among the best known religious sites in the city is the Temple of Heaven (Tiantan), located in southeastern Beijing, also a World Heritage Site, where the emperors of the Ming and Qing dynasties made visits for annual ceremonies of prayers to Heaven for good harvests. To the north of the city is the Temple of Earth (Ditan), while the Temple of the Sun (Ritan) and the Temple of the Moon (Yuetan) lie in the eastern and western urban areas respectively.

There are over one hundred museums in Beijing. In addition to the Palace Museum in the Forbidden City and the National Museum of China, other major museums include the National Art Museum of China, the Capital Museum, the Beijing Art Museum, the Military Museum of the Chinese People’s Revolution, the Geological Museum of China, the Beijing Museum of Natural History.

On the outskirts of urban Beijing, there are the Thirteen Tombs of the Ming Dynasty, which is the burial sites of thirteen Ming emperors. There are several sections of the UNESCO World Heritage Site Great Wall of China, the most famous being Badaling, Simatai and Mutianyu.

Beijing’s primary airport is the Beijing Capital International Airport, which is about 20 kilometres northeast of the city centre. It is currently the second busiest airport in the world (after Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport). After renovations for the 2008 Olympics, the airport now boasts three terminals, with Terminal 3 being one of the largest in the world. Most domestic and nearly all international flights arrive at and depart from Capital Airport.
The Airport Expressway links the airport to central Beijing; it is a roughly 40-minute drive from the city centre in good traffic conditions. Prior to the 2008 Olympics, the 2nd Airport Expressway was built to the airport, as well as a light rail system, which now connects to the Beijing Subway.

The Beijing Metro opened in 1971. Right now the subway has expanded to 14 lines, 198 stations, and is 336 kilometres in length. The fare is 2 yuan, with unlimited transfers except for the Airport Express line, which costs 25 yuan per trip. One Canadian dollar is equal to 6 yuan. In 2015, there will be at least 19 metro lines and the Metro will be 660 kilometers in length.
There are nearly 700 bus routes in Beijing .

How to Fail Your Driving Test

Written by robotchocolate

Comments Off on How to Fail Your Driving Test


How to fail your driving test

Failing a driving test is not a rare thing to happen in Quebec. I took my first driving test last month and I failed it.

Now, let’s analyses how to fail your driving test.

1. Experience

If you want to fail your driving test, you have to drive as little as possible. Do not drive to places where you have never been because your ability of noticing the stop signs would be improved. Furthermore, do not force yourself to drive in the rain and snow because experienced drivers are all able to drive in these situations. Most importantly, more you drive, more you will know how to react in an emergency situation. This fact would not help you to fail your driving test.

2. Overemotional

If you want to fail your driving test, I strongly suggest you to be extremely stressed or extremely self-confident during the test. No matter what your reaction is, if you are overemotional during your driving test, you will certainly fail it because you would make stupid mistakes, such as forgetting to look at the blink side. When you become overemotional, you are not able to control the driving speed: if you drive too fast, the inspector would say that you drive dangerously and you will fail the test; if you drive too slowly, the inspector would say that you need more practice so you cannot pass the test. As the result, being overemotional would certainly help you to fail the test.

3. Age

If you are under the age of 25, your dream of failing your driving test would probably be realized. In Quebec, most car accidents are caused by young people. Young people usually overestimate their driving abilities; in consequence, they pay less attention when they drive so they cause accidents. As the result, some inspectors prefer to fail young people’s first driving test even if they drive every well. Failing once the driving test may make young people to pay more attention when they drive.

4. Chance!

Of course, chance would destroy your dream of failing the driving test. First, there are different roads in driving test; some of them are extremely difficult and some of them are very easy. If you are lucky, your inspector would choose an easy road for your test. In this case, you may pass the test even if you do not drive very well. Secondly, some inspectors are stricter than others. For example, if you cannot park your car successfully, a “nice” inspector would give you a second or a third chance to park again. However, an “evil” inspector will tell you that you fail your test and “see you next time“. Being unlucky is one of the best methods to fail the test.

In conclusion, you may hear that many people pass their driving tests by chance and you are jealous of them. However, if you do not drive very well because of the lack of driving experience, I hope you will fail your driving test. Remember, one day you have to drive alone; at this moment, you are not allow to make any mistakes on the road because these mistakes may lead you to death. Sometimes, failing the driving test may be a good thing.

And the winner of the Wilson R. Thornley Blog award is…….

Written by Alex

Comments Off on And the winner of the Wilson R. Thornley Blog award is…….


X? Y? or Z?

No, no, no. Not so fast. Come to the party this Saturday and you’ll find out who wins – maybe it’s you.

Background to the Prize:

When I was in high school (Ogden High School) I had a Creative Writing teacher named Wilson R. Thornley. He was so good and his courses impacted the lives of so many of his students that I have decided to honor his memory by instituting a Wilson R. Thornley prize for Best Blog.

Wilson R. Thornley was an exceptional teacher. He marked his students for life, I would say. His classes were always a bit experimental. In the first place, you had to undergo an interview with him before he would accept you into his class. I point out that this was a public high school, but getting into his class was a special honor.

My recollection of that interview consists of just one question, which, unfortunately, I answered incorrectly:

“Why do you want to be in my class?” he asked over his tiny metal glasses, which often rode on the tip of his nose.

I hadn’t really prepared for this interview. I had just worried a lot about it.

“I like to write and I want to write,” I answered tentatively, maybe with the intonation of a question in my answer.

At any rate, he was ready with a red-pen comment:

“That’s really the wrong answer, isn’t it, Sheila?”

Sheila was not her name. I don’t remember her name. She was just working there in his classroom on Literary Harvest, the school literary magazine, for which Thornley was the advisor.

Bottom Photo: Thornley seated in front and Dean Hughes standing in the back row

“The correct answer is,” he quickly said,  “that you want to have an audience. After all, if you like to write, you can do that without a class.”

But I passed the interview and got into the class even though I failed the question. Huh? Well, that was what it was like to be around Thornley.

Being around Thornley was always a bit of a surprise, a pleasure, an entertainment.

I am endebted to him in a thousand ways, a thousand memories. He helped me win a scholarship to Occidental College. He proctored my writing sample that I submitted to Amherst College.

But more than that he (and the fantastic film The Miracle Worker about Helen Keller and her teacher Anne Sullivan ) inspired me to become a teacher.

“Thank you, Mr. Thornley. You were the best. We have not forgotten you.”

He so marked us as students that we are forever in his debt. We are his “grandchildren”.

National Recognition

He was a successful teacher. The National Scholastic Magazine had a yearly prize for the best writing to appear in its pages. As I recall, his students won the award 11 times! The editors of the magazine were so impressed that they sent experts or reporters to interview him to find out what his technique was.

And what was that technique?

Well, it consisted of a lot of preparation before writing poetry or stories. He had a well-organized, long list of exercises. I remember several of them to this day and have used them myself as an English teacher. Here are some of the assignments.

“Go sit under a pine tree and describe what it is that you see, smell, hear, taste, feel – and don’t forget the light! “

With Thornley, it was never “think”. He admitted, rather regularly and freely, what I myself also believe, that stories and poems do not consist of “thoughts”. They consist of sensations, I believe he would say.

Another assignment was, “Describe a pair of hands involved in some manual activity.”

For the first of these assignments I convinced my brother to drive me up into one of the canyons close to Ogden, Utah, Weber Canyon, I believe. He parked the car on the dusty canyon road while I scrambled up over rocks and sagebrush to a grove of pine trees.

I sat there for about twenty minutes trying to “sense” what was going on up in, around, and under the pine tree. My brother, who was not one of Thornley’s students, must have been a bit puzzled by the whole exercise.

For the second assignment, the script went something like this:

“Mom, make some bread.”

She understood it was an assignment, a “Thornley” assignment. So she agreed and I sat there at the kitchen table with my notebook and pencil while she kneaded the dough.

The Argo Bookstore

November 22, 2011

Written by chocolatetransformer

Comments Off on The Argo Bookstore


Every bookstore has its very own atmosphere. In my opinion, out of all the bookstores in Montreal, the Argo bookstore is the most charming one. I like it because of its personal service, its history and its ambience. Going into the Argo is just like escaping onto an old sailboat gently rocked by the caress of the waves.

First of all, it provides a very personalized service. Though it stores no more than six thousand books, every single one of them has been chosen with care. When you go into the shop, you can be sure that you will be able to retrieve a fine book. Moreover, if you are searching for a specific book that is hard to find, Argo can directly order it for you. From its careful and personal services, one can understand that the purpose of this bookstore is not to make profits, but to commit itself to literature by providing for those who appreciate culture and poetry. It serves this purpose like the devoted crew of a creaking longstanding ship that travels to the rhythm of the winds.

Second of all, the Argo bookstore has an interesting history. It was started by John George in 1966, which makes it one of the oldest bookstores in Montreal. On its first day of business, it only made three sales, but George was not discouraged since he had little interest in making money. Even after a fire a year later, he continued running the store on the other side of St. Catherine Street. Thus, he devoted himself to his bookstore for forty years. In 2006, he fell ill and had no choice but to be hospitalized. In such a critical condition, he kept working until the very day he was admitted into the hospital. In fact, his touching devotion and contribution definitely add more charm to the Argo because George’s commitment itself is fascinating. After his departure, the store has been run by Chris Lake. He will soon hand it over to three recent university graduates, J.P. Karwacki, Jesse Eckerlin, and Meaghan Acosta, who will take charge of the bookstore together. Despite many difficult moments, the Argo has never deviated from its original dedication, as said on its website, to “quality over quantity”.

Last of all, the Argo bookstore offers a unique ambience. As a matter of fact, the whole store is nothing but a narrow alley, enclosed by walls covered with wooden shelves carrying as many books as possible. Additionally, the advertising sign on the entrance of the Argo is not very large. This makes it appear insignificant in the colourful chaos of St. Catherine Street. However, all these unusual characteristics give to it an intriguing side. It is because the Argo bookstore is so fundamentally different from all the other stores that it generates a unique ambience of its own. Its narrow space filled with the warm and sweet smells of wood and books makes the atmosphere feel comfortable, safe and mystic. Then, its old style adds even more to that feeling. Its mysterious character creates the impression that every book on the wooden shelves holds an enthralling story and that every story hides an enchanting secret. Entering into the bookstore gives the feeling of stepping into an enclosed little world full of mysteries waiting for you to discover. Without doubt, the ambience of the Argo is mesmerizing. Actually, it smells just like the miscellaneous, mystifying, and warm odour of an old wooden ship that has sailed the seven seas.

Over all, the Argo bookstore has a personalized service, an intriguing history, and a unique ambience. Hence, I find it to be the most charming bookstore in the whole of Montreal. Anyone in search of a pleasant spot full of lovely books should go to the Argo to be surrounded by the subtle poetry that floats is the air like a quiet sailboat drifting swiftly in the cool wind.

Curling as an Expression of Canadian Culture
		

									November 19, 2011
						

	

			

			
Written by dodolo1001

			
Comments Off on Curling as an Expression of Canadian Culture

		

		

		

Curling and Canadian Culture


One of the biggest inventions in human history is sports.


Curling is a typical Canadian sport that is rather different from all the other team sports.


The reason why curling is considered as a typical Canadian sport is because it’s relaxing, because its playing environment is suitable for the winter, and because it can be played by everyone.


Relaxing


First of all, curling is a quite relaxing sport compared to all the other team sports. It has its reasons for being called a typical Canadian sport. When we explain how to play the game, it is like describing the style of a Canadian life: take your time, do not rush. Great strategies play a big part in the game that lead the team to the victory. That is why the sport is also called “chess on ice”. On the team, there is always a curler to slide the rock across the ice, while two other people, in order to help the rock to reach the goal, have to sweep the ice with brooms as the rock slides. Just like Canadians, they like to think slowly and carefully before they take every single small step.


Ice is Nice


Second of all, the fact that it has to be played on ice makes it a typical Canadian sport. When I picture Canada, all I have in mind is a completely white background with everything covered in snow. In Canada, winter lasts for almost half of the year while summer comes and goes after only two months. During winter time, Canadians get bored easily because of the snow and ice outside that stop them from doing most outdoor activities. Despite the weather, Canadians never fail to entertain themselves: they invent games to play on the ice. Curling is one of the examples of how Canadians enjoy their life on the ice.


Democracy on Ice


Lastly, curling can be played by every person. It doesn’t matter what the physical condition of the person is. Children of any age can play it. Old people can play it. People in excellent physical condition can play it. People who are sick and fat and handicapped and out of shape in any way can play it. It is by far the most democratic sport in existence.


In conclusion, curling represents the life style of a Canadian and it is a perfect entertainment for someone who has nothing to do on a tedious winter day. That is why we call it a Canadian sport. Curling might be relaxing physically. Curling is one of the greatest sports in the world.

How do we purchase books at Concordia University?
		

									November 16, 2011
						

	

			

			
Written by City Rose

			
Comments Off on How do we purchase books at Concordia University?

		

		

		



How do we purchase CHEAP textbooks at Concordia University?


            Purchasing textbooks was no big deal back in high school and college. However, at university, purchasing books gives students a headache because of the price and the inconvenience. Students have to find their own ways to purchase cheap textbooks. Personally, I save money by renting books in the Concordia Bookstore, buying second hand books at a student-owned bookstore, and purchasing second hand books from online classifieds.


First, renting books save time and money. Concordia bookstore offers textbook rental for 30% of the original price. Students can easily rent the books by going to the bookstore without the effort of searching and reselling afterwards. However, the textbooks run out fast. According to the clerk, the textbook rental ran out right after the clerks put them on stock. So if one really wants to rent a textbook, one should pay a visit to the bookstore right away. Textbook rental is cheap and convenient.


Secondly, getting textbooks from a student-owned second hand bookstore is also a great option. I cannot recall the name but it is located on Bishop Street right beside Concordia University. It is a self-serve bookstore. This means that buyers have to search by themselves. Of course, one can still ask the cashier to look up the availability of a textbook. The textbooks in this bookstore cost between 50%-70% of their original price. It is possible to find a newer book with a cheaper price compared to others. It happens because it is the seller who set the price and the bookstore merely provides a place to sell the book. After the book is sold, the bookstore will add a certain percentage to the price as commission. So, after finishing the textbooks, a student can sell them back. Getting textbooks at the co-operative bookstore is also really easy.


Finally, the cheapest method of getting a textbook is through online classifieds. Concordia University does offer a site for selling and buying second hand books. However, it seems to me that it is not very popular because the link is not obvious. There are also several websites that offer the opportunity to buy second hand books. Kijiji and Con-Ads are my two favorite websites in terms of buying second hand textbooks. You can also find other second hand things on the website, too. Usually, you can get the textbook for less than 60% of the original price. If you are a bargainer, it is not a problem to get a book for less than 40% or even 30 % of the original price. However, the buyer and seller have to arrange a date in order to meet. I really do not like to arrange a date because it is not easy to find the right time to meet. Nevertheless, getting textbooks through online classifieds is the cheapest way.


In order to save money on textbooks, students can rent the books from the bookstore, purchase them in the second hand bookstore, or buy them through online classifieds. Each of these methods has its own advantages and disadvantages. You can try them all and then see what your favorite is.

Living Downtown – Nightmare or Heaven?
		

									
						

	

			

			
Written by luckylidia

			
Comments Off on Living Downtown – Nightmare or Heaven?

		

		

		

I have lived in the downtown area for more than three years. My friends always remind me that it is so noisy downtown and ask me why I don’t want to move to another quiet place. I do not deny that it is crowded, there is a lack of a greenbelt and park ground downtown. Though there are many disadvantages, I still think that it is the best choice to live in center city. The streets are clean, life is convenient, and the people are closer to each other.


The streets downtown are clean and neat. People always think that center city may be dirtier than other areas. It is not right. Before I moved to center city, I lived in other areas, in which the streets are clean when the winter is gone because garbage men clean all the streets after the snow is melted. After that, the garbage men seem to disappear and the streets become dirtier and dirtier till the winter snow. In the later autumn, there is garbage everywhere, such as paper, empty containers and food thrown away. In contrast, the streets downtown are very clean and neat.  Garbage cans are set at every corner and people can easily find garbage cans every ten feet. In the morning, I always see many garbage men who are working in the street. Some of them push garbage cans and pick up every small piece of garbage. Others pick up the garbage cans. Sometimes I see garbage trucks picking up the garbage automatically.


Living downtown is convenient. On the one hand, shopping becomes easier. We can go shopping by only going downstairs or by taking a 3 minute walk. All kinds of shops are around us, such as Provigo, Jean Coutu, and PA. The famous Atwater Mall is in our area. We can buy whenever we want even though it is midnight, without driving far away on the weekend and without filling our cars with food and vegetables for the whole week. Also, going and coming are convenient. The Metro is only a 3 minute walk away. And many bus lines go along the downtown streets. We can easily go all over Montreal by Metro and bus.  When people complain that they have to spend more than 1 hour on the way to their work, we are happy that we don’t have to waste too much time.


People are closer to each other when living downtown. It is not like living in a suburb, where the street is so quiet and sometimes you can not see one person in the street. However, we may easily meet a friend along the way if living downtown. We live so close that we can visit each other frequently or we can meet in a coffee shop. Also, Chinese restaurants are good places to meet with friends. On the other hand, we have more opportunities to communicate with people who come from other countries and to know more about other communities.


These are the reasons why I like the center city. However, there are some shortcomings, such as there are no parks for children, and we spend too much money because we live near the shops. But it doesn’t change the fact that I like this neighborhood.

Rebecca, a Fascinating Movie
		

									November 8, 2011
						

	

			

			
Written by chocolatetransformer

			
Comments Off on Rebecca, a Fascinating Movie

		

		

		

Every movie has its own characteristics and qualities. I find the famous director Alfred Hitchcock’s Rebecca to be an exquisite great movie. I like it because of its outstanding actors, its fascinating characters and its intriguing g psychological components. It is a movie that can fill one’s wind like a powerful flood.




First of all, the actors in Rebecca are absolutely outstanding. All of them perform their parts very naturally, but with accuracy and precision. For example, Joan Fontaine, who plays the female protagonist, delivers her part with openness and clear emotions to illustrate the character’s innocence and youthfulness. Laurence Olivier, who acts as the male protagonist, ingeniously expresses with subtle facial expressions and well carried out temper jumps the complicated past of his character Mr. de Winter. Furthermore, the actress of Ms. Danvers skillfully demonstrates a twisted personality of a woman who is both decent and wicked. The actors’ performances are all very vivid, convincing, and strong. Hence, their acting skills have as much as impact as raging waves hitting on a rocky cliff.


Second of all, the characters in the movie are fascinating. Each of them has a very intriguing personality and point of view. For instance, Joan Fontaine’s character, the new Mrs. de Winter, shows an undying believe in love by deciding to stay at her husband’s side after learning that he has accidentally killed his previous wife, Rebecca. As for Mr. de Winter, a proud man tormented by the sins of his past, his struggle with moral ambivalences is enigmatic and deep. Moreover, Ms. Danvers, Rebecca’s personal maid, displays a wicked, abnormal, obsession and loyalty towards Rebecca. Of course, Rebecca herself, who is a magnificent and powerful creature, is extremely interesting. Even dead, she greatly influences the lives of the people who have surrounded her. The memory of her lingers everywhere is Mandalay. She was a person who wanted absolute perfection to the point of preferring to die immediately than to live with sickness. Thus, she has plotted her own accidental murder so that, even in death, she would be in control of everything. The mysteries of her personality are truly enthralling.


Third of all, Rebecca has some very intriguing psychological components. In the case if the new Mrs. de Winter, she is tortured by the comparison between her and Rebecca, who seem to be a divinely impeccable being. She is so convinced that she is incomparable with that perfect creature that she begins to believe that her husband still loves Rebecca. Meanwhile, Mr. de Winter, tormented by the memory and secrets of his dark past with Rebecca, hates and fears her so much that he failed to go one with his life. Furthermore, there is Ms. Danvers, who is absolutely devoted to Rebecca and who worships everything that has formerly belonged to her. By constantly thinking about her, Ms. Danvers has gone from obsession to madness. As a matter of fact, all those people are hunted by the memory of Rebecca. Indeed, she was a powerful and fabulous. However, she has been dead for long. Therefore, it is not her who is controlling those people, but their own consciousness that keeps being fanatical by the memory of her. Their own memories are leading them into a strange situation where everyone is steered by the past. Though there is no need to be concerned about a long dead person, Mr. de Winter and his new wife still foolishly fear Rebecca while Ms. Danvers still serves Rebecca’s purpose with obsession. In fact, it is because their minds are hunted by her image that her presence persists in Mandalay. Therefore, it is their paranoia that has given power to Rebecca since they let their memory create illusions that torments them. These psychological issues can devastate one’s life with as much power a ferocious tornado.


In short, this movie has outstanding actors, fascinating characters and intriguing psychological components. These are the reasons why I consider it to be a great movie. I recommend Rebecca to everyone who wants to watch a movie that can blow their minds like a wild hurricane.
