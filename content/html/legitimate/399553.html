Board/administration relations at colleges and universities

College administrators and board members provide different skill sets and perspectives, oftentimes having divergent and competing responsibilities. But its critical for the success of the institution that they can work together smoothly. Knowing how to make this relationship function harmoniously and knowing what attorneys can do to help facilitate this can ultimately help all parties involved. Communication The administration must fully communicate all significant information about any topic on which the board votes. This communication should include information such as why the issue in question is important to the institution and how the proposal supports the institutions goals. The administration should also provide data as to how other institutions manage the same issue. This information should be communicated in the form of a brief written report, as appropriately prepared with the universitys legal counsel, and then discussed in person during the board meeting. If the item involves legal risk or advice, legal counsel should participate in any advice portion of the board discussion. Its important that this information is given to the board members with sufficient time for them to understand the matter. Relationship building and orientation For the administration and the board to work together, its important to build relationships. This can start with the onboarding of new members to the board. Depending on the available time of the board members, an orientation that takes them to the academic, financial, research, security, athletic, legal, and operational segments of the institution is often enjoyable, beneficial and leads to great relationship building. Many institutions that have conducted these types of orientations have reported success. These institutions have found that experienced board members enjoyed them so much that they desire to keep taking part in yearly orientations. It is appropriate to invite them each year. This creates great team building between the board members (both seasoned and new) and the administration. It also helps the board have a deeper understanding of the day-to-day operations and facilities of the institution. Administrators should also take the time to talk with board members one on one. This allows board members to learn the job responsibilities and different priorities of each of the colleges major administrators. It also allows administrators to learn the backgrounds of the board members and what matters are important to them. It would be prudent to also have the board chair and the institutions counsel sit down with board members to explain the duties and responsibilities of their board position. Legal counsel can help draft and present a briefing that will explain how a board works, why and how board discussions and decisions are important, and what responsibilities each board member has. This discussion should address confidentiality, privilege, FOIA and the Open Meetings Act (as applicable), press relations/avoiding leaks, proper privileged discussion of pending litigation and the like. It is especially critical that attorneys for the institution ensure that board members understand their legal obligations relating to sharing information through numerous methods such as text messages, e-mails, and discussions about the institution with outside parties. Counsel should also explain how the client is the institution itself and not the president or a particular board member (unless the board retains its own counsel). Also (by statute in some cases), board members must always remember that the chair leads the board and the president leads the institution. Each board member (as well as the officers of the college) has a fiduciary duty. Training as to such duty is important, as well as on ethics and conflicts of interest. Attorneys for the institution should also use these meetings to review important themes of education law that the board will see in their time in service. Recommended topics to review include tenure; collective bargaining and labor relations; the student discipline process; FERPA (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act); Title IX; Americans with Disabilities Act; EEO obligations; and research obligations such as ITAR. It is prudent also to include a finance orientation that explains the budgeting process, cost centers, sources of revenue (including tuition, grants, and donations/endowment), the purpose and significance of any loss leaders, bank relationships, and the flow of annual receipts and expenditures. Best practices for board meetings Administrators should provide brief summaries with the meeting agenda that highlight the most essential information and why it is important to the institution. These summaries should include enough information so that a busy board member who may not have read all the documentation before the meeting can make an informed decision. Administrators should engage their legal counsel in helping to write these summaries when the topics are law or risk related. When orally briefing an issue to the board that will be the subject of a vote, the administrator involved should recap what the issue is and why voting for or against the proposal is important to the institution. Board members should understand clearly what the consequences of a vote for or against the proposal may or will have. Board members, as indicated above, will often benefit from learning what analogous institutions have done with the same issue. Counsels role at the board meeting: Legal counsel should be certain that the record is properly demarcated so that privilege is maintained. One way to approach this is to adjourn the board meeting while counsel provides privileged advice and takes questions. Some colleges are subject to statutes or rules that require a super majority vote in order to adjourn for a privileged discussion. The role of the attorney It is important for the operation of the university or college for there to be a good relationship between the administration and board members. To help in creating a productive and efficient board, attorneys play a critical role. One task that attorneys provide is helping to ensure that board members understand their responsibilities. Attorneys can also be helpful in providing information and conducting briefings on both recurring legal topics as well as current topics on which the board will vote. Attorneys who are general counsel for the institution need to provide in-depth briefings, also explaining that their responsibility (i.e., their client) is the university and not any member of the institution or the board members. When it comes to running a university or college, board relations are critical, and attorneys play an important part in developing and maintaining that relationship.
