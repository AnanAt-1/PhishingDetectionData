I was talking with a fiendabout Chris Nilan, and I adjusted Nilan's PIM by historical averages. There's really no other way to do it. Like goal scoring, penalties aren't always worth the same number of minutes depending on the year. In one season 300 PIM may not be very much, while in other seasons it can be a tremendoud amount. Further showing that some work is needed to view PIM numbers properly, of the top 234 career PIM leaders, 7 did not play the majority of their careers in the 80s or 90s. That's only 3%...

So for fun, I adjusted the penalty minutes for the top 5 career leaders, and then added a couple of historically infamous penalty machines.

Top 5 Adjusted Penalty Leaders
1. Red Horner: 3385
2. Tiger Williams: 3030
3. Eddie Shore: 2465
4. Sprague Cleghorn: 2338
5. Tie Domi: 2195

I could well be missing some players, and I may take the time to fill more of this out over time, but I mostly interested in the fun value of it for tonight.

For the uninitiated, Horner was the filthiest player in the 1930s, leading the league in penalties *** 9 *** seasons in a row.

Shore is, of course, easily the best player on this list, being a 4-time Hart Trophy winner. If there was such a thing as a Norris Trophy at the time, he'd have snapped a bunch of them up too.

Cleghorn is the other defenseman on the list, and like Shore, was a great and brutal player. His violent and psychopathic on-ice tendencies are legendary, especially the battles against his ex-Ottawa teammates. He was the great puck-rushing defenseman of his generation, but will forever be remembered for his antics: he assaulted his own wife with his crutches, while nursing a broken leg. In 1923, he went after the Sens' Lionel Hitchman and bashed his face so badly that Cleghorn was fined $200 by his own club, as well as nearly inciting a riot. The next year he followed that up by going after Hitchman again, butt-ending him in the eye, and later took out Buck Boucher's knees. His career was dotted by cross-checks to the head and face, spearings, kneeings, butt-ends, running opponent's faces into the boards, as well as administering some of the worst beatings in the league, which is saying something for hockey in the 1920s. Along the way, Cleghorn may have even purposely cut Boucher's face with his skates. A great guy.

Tiger Williams... What can we say about this guy? A goon through and through, and he rode his stick after scoring a goal... In the meantime, his career was filled with incidents of ramming teammate's faces into the glass, he bit Dave Schultz during a fight, stick swingings, etc, etc, etc...

Tie Domi - see Tiger Williams, but better behaved. There's been better fighters, but has a habit of "going off" on guys on the ice, which HAS to be intimidating.

Ah, the fun of looking at the careers of such miscreants.

Daryl

This post is a little over two years old, and is now out of date, considering that I have since completed a much more thorough study of the subject. From my site:
-------------------------------------------------------------
If you were to look at a list of the top 100 career penalty minute leader, you would see that 97 of those players all began their careers in the last thirty years. Only Tim Horton, Gordie Howe and Ted Lindsay have careers that began before the period of the 1970s, 80s and 90s. Is it possible that the 97% of the game’s roughest and most violent players all played just within this small period of time that is only 1/3 of the history of major league hockey? Of course not, that would completely unreasonable to suggest.

Other eras have featured incredible amounts of violence, with games featuring stick swinging incidents, massive bench clearing brawls, and huge amounts of blood-letting in general. In my essay, Normalized Production, I showed an alternative method for viewing player statistics that allows for cross-era comparison. Using the same principles as Normalized Production, I now present Normalized Penalty Minutes (NPIM).

The Top 25 Career Normalized Penalty Minute Leaders:
T25. Hooley Smith, 2220 NPIM- Beat the living crap out of the Bruins Harry Oliver during ’27 Cup Finals. The drubbing was so savage that Smith was suspended for the first month of the next season.

T25. Chris Nilan, 2220 NPIM- Here’s one that most fans don’t know: Nilan was longtime friends with Monsignor Frederick J. Ryan; close enough to perform Nilan’s wedding ceremony as well. In 2002, Ryan told him that he molested three boys during the 1970s and 80s. Nilan did the right thing and testified in legal proceedings against his old friend.

24. Buck Boucher, 2225 NPIM- Played football for the Ottawa Roughriders before turning to hockey, never had a huge number of NPIM in any season, but built them up steadily.

23. Bill Gadsby, 2238 NPIM- True story: he took out insurance against stitches to his face, as he had over 600 of them on his mug.

22. Tim Hunter, 2250 NPIM- Born with a nose just dying to be broken.

21. Bert Corbeau, 2304 NPIM- Rather unhinged as an on-ice personality, but more of a foot-note player than anything. In 1923 the Leafs traded him to Hamilton for Ken Randle and Corb Denneny. Nice trade for Toronto.

20. Craig Berube, 2380 NPIM- 71 fighting majors since 1998…

19. Art Ross, 2403 NPIM- Influential more as a "builder” than as a player, and that's saying something. His HPR score of 509 puts him right between Flash Hollett and Lionel Conacher.

18. Duke Keats, 2420 NPIM- Skill and toughness rolled into one. Keats was especially grumpy in 1926, the final year of the WCHL, when he collected 401 NPIM.

17. Bob Probert, 2437 NPIM- Legendary fighter. Will Probert’s battles with Tie Domi and Mart McSorley be remembered, or his drub problems?

16. Marty McSorley, 2484 NPIM- For some reason, he never had a bad reputation as an extremely dirty player, yet when you look at his record, you see repeated suspensions for flagrant stick violations.

15. Dave Schultz, 2527 NPIM- The most memorably violent player from the most recent era of hockey violence. It was his job to end the monkey business that Bobby Clarke started. “The Hammer” holds the record for the most NPIM in a single season, with 492.

14. Bryan Watson, 2617 NPIM- A fine pest in his day, a decent defensive player, but played for 15 teams in 16 seasons. Watson retired as the all-time penalty minute leader, the record broken first by Dave Schultz.

13. Sprague Cleghorn, 2622 NPIM- Where to begin? Cleghorn was both one of the dirtiest and most skilled players of the 1910s and 20s. One of the 20 best offensive defensemen in history, he was one of most violent men ever to set foot on ice. From the time his career began to when it ended, his life (on and off-ice) was filled with fights, kneeings, butt-ends, attacks from behind, and wife beatings.

12. Rob Ray, 2632 NPIM- A fighter with a 10 year career, whose offensive value is the second lowest among players with that tenure.

11. Dale Hunter, 2652 NPIM- Obsessively dirty player, probably most famous for nailing Pierre Turgeon from behind during the 1993 playoffs, about 5 seconds after Turgeon had scored. A product of 80s hockey, his normalized totals aren’t nearly as good as they are unadjusted. The most similar player to Hunter: Reg Noble.

10. Eddie Shore, 2658 NPIM- The great and brutal defenseman of the 1930s. Shore nearly killed Ace Bailey when he hit him over the head with his stick. Shore was both a son of a bitch, and a truly great player.

9. Tie Domi, 2666 NPIM- We all know this guy...

8. Cully Wilson, 2707 NPIM- Wilson was considered to be a violent player during a time (1910s and 20s) when clubbing players over the head with sticks was a relatively common occurrence.

7. Ted Lindsay, 2747 NPIM- Perhaps the scrappiest player ever. Lindsay, clawed and fought his way for every single goal he scored, paying a tremendous price physically. Later in his career he would be so sore when he woke up, he would literally have to roll out of bed to get dressed. A true “gamer” if there ever was one.

6. Gordie Howe, 2773 NPIM- Never took huge numbers of penalties in a season, he played for 30 years. He beat the living tar out of reigning heavyweight king Lou Fontinato so badly the Rangers’ tough guy needed to have his face surgically repaired.

5. Tom Dunderdale, 2844 NPIM- Close your eyes and picture Pat Verbeek…

4. Red Dutton, 2967 NPIM- Begged WWI surgeons not to amputate his leg, and spent the next 18 months rehabbing. He was a tough customer that didn’t have his job because of any high amount of talent, so he hit and used his fists.

3. Red Horner, 3160 NPIM- Horner was a fantastically hard hitter and an even better fighter, that led the NHL in penalty minutes eight consecutive years, from 1933 to 1940. In 1933, Horner whooped Eddie Shore, after Shore flipped Ace Bailey over, landing him on his head, and injuring him almost to the point of death.

2. Tiger Williams, 3521 NPIM- Williams once beat up teammate Pat Bouttete, smashing his face against the glass, and then attacked Lanny McDonald when he attempted to break up the “fight”. In 1976, Williams bit Dave Schultz during a fight, hit Dennis Owchar over the head with his stick, causing a gash that required almost 50 stitches to close. Williams attempted to strangle Billy Smith after hitting the goaltender in the throat with a shot, and one time physically threw a reporter out of the Canucks dressing room after the writer had less than grand things to say about him in an article. Williams is a regular guest on Off the Record, a sports-talk show, and even continues his old ways by intimidating other guests into sharing his opinion.

1.Joe Hall, 3597 NPIM- Also a pretty good offensive defenseman (HPR of 439, right in the Tim Horton range) but will be forever remembered for his on-ice antics. In one game, after getting his face rearranged by Frank Patrick, Hall promptly beat the crap out of referee Rod Kennedy. Hall didn’t pick his spots, constantly feuding with the tough players like Newsy Lalonde and Sprague Cleghorn. Hall was undoubtedly helped by the fact that he almost constantly was paired with equally (if not more) terrifying players. Over the years Hall’s teammates included guys like Bert Corbeau, and the incomparably scary Harry Mummery (picture Georges Laraque with Tiger Williams’ temperament in a league where the average player was 160 pounds.)

Not all of those players were goons, per-se. Some, as in the cases of Shore and Cleghorn, were brilliant offensive player that also enjoyed partaking in acts of violence that would make a gladiator blush. A number of players in this top 25 list certainly were not good (or even decent) offensive talents, were men that required violence to keep their jobs, and really were goons more than anything.

Before I go too much further, I may as well define what “goon” means, for the purposes of this study. According to dictionary.com, a goon is “a thug hired to intimidate or harm opponents”. That actually goes along completely with my long-held opinion of what a hockey goon is, but doesn’t say it completely. So, I shall define a hockey goon as:

"A player hired to intimidate or harm opponents, whose penalty totals far exceed his offensive value."

I believe that’s a fair description of the hockey goon. I’m not necessarily referring to guys that are tough as well as talented. It would be unfair to call Ted Lindsay a goon, as he was a good player beside that. Dave Brown, however, was a perfect example of a player whose skills at acquiring penalty minutes far outstripped his offensive ability.

Can a goon be statistically identified? It can be done quite easily, actually. The formula:

GOON = (1- (COPS / CarNPIM) * 100)

Where COPS is Career Offensive Player System, and CarNPIM is the player’s career Normalized Penalty Minutes.

This produces a number, so that a player with a GOON of 100 would be a perfectly defined goon. To have a GOON rating of 100 would require a player to have almost literally zero offensive value, which is nearly impossible, since even the worst hockey players have been able to produce even a small amount of goals if given enough time.

For sure, it was the first thing I noticed as well. I think it's the product of a few things:

-Previous eras had smaller rosters. Up until 1926, teams couldn't even dress 10 players. This number slowly grew over the year, but this would undoubtedly squeeze a supremely tough, but non-NHL qualified player from the roster most of the time, especially considering that nobody ever rolled out four lines in those years. Clubs, instead, preferred players that were tough and hard-nosed, but otherwise able players. Sure, there were always guys like Johnny Mariucci around, but it was much more of a rarity than now. Today, with 18 players to dress, and 4 lines being used, there is more space for a goon.

-Starting in the 1980s I'd say that fighters were being used differently than before, and were being used as constant protection for less physical superstars (the Gretzky/Semenko example). This wasn't exactly a brand new tactic, but it certainly became popular, and, I think, is another factor in 80s and 90s fighters comprising a big portion of the list.

-Repeated expansions helped some of the big name fighers hold on to their jobs longer. What's the one thing that happens when leagues expand? The average increases, as opposed to descreasing, as many feel happens. Veterans stick around longer because clubs have more confidence in them. For guys like Stu Grimson and Rob Ray, this had the effect of allowing them to sitck around and accumulate larger penalty totals.

-Expansion also factors in another way: there are more teams and players now. If each team had one goon in 1945, that's 6 goons. If each team has one today, that's 30. The league increased in size by 30% during the 90s, and also by large amount over older decades. Combined with the above factors, we'll just see more modern players in a list like this.

How I see it, anyway.
