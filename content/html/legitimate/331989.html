    Old Page Turners for a New Presidential Campaign

    By John Williams
    Allison Joyce/Getty ImagesAt New Gingrich’s primary-night party in Manchester, N.H., on Tuesday before Mr. Gingrich, who finished fourth, took the stage.

    Michael Lewis’s book about the 1996 presidential election, “Trail Fever” (since republished as “Losers”), includes this description of the attendees at a banquet for Republicans in New Hampshire: “Every ounce of being they have left over after pledging allegiance to the United States and singing the national anthem goes into despising the United States government.”

    As the Republicans leave New Hampshire and ready for the South Carolina primary, and as President Obama readies for his eventual opponent, readers might consider selecting from several classic accounts of previous presidential campaign seasons to see them through this one. The 1960s, with their social and political tumult, were a serendipitous time for the genre to flower. Theodore White’s “The Making of the President 1960” — he published similar treatments of the 1964, 1968 and 1972 elections — won the Pulitzer Prize, and is widely credited with creating the modern blow-by-blow form of election books, but practitioners haven’t precisely followed his lead. Mr. White, a former China correspondent for Time, approached the story from a traditional reportorial perspective, and wrote of the office of the presidency with something approaching reverence. As the decade progressed, accounts by other writers became, like the populace, louder and more cynical. Read more…

    A Passion for the Cinematic Avant-Garde

    By Dave Itzkoff
    Matthew Septimus A montage of Jonas Mekas images, from 1960 to the present, for a P.S. 1 gallery exhibition.
    Keith Sikora Chuck Workman

    Even if you don’t immediately recognize Chuck Workman’s name, you almost surely know his cinematic output: for two decades he has made film montages for the Academy Awards ceremony, including its annual “In Memoriam” tribute sequence. He is also an Oscar winner for best short film and the director of features like “Superstar,” a documentary about Andy Warhol, and “The Source,” a film about the Beat poets.

    When he is not working on movies that are seen by more than 40 million people in a night, Mr. Workman has another passion: the avant-garde cinema, and the work for experimental filmmakers like Stan Brakhage, Kenneth Anger and Jonas Mekas, the artist and critic who helped make Anthology Film Archives an epicenter of the avant-garde movement.

    In Mr. Workman’s film “Visionaries,” which has its premiere on Thursday at the Tribeca Film Festival, he tells the story — or, at least, a story — about Mr. Mekas and the development of this movement. Mr. Workman spoke recently to ArtsBeat about the experimental films of Warhol and Mel Brooks, a famous fight between Norman Mailer and Rip Torn, and how he can make documentaries about avant-garde cinema and Michael Jackson alike. These are excerpts from that conversation.

    Q.

    What were your earliest introductions to avant-garde film?

    A.

    When I was a kid, I went to the New Yorker and I sat through “Empire,” or I sat through two hours of it. That’s Andy Warhol’s six-hour film of the Empire State Building. There was absolutely nothing to do. You smelled the popcorn and just sat there. Those little changeover marks that come every 20 minutes on a reel, in the upper right-hand corner, you were waiting for those to come.     Blurbing ‘Catch-22’

    By Jennifer Schuessler

    Nina Bourne, the veteran publishing executive who died last Friday at age 93, was known for the crisply stylish ad campaigns she created in her four decades at Alfred A. Knopf.

    Bourne’s biggest triumph was the campaign for Joseph Heller’s “Catch-22,” which helped lift the book from the ranks of the medium-sellers to a full-fledged sensation. In a move new at the time, Bourne ran advertisements with quotes from ecstatic British reviews after the book topped best-seller lists over there. “Come on! Don’t let the English beat us!” one of the ads declared.

    But as William Grimes writes in his obituary, Bourne failed to get a blurb from one Englishman, Evelyn Waugh, who wrote to her: “I am sorry that the book fascinates you so much. It has many passages quite unsuitable to a lady’s reading.”

    That isn’t quite the whole of the story, however. In a letter written in Sept. 1961, Waugh anatomized the novel’s faults, writing: “It suffers not only from indelicacy but from prolixity. It should be cut by about a half. In particular the activities of ‘Milo’ should be eliminated or greatly reduced.” Read more…

    Win a Stay at Norman Mailer’s House! (If You’re British.)

    By Dave Itzkoff
    Associated Press Norman Mailer

    If you’re going to spend several weeks crashing at Norman Mailer‘s place, it’s probably for the best that the master of the house is no longer around. The British edition of GQ magazine is offering young writers an opportunity to stay for a month at the disheveled if singularly productive author’s former home in Provincetown, Mass., where Mailer lived from the 1990s until his death in 2007, and which has since been turned into a writers’ retreat. The Guardian reported that Dylan Jones, the magazine’s editor, announced on Tuesday the start of an annual nonfiction writing competition, open to undergraduates and post-graduates at British universities who are over the age of 18. The contest will be judged by a panel that includes GQ editors and the pop singer Lily Allen; its winner may be published in GQ, and will for certain receive a prize of about $1,600 and a July stay at the Norman Mailer Writers Colony, whose activities include writing workshops, seminars and not getting beaten with a hammer by Rip Torn.

    Mailer’s List

    By Steve Coates

    For list fans, here’s Norman Mailer’s choice of “Ten Favorite American Novels,” as of January 1988. The list, included among a selection of Mailer’s letters that The New York Review of Books published in its issue of March 12, was compiled for The Reader’s Catalogue, a project of the magazine’s founders.

    I like this list for the daring inclusion of the noirish “Postman Always Rings Twice” and, particularly, for the unapologetic recognition of two Great American Novels, “Look Homeward, Angel” and “The Grapes of Wrath,” which if I’m not mistaken were as out of literary fashion in 1988 as they are today. It’s unclear whether the order of books on the list had any significance of rank.

    In part by way of explanation for the list’s ethos, Mailer explained: “With the exception of “Huckleberry Finn,” which I reread recently, the other nine books were devoured in my freshman year at Harvard, and gave me the desire, which has never gone completely away, to be a writer, an American writer. … Freshman year at Harvard is luminous because of these books.” (Mailer graduated in 1943.)

    Ten Favorite American Novels:

    “U.S.A.,” by John Dos Passos
    “Huckleberry Finn,” by Mark Twain
    “Studs Lonigan,” by James T. Farrell
    “Look Homeward, Angel,” by Thomas Wolfe
    “The Grapes of Wrath,” by John Steinbeck
    “The Great Gatsby,” by F. Scott Fitzgerald
    “The Sun Also Rises,” by Ernest Hemingway
    “Appointment in Samarra,” by John O’Hara
    “The Postman Always Rings Twice,” by James M. Cain
    “Moby-Dick,” by Herman Melville

    Washington Post Cracks FBI’s Files on Norman Mailer

    By Dave Itzkoff
    Norman Mailer in the garment district of New York during his 1969 race for mayor. (Neal Boenzi/The New York Times)

    When you’re an outspoken critic of, oh, just about everything, as Norman Mailer was in his lifetime, perhaps it’s no surprise that the Federal Bureau of Investigation would keep a file on you. Last week, the FBI responded to a year-old Freedom of Information Act request filed by The Washington Post, and provided Mailer’s FBI files, compiled over 15 years, to the newspaper.

    Having spent a week wading through the briefings on Mailer that FBI agents frequently filed to J. Edgar Hoover, the Washington Post reports that:

    The bureau’s first confidential memo on Mailer, dated June 29, 1962, noted that the writer “admitted being a ‘Leftist’ ” and said that he had described the FBI as a “secret police organization” that should be abolished. An informant claimed that Mailer had been invited to a 1953 reception at the Polish Consulate in New York, though it was unknown whether he had attended. The memo quoted Louis Budenz, a former managing editor of the Daily Worker who broke with the Communist Party in 1945, as saying Mailer was a “concealed Communist.”

    Elsewhere in the file, an FBI agent offers his review of Mailer’s book “Miami and the Siege of Chicago,” writing:

    It is written in his usual obscene and bitter style. Book contains reference to . . . uncomplimentary statements of the type that might be expected from Mailer regarding the FBI and the Director.
