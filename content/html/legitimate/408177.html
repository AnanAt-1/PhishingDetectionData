Wed 4 Dec 1929 - The Sydney Morning Herald (NSW : 1842 - 1954)
Page 15 - DRAMATIC SCENES.
DRAMATIC SCENES.
Arrest of F. D. Green.
MURDER CHARGE.
Wounded Man's Remark-
able Story.
Swift moves by a strong posse of police,
under Inspector Lynch, resulted yesterday
in Francis Donald ("Frank") Green, 27,
labourer, being charged with the murder of
Bernard Hugh Dalton, who was shot in cold
blood outside an hotel early in November.
Green has also been charged with shooting
Walter Tomlinson and Edward Brady with
intent to murder them.
Tomlinson had told a magistrate that Green
had shot him, and the police had been
searching for him for nearly a month. Then
a woman, an underworld figure, patroness of
a gang of gunmen, whispered a significant clue
to the police, and a squad was quickly
mustered and directed to a small shack near
Cronulla.
Tomlinson is the only man who has figured
in a shooting affair in recent years who has
made a direct statement to the police, and
yesterday when Mr. V. H. Wells, stipendiary
magistrate, asked who had shot him he
unhesitatingly indicated the arrested man.
Women have played an important if de-
based part in some of the gun duels that
have occurred in the city, and one woman
told the police that if a certain man were not
arrested he would be shot.
After holding a Court at the bedside of
Tomlinson at St. Vincent's Hospital yester-
day, Mr. Wells remanded Green, for whom
Mr. Heavener appeared until December 10.
Bail was not granted.
WOUNDED MAN'S STORY.
Tomlinson, the badly-wounded man, was
white, almost haggard, but he replied unhesi-
tatingly to the questions put to him until at
last he said, "I am too ill to answer any more
questions."
The magistrate read Tomlinson's dying
depositions, a remarkable document, which
was as follows:
"My name is Walter Tomlinson and I reside
at 7 Hincks-street, South Kensington. About
6 o'clock on Saturday night, November 9,
I was outside Sharland's Strand Hotel, Crown
and William streets. 'Barney' Dalton was
with me. When I came out of the hotel we
got 'banged.'
"We didn't have time to do anything. It
was Frank Green who shot me. I heard him
say, 'Devine is waiting at the corner for me.
I saw the revolver in Green's hand. It was
a black revolver with a long barrel. I heard
Green say, 'You—you b—!' and point the
gun at Dalton. He said, 'You brought him
down for a back-up.'
"When Green fired Dalton fell, and I was
hit. I staggered. We were both put into
a motor car and brought here. I have known
Frank Green for the past seven years by
sight, but have only spoken to him about
twice. Les Garrad and Tommo Thompson
were with Green. Archer was inside the
pub. I didn't see anyone else with a re-
volver.
"There is no doubt that Green shot the
both of us. I see the revolver now, shown
to me by Sergeant Lynch. It was a similar
gun that Frank Green shot me with. I
saw blood on a third man's hand after the
shooting, but I didn't know that man. Tommy
Kelly had some drink with me and Dalton
in Sharland's Hotel, and after the shooting he
drove me and Dalton to the hospital. Green
was about six feet from me when he fired.
I had no conversation with him before he
said 'You—you b—!' and then fired. I was
speaking to Archer in the hotel just before
we went out."
(Signed) Walter Tomlinson.
"THAT'S HIM."
Mr. Wells asked the wounded man whether
the Frank Green referred to in his dying
depositions was the Frank Green present.
"That's him," said Tomllnson grimly, in-
dicating the man in custody.
Mr. Heavener (for Green) asked Tomlinson
whether he was very ill when he had made
his dying depositions. Tomlinson ruefully
admitted that he was. Mr. Heavener then
suggested to Tomlinson that his memory was
poor concerning the affair, but the wounded
man replied that his memory was clear.
Tomlinson was then asked whether, when
he made his dying depositions, he thought
that he was going to die. Tomlinson said
that he did not think so.
The unorthodox police court which had been
constituted in the hospital at the badly
wounded man's bedside was then adjourned,
and the accused was remanded.
"I can hardly get my breath," said Tom-
linson.
HOW GREEN'S ARREST WAS MADE.
After securing certain information on Mon-
day evening Inspector Lynch quickly organ-
ised a strong and heavily-armed posse of
police to make a raid. At 8 o'clock he and
Detective-sergeants Miller and Lynch, De-
tective Swasbrick and Plain-clothesmen Wal-
ker, Jones, Thompson, and Hardy, and Con-
stable Boyd, of Cronulla, set out in motor
cars for Cronulla.
Upon arrival they commenced a long and
difficult crosscountry march to a lonely hut.
The night was dark, the country difficult, no
light could be shown—not even a match could
safely be lit—hardly a word was spoken, and
the determined squad pressed forward mile
after mile. They traversed beaches and
swamps, forced their way through a tangle
of thorny undergrowth, clambered over great
boulders, and slid perilously down the sides
of small cliffs in the hills. The adventure
did not end without a casualty, for one con-
stable was slightly injured by a fall.
The first three miles was along the beach
towards Kurnell, and at times the members of
the party were splashing through water above
their ankles. They had not gone far when
they were surprised to see a figure emerge
from a tumbledown shack. Then they saw a
man with a double-barrelled shotgun in his
hands. The stranger took up a stand as if
he intended to open fire.
"STAND, OR I FIRE."
"Stand back, you —, or I'll fire," cried the
stranger in a hoarse voice.
The police stood uncertainly for a moment.
They were puzzled by this development, for
it seemed incredible that anyone could know
of their secret mission. This sudden opposi-
tion was as aggravating as it was unexpected.
They shouted to the man that they were police,
that to make a stand was useless, that he was
greatly outnumbered. The man dropped his
gun.
It transpired that he was a fisherman, who,
following recent thefts of fishing nets, was
guarding against any further depredations.
He had thought that the police were raiders
about to descend upon his nets, and he had
intended to protect them as long as he could
stand up and shoot.
The police proceeded on their way, and for
the last half mile were scrambling over rough
country. Their clothes were completely ruined,
and were drenched from the swamps through
which they had passed. They were afraid
to use a lantern, fearing that they might
be observed. As they helped one another
over the rocks, Constable Boyd slipped and
received a severe shaking as well as several
slight lacerations. At last they saw the shack,
which was a one-roomed weatherboard place,
with a tin roof almost completely hidden in
the dense undergrowth.
Inspector Lynch walked forward till near
the door and shouted for the occupants to
come out. At once a man asked, "Who's
there?" Inspector Lynch said, "We are the
police and there is no use fighting as we out-
number you."
The door was opened from inside and the
police arrested two men they found there, the
second man being charged with vagrancy.
The shack was searched but no weapons
were found and the walk back to the cars
commenced. Cronulla was reached shortly
after 3 a.m., and at 4 a.m. the party arrived
at the Darlinghurst Police Station.
It is alleged that the arrested men had
been supplied with food by friends who called
every Tuesday. Both men had been there
since November 10, according to the police
report. They showed little signs of their
stay and said that they had cut each other's
hair with a pair of clippers they found in the
shack.
