Jordan Peele's Get Out Was Originally More Disturbing

"At the end of the day, I felt like we're now in an era now where the audience needs an escape from the horror of reality."

By Drew FortunePublished: Mar 1, 2017

As the less abrasive, teddy bear half of comedy duo Key and Peele, Jordan Peele and his partner Keegan-Michael Key often used race relations as a comedic lightening rod; the pair took equal shots at the absurdity and micro-aggressions of the political climate. The now-classic "Substitute Teacher" sketch flipped the script on the "urban education" dramas of Lean on Me and Dangerous Minds, with a hot-headed African American teacher berating a class of lily white students like Samuel L. Jackson in 187. The humor hits, but it also makes you think.

In essence, Get Out is an extension of the Key and Peele formula, as the African American protagonist finds himself at the mercy of a seemingly idyllic, upper class white family. In between the obligatory jump scares and gore (Peele is an avid horror fan), the subtext of the loss of identity and cultural appropriation is scarier than any Grand Guignol set piece.

And the film, which opened Friday, has received massive acclaim. When asked about the coveted Rotten Tomatoes 100% rating of his directorial debut Get Out (as of press date, the film has been knocked down a peg to 99%) the soft-spoken comedian exhales slowly. "Man, I can't believe it. It's crazy."

The child of mixed-race parents, Peele is also an expectant father with his comedian wife Chelsea Peretti. As he told Esquire.com recently in a conversation about Get Out, Peele isn't entirely ready for fatherhood (who can be?), but it might just inspire one of his next social horror films.

There wasn't a single "eureka" moment when the Get Out idea struck, as pieces of the story had been percolating for years.

There were several "eureka" moments. There's twists, turns, and revelations that all came at different times in the gestation period of the idea. It was definitely during the Obama administration that talking about racism, or calling it out, suddenly seemed taboo. It seemed like talking about race was somehow summoning the evil of racism. We had this black president, so it didn't really get brought up as much.

While growing up on the Upper West Side and attending Sarah Lawrence College, racism was both veiled and blatant.

New York is about as cosmopolitan as it gets. It's a fairly mixed and woke town, so there weren't a lot of situations growing up where I felt like the outsider or the alien. Instances still happened, though. I was stopped by the police as a kid and accused of being a subway dart thrower. Somebody was throwing sharp darts at people, but it certainly wasn't me. That experience was very disorienting and confusing. I couldn't tell if they were messing with me, but it was my first experience of committing the crime of being black in public.

Racism comes in many different forms. Sometimes it's subtle and sometimes it's overt. Sometimes it's violent and sometimes it's harmless, but it's definitely here. It's something that I think we're all guilty of, and we just have to make sure that we deal with our own personal racism in the right way.

Get Out was a very personal project, even if Peele doesn't get nervous when surrounded by white people.

I don't get nervous, but I do look for things. I find myself very hyperaware of the social dynamic. That's just a part of being black in this country, and a part of the African-American experience. My whole life experience went into the movie.

Going to Chelsea Peretti's home for the first time to meet her parents, thankfully, wasn't nightmare fodder for the film.

It was terrifying! [Laughs] I had never encountered a group of monsters like them! No, they were amazing. Like I said, I'm not scared of white people or frightened by the interactions that we deal with in this movie. Chelsea and her family are very intelligent, funny, empathetic people. Even when I do meet people who are a little bit behind the curve, I recognize it for what it is. It's just human nature in action. It's pretty harmless stuff now, but it's just part of being a minority. It's part of the identity of being an "other" in this country. I do find it to be a reminder that race and racism is very much part of the way that many interactions go down in this country. When people were claiming we were in this post-racial era when Obama was president, those little moments were a reminder to me that we couldn't possibly be post-racial.

There was an initial tendency to push Get Out into even darker territory.

I had many different endings in mind, and many of them were darker. At the end of the day, I felt like we're now in an era now where the audience needs an escape from the horror of reality. If I'm asking them, as an audience, to sit through some racially tense, suspense scenes, I need to pay them back with some cheers at the end. I've been to about six screenings, and that response seems pretty consistent. The first half of the movie elicits some uncomfortable giggles with some jump scares here and there. By the end, the crowd is always cheering. I love getting cheers. I love giving scares. Anything that really works with the audience makes me happy. I've also seen the film with a predominantly white crowd, and there was an enormous cheer as well. Revenge and killing the deserving bad guy really works, regardless of skin color.

His improv training made stepping into the director's chair a smooth transition.

My improv background is really what made it work. The ability to adapt on the fly is essential, and I feel that's true with any job. It was a really quick shoot. Blumhouse has a way of doing things that's very efficient, but also very difficult. They work quickly, so it was a very quick shoot for an ambitious project.

Impending fatherhood is good material for horror.

I don't think you can ever be totally ready. It's a lot like when I was starting to make this movie, I thought, "If I'm not ready now, then I'll never be." But at the same time, no. I'm not ready. The whole thing is a total mad science thriller unfolding before me. As a horror guy, it's good for inspiration.

Social commentary will remain a key component in upcoming horror projects.

I love making horror, and I call these projects "social thrillers." I actually have my next four projects in mind. Any horror that works for me has, one some level, a social connotation. In some way, they represent a very real, grounded horror. Even if it's not directly clear, like Get Out, horror movies are also great when there's a vague allegory.
