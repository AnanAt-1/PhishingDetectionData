Inside Hockey's Tim Rosenthal and Biz Jacobs Discuss Hockey East, Beanpot
January 31, 2010, 9:23 PM ET [ Comments]

RSS • Archive
In the city of Boston, the arrival of February is synonymous with the Beanpot for fans of the great sport of hockey. The tournament will commence on Monday, as the TD Garden will play host to the 58th edition of the storied event.

With the Beanpot about to kick off tomorrow, and things starting to heat up in Hockey East, I chatted with a few of the experts over at Inside Hockey to get the latest on the big tourney, UNH's Blake Kessel, Bruins' prospect Tommy Cross, the early favorites for the Hobey Baker Award and much more.

Joining me for this media-to-media discussion, something I'd like to incorporate on a regular basis on here, were two individuals who dedicate an enormous amount of their time to covering collegiate hockey.

One of them is Tim Rosenthal, a 23-year-old from Easton Mass. Tim has been with Inside Hockey for two years now, and recently graduated from Suffolk University in 2009. He currently serves as an associate editor for the site.

The other is Biz Jacobs, a charming 23-year-old from Hampton, New Hampshire. Biz, who graduated from UNH in 2009, is in her first year of duty with Inside Hockey. She is the beat writer for UNH's hockey team, a contributing writer for the site's Bruins' coverage and a self-described "pain in the butt to Tim Rosenthal".

Without further ado, here is our Q&A:

JC: Let’s start with the Beanpot, an annual tournament between Harvard, Northeastern, Boston College and Boston University, which kicks off on Monday. If you could, talk about the history of the event, the tradition, and how teams have done recently.

JACOBS: Well, despite being around for 58 years, it's been the past couple of Beanpot Championships that have been unforgettable. Of course there is always the desire and motivation for teams to beat Boston University who now boasts 29 championship titles—15 more than second best Boston College (14)--with the most recent of those coming last year after beating Northeastern University 5-2. But of course, it's not always about BU. BC came back in 2008 with an incredible win against Harvard, thanks to Nick Petrecki's two goal game—the second coming in overtime—for the first two collegiate goals of his career. Regardless of how a team is playing in the regular season, you can bet that the Beanpot will be both unpredictable and full of excitement.

JC: What can we look forward to in the 58th annual Beanpot? How have each of the four teams performed thus far?

JACOBS: Be prepared to be dazzled. After all four teams have been plagued this year with injuries or rough starts, this year's Beanpot has been deemed the most balanced and evenly matched championship to date. Current national champion BU sits sixth in Hockey East after a trying year that has had coach Jack Parker pulling his hair out.

Northeastern has not had the same lineup for two consecutive games this season due to injuries, and sits tied for seventh with Vermont. Harvard is on the upswing of its season after garnering a 2-8-3 record before the break and has momentum going into this series. Perhaps the most consistent team for this year would be Boston College who went into the break with five straight wins and currently is tied with Umass-Amherst for the second spot in Hockey East.

ROSENTHAL: This year is probably the most evenly matched Beanpot in recent memory. Looking at the records, BC is the only team above .500 right now, but the other schools have gotten better during the second half. BU finally has a healthy lineup, Northeastern has been decent and Harvard had a big win over Union just recently, and in the process handed Union their first loss in ECAC play. The product on the ice should be great for the first two Mondays in February.

JC: Who do you expect to win and why?

ROSENTHAL: I expect BU to win. It is still their tournament to lose, like it is every year. They can have zero wins going into the Beanpot and they would still be the favorites. After all, I don't call it the Jack Parker Invitational for nothing.

JACOBS: If you looked strictly at the past, and even the games played between these teams this year, Boston University has taken it to Boston College in some of the most publicized games--for example the Frozen Fenway game. However, with the way BC has been playing throughout the season, I would not be surprised if they took home the championship this year. Brian Gibbons, who held the MVP title for the 2008 Beanpot championship, still leads the way for BC with 28 points, and he will play an important role for this year's series as well.

JC: One of the players participating will be BC d-man Tommy Cross, who the Bruins selected 35th overall in 2007. Cross has had some injury issues, can you talk about those?

JACOBS: Cross missed the last 12 games last season due to a reoccurring knee injury he sustained back at Westminster High. Most recently, he also sprained his MCL and will probably be sitting the Beanpot out. With all the injuries the Bruins have sustained, it seems pretty on par for the course that one of their prospects also be plagued with injury.

JC: What do you make of Cross as a player? What are his best attributes? Does he have NHL potential?

ROSENTHAL: I think Cross is a good two-way defenseman, but his best asset I would say is his puck-moving ability. He is a good point man on the power play, and has a pretty good shot too.

JACOBS: I think one thing he has going for him is his size. As a sophomore he's 6'3 and weighs 195 pounds—and there's still time to grow stronger, and he will need to in order to produce at the professional level.

JC: Last year's Hobey Baker winner Matt Gilroy, who captained BU to a national championship, is having a pretty solid season for the Rangers. Do you see any standout upperclassmen in Hockey East having a shot at replicating what Gilroy did, as he quickly found success in the NHL despite not being drafted?

JACOBS: Of course there are a number of outstanding players in the Hockey East conference, but the one that sticks out the most to me, is UNH Hobey hopeful Bobby Butler. Butler has been a successful key to UNH since he started there, but this year he stepped up his game even more. Posting 36 points so far this year (18 goals and 18 assists), as well as becoming part of the century club at UNH, Butler has been a force that teams don't know how to reckon with. His line with Phil DeSimone and Paul Thompson has been nearly impossible to stop this year and the points just keep coming.

JC: The Avalanche, who seemingly own the rights to half the Terriers’ lineup, went and grabbed Kieran Millan in the 5th round of the draft last summer after the netminder's outstanding freshman season. How has Millan performed this year?

ROSENTHAL: Millan has been better as of late. He was a bit shaky early on and was going down the route of BC goaltender John Muse, who also had a sophomore slump last year after the Eagles won the National Championship in 2007-08. Millan had two huge wins against BC, including the Fenway game, and he should get better as we head down the home stretch.

JACOBS: There is no doubt in anyone's mind that Kieran Millan was incredible last year. While he still is an outstanding goaltender, I think he also had a hot season and he is cooling down a little bit. He has a .881 save percentage this year while keeping a 7-10-0 record—still commendable but not consistent with his efforts last year. We know he has the skills, and if placed with the right defense and if he remains consistent, Millan will be a great pick up for the Avs.

JC: Blake Kessel, Phil's younger brother, has 6-21-27 totals in 22 games for UNH. Sounds like Blake, a sophomore defenseman for the Wildcats, is the more generous of the two brothers when it comes to sharing the puck. What do you make of the younger Kessel, picked in the 6th round in '07 by the Islanders?

JACOBS: Whenever I watch Blake on the ice, I constantly have to remind myself that he's only a sophomore. He has a dominance and poise out on the ice that exudes confidence. The UNH coaching staff has relied on him much like they would a senior defenseman, and he has awarded them with an outstanding season and 27 points. He is always willing to give the credit to another player on a nice play and take blame upon himself after a lack-luster flub. He is smart in his own zone and his transition game is spot on. I think it's fair to say whenever he's on the ice, the entire UNH bench breathes a little bit easier.

JC: Any Hober Baker candidates jump out at you in Hockey East this season?

JACOBS: Well, as I answered previously, Bobby Butler remains a top candidate for the award this year, and rightly so. However, another candidate you have to look at in Hockey East is UMass-Amherst's junior forward, James Marcou. While he doesn't have nearly as many goals as Butler, he has a staggering 32 assists and 8 goals for 40 total points. So, even when he's not putting them in himself, he's making the opportunities and capitalizing on them while remaining a constant for the team all season.

ROSENTHAL: Marcou is one that really stands out right now. Scott McLaughlin, who does our Hobey Watch every week at Inside Hockey, consistently has him in the top ten and with good reason. He isn't an elite goal scorer, but he reminds me a little bit of David Krejci in his playmaking ability. He should be up there, and I wouldn't be surprised if you see Gustav Nyquist, a sophomore center for Maine, get consideration as well.

JC: Who are the top players the Bruins, who have a heap of picks next summer, should consider drafting from the conference?

ROSENTHAL: Well obviously a lot remains to be seen on those draft picks in the first two rounds, but there are some names that the Bruins could take a look at in the later rounds. Freshman Sean Escobedo from BU comes to mind as a mid to late round pick who is pretty quick at his position and Billy Arnold, who will be coming to BC in 2011 could also be someone the Bruins look at.

JACOBS: Well again, you have to look at the top Hobey candidates from Hockey East. Both Butler and Marcou are undrafted free agents and have shown their capabilities on the ice all year. And I'm sure, with Butler born and raised in Massachusetts, and Marcou getting a taste of Boston hockey with his time at UMass, neither would be disappointed to stick around the New England area for a while.
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Great thanks to both Tim and Biz for their time and their stellar insight. If you'd like to check out more from them: Inside Hockey's Website
