La Route Verte in Quebec, Canada - Experiences?
Hey Everyone,

I'm finishing my last year in University, and trying to plan a graduation vacation for May 2005. I got into cycling about 2 years ago, primarily as a commuter, and was doing 30km a day for a summer. I bought last months Bicycling magazine which is how I first heard about La Route Verte. One piece of advice I seemed to notice consistently was to plan well in advance! I have never done a bike tour before... but the idea REALLY appeals to me, especially since after graduation I can easily find the time.

I'm not sure about my route... but I'd most likely fly to Montreal to start, and follow the coast of the St Laurent.. maybe... probably aim for a 14 day trip.



Some questions that I'd appreciate any info on!

1. Have any of you done La Route Verte? What portions did you do and what did you think of it? Would you recommend it to someone who's never done a bike tour before?

2. For a first bike tour, would it be too ambitious to try to go on a self sufficient tour? My companion is someone experienced in camping (altho he has never done a bike tour either).

3. Any advice of renting bike vs bringing bike, and renting panniers vs buying panniers? I'm riding a Norco Volante.. do you think that'd be a good touring bike?

4. What kinda budget in terms of $$ per day should I budget for: a) credit card touring vs b) self sufficient touring. Money is a factor for me.

5. For newbies, how many KM should we expect to complete each day? Both of us have spent a fair amount on a bike, but for myself I haven't done over 60KM at one time yet.. but I'm willing to train lots too.

Um yah that's my questions so far Appreciate any info, tips or testimonies!

-xan-

I rode the Route Verte this summer from Montreal to Quebec City, then from Quebec City through the Cantons de est. to Vermont and NY.The routes I rode were good, The signage was poor in some areas, The $21 (US) I paid for the english version of the guide was a waste of money(no campsites was listed). I'm told the french language guides are better, (I did much better with the $4 maps I bought). There are not many camping choices between Montreal and Trios Rivieres. Those were the only negatives I had while riding in Quebec.
In September I went back with my wife and rode our tandem on the Route Verte' from Quebec City to St Andre'. It was a great ride to St Andre the roads were very good and the vies of the St Laurent were often spectaclar! We took a train back to Quebec City
Quebec province is probaly the cleanest place I have ever been, the people are very friendly and helpful, and even though I don't speak french there was never a problem communicating. I never had any problems with motorists even on busy roads without a shoulder or bikelane. The Route Verte from Quebec to St Andre was very scenic and my favorite part of the route. I have been told the route from Rivere du-loup to Gaspie can be challenging with lots of hills and often sharing the road( no shoulder) with large trucks.
One thing to keep in mind is that the wind tends to blow strong from the west especially on the South shore of the St Laurent, I spent 3 challenging days riding into the headwind during my tour. Also don't let the maps of Quebec that show roads that run in perfectly lines fool you into thinking they are flat, In quebec they don't waste asphalt by winding roads up a hill, they go straight up the hills and straight down the other side! I climbed some incredibly steep hills in the Cantons De-l- Est that looked flat on the maps!

Rick

Hi there,
I rode from Montreal to Gaspe last summer. It took about 10 days (I actually started in Kingston, and it was 14 days from Kingston). I didn't really follow la route verte all the time, just rode on highway 132 to Quebec, crossed to the other side, and followed 138 to Gaspe! Pretty simple... La route verte follows these roads most of the time, if I recall correctly, but at some points it makes you do little detours (that I didn't do). Most of it is pretty flat, and the traffic isn't too bad as long as highway 20 is still there, because most cars would go on it instead of the 132 and 138. So it gets really busy only at the end. It's also where it gets really hilly. But I guess la route verte officially ends up berfore that. If you start from Montreal you should get a tail wind most of the time. It was actually pretty easy, so I ended up doing 150 km per day (averaging up to 26 km/h some days!). There are campsites all along the way, most of them in beautiful sites along the St Lawrence river.
Hope that helps,
Claire

The Route Verte is a huge pleasure. It's remarkably easy from Gatineau/Hull to Quebec (I did Montreal-Quebec in one day, i.e. 275 km, fully loaded)... but extremely difficult after Quebec (north-east-bound) if you stay on the north shore. You'd better choose the south shore to Gaspesia... which is not flat either. There is always a spot to stop (hotel, camping site, motel, B&B), except if you are too early or too late in the year (before June or after September).

I agree with VéloNomad. Get a provincial map as well as regional maps such as the "tourist maps" sold by Mapart, and you will have information on major trails, as well as roads, local roads, and the like. The "tourist maps" by Mapart differenciate between gravel and paved roads (most of the time) and indicate quite a few campsites. The official government-sponsored camping book (free) will list most hotels, campgrounds and tourist places.

As for the actual books, both in English and French, they simply list a series of turns with very sketchy maps, disregarding the quality of the "route verte", attractions, other nice roads, etc. You would get as much information by printing the various regional maps seen at https://routeverte.com (yes, I know there aren't many details, but there aren't much more in the book), and by following information found in the regional tourist maps and at various regional information centres.

As for the Route verte itself, well, it depends.
It generally follows interesting routes outside of cities, but not as much in cities. Their design criteria calls for avoidance of roads, unless there is a bike way (oftentimes a very poorly designed bi-directional "path" on a single side of the road, separated from the highway by a good white line..., or unless there is a paved shoulder or really light traffic.
For instance, between Montréal and Trois-Rivières, I'd prefer highway 138 between Montréal and Berthierville rather than the Route verte which goes along the L'Assomption river (lots of historic buildings on that stretch). Ditto between Yamachiche and Québec City. On the other hand, the Route verte follows a nicer path between Berthierville and Yamachiche.

Some stretches have been built as a railtrail. The Petit Train du Nord follows a much more scenic -- and much easier -- path than the very busy highway 117. Ditto for the Piste Jacques-Cartier-Portneuf or for Le Petit Témis. THe Véloroute des Bleuets has many great offroad sections which are great, except for a few 15% grades when the road crosses under the highway in a deep culvert.

And finally, a few stretches, including some of the nicest were accessible with a $15 season's pass.

Hi Xanatos

In 2002, my wife and I participated in a three day tour of the veloroute des bleuets (blueberry trail) which is part of the Route Verte circuit. We stayed in a bed and breakfast the first day and left our car there for the duration of the tour. We stayed at a major hotel in Alma and in Dolbeau-Mistassini. The following web site which is in both French and English will provide you with all the details you need regarding accommodations, the route, etc. The route which is approximately 300 km in length is incredible and certainly worth the trip to get there. There are either signs or painted arrows on the road telling you where you should turn to be on the right track. You will be cycling close to Lac St-Jean, go through a first nation village, the Parc National Taillon and cycle by beautiful falls in Dolbeau. The food is excellent and the prices are very reasonable. On your way back from Alma, you can stop and hike or kayak in the Parc du Saguenay, depending on how much time you have, you could also hike in a park called "Les Hautes Gorges de la Riviere Malbaie, which is absolutely incredible. The following web site has very detailed information about Quebec parcs. If biking is your only interest, you can bike on bike only trails in Quebec City, Montreal, Rimouski and in the Parc de la Mauricie. The Route Verte Web Site also proposes many other long rides.

www.sepaq.com
www.veloroute-bleuets.qc.ca

Hope this will be of help

JJD

OK, Here are a few answers.
Originally Posted by xanatos
2. For a first bike tour, would it be too ambitious to try to go on a self sufficient tour? My companion is someone experienced in camping (altho he has never done a bike tour either).
No, I don't think it is too ambitious, but a lot depends on you and your equipment. You need a decent bike in good order (I doN't know your Norco), but also good equipment. You should do a few single-day ridea and shake-down trips (2-3 days) with your friend to develop stamina and iron out issues that might exist. And do at least one of these on a rainy weekend.
Amongst issues: what happens if it rains? Do you ride together all the time or do you ride on your own until you reach a meeting point? There is no right or wrong answer, except both partners need to have similar expectations.

Another issue, especially for a new tourer, is that you should have a "Plan B". IOW, if you get nasty weather, or find it too hard, or feel more tired than expected, you need to be able to limit your distance and change your tour in to a "Tour du lac" (i.e. ride around the lake).


Originally Posted by xanatos
3. Any advice of renting bike vs bringing bike, and renting panniers vs buying panniers?
The only bikes I have seen for rent in Montréal are hybrids (a bit like yours), with a rear rack. No front rack. I haven't seen panniers for rent (though MEC and La Cordée might rent some). So I think rentals would fit more if you sleep in hotels or, at minimum, in hostels.




Originally Posted by xanatos
5. For newbies, how many KM should we expect to complete each day? Both of us have spent a fair amount on a bike, but for myself I haven't done over 60KM at one time yet.. but I'm willing to train lots too.

I would say anything between 150-200 km on flat terrain -- which means the St. Lawrence Valley -- and 50-80 km in really hilly terrain. An average of 100-130 km seems a reasonable expectation.
On the other hand, at your point, I would say you should start with 50-70 km per day maximum for the first few days and build up from there. Remember that if you have never done more than 60 km at once yet, you need to make sure your hands and rear end will support cycling day after day after day....
