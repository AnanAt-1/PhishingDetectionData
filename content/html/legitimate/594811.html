    Goodell helps in pool and out

    Share this:

    • Click to share on Facebook (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on Twitter (Opens in new window)
    • Click to share on Reddit (Opens in new window)
    • Click to print (Opens in new window)
    By Randy Youngman | southlandgolfry@aol.com | Contributor
    PUBLISHED: October 4, 2011 at 5:23 PM PDT

    Even at age 52, former Olympic gold medalist Brian Goodell still has to set his alarm clock to go swim laps early in the morning.

    That’s because he is now a training partner for an Olympic hopeful.

    Her name is Janet Evans — yes, that Janet Evans — a four-time Olympic gold medalist who came out of retirement this summer and, at age 40, is training competitively to try to qualify for the U.S. Olympic Team Trials in Omaha (June 25-July 2) and then, hopefully, the 2012 London Olympics.

    Goodell, a Mission Viejo resident, and Evans, a Laguna Beach resident, have been training partners for the past 10 months, usually swimming early mornings at Cypress College or Golden West College.

    “We meet at 4:45 and drive up together,” Goodell said. “It’s been fun helping Janet with her comeback bid, and it’s been really good for me, health-wise, because I’ve lost 20 pounds. I never thought I’d swim this much again.”

    Goodell will take a break from his training duties to participate in Sunday’s second “Celebrity Charity Walk ‘n’ Roll,” a fundraising walk that begins at Heritage Park in Irvine (10 a.m-2 p.m.) to raise awareness and financial support for fighting Facioscapulohumeral Muscular Dystrophy (FSH MD), the most prevalent form of the progressively debilitating muscle disease.

    Event host is Max Adler, who stars as Dave Karofsky in the Fox-TV series “Glee” and will participate along with more than 20 Hollywood celebrities and well-known athletes, including Olympic swimming champions Kaitlin Sandeno and Goodell.

    The event is open to the public, with a tax-deductible fee of $25 to register for the walk or a $40 fee for the walk and a VIP lunch with the celebs that is sponsored by Dave & Busters of Irvine. To register, or for more information, contact Amy Bekier (619-972-0993) or go to the online site: fshsociety.org/IrvineWalk.

    Goodell also keeps busy these days by selling real estate, serving as a motivational speaker (see goldmedalminds.com) and participating in numerous community and charity events. But he has a personal connection to this cause, because one of his Olympic teammates, former breaststroker Rick Collela, has a son that was diagnosed with FSH MD. Collela’s family has been running a foundation in Seattle to raise money to find a cure for the disease.

    After Sunday’s walk, Goodell will head back to the pool to help Evans, now a mother of two, with her goals. Earlier this summer, she set age-group records in the 400-meter and 800-meter freestyles in her first competition in 15 years, at the Janet Evans Invitational in Fullerton.

    Two months later in August, she lowered her own Masters world record in the 400 (4:22.87) during the Southern Pacific Masters Association’s long course championships in Mission Viejo. She’ll need to post a time of 4:19.39 to qualify for the Olympic Trials.

    “Janet is doing really, really well,” Goodell said. “She’s made a ton of progress, and I’m pretty sure she’ll qualify for Trials. It’s pretty amazing considering she hadn’t competed in so long.”

    Goodell won his gold medals and set world records in the 400- and 1,500-meter freestyle at the 1976 Olympics in Montreal, at age 17; Evans also was 17 when she won three golds at the 1988 Olympics in Seoul and added a gold and a silver in the ’92 Olympics in Barcelona. She retired after the 1996 Games in Atlanta, where she handed off the Olympic torch to Muhammad Ali and finished sixth in the 800 freestyle.

    But it’s never too late to get up early. Ask Goodell and Evans.

    Scioscia weighs in: If there were any doubt about Manager Mike Scioscia’s interest in succeeding Tony Reagins as Angels general manager, he ended it Monday.

    “I’m happy with what I’m doing,” Scioscia said by phone. “I have no aspirations to be the GM.”

    That actually is not a surprise, but team president John Carpino made it an issue during Friday’s conference call when he said “it would probably be a question for Mike” when someone asked if Scioscia would be considered.

    Scioscia said he “always had a good relationship with Tony,” but he declined to comment further on Reagins stepping down to accept an unspecified role as special assistant to team chairman Dennis Kuhl.

    Double ouch: As if it isn’t painful enough for everyone in the Angels organization to watch the playoffs on TV, you can imagine what Reagins and owner Arte Moreno were thinking when Mike Napoli hit a go-ahead two-run homer in Texas’ 4-3 victory over Tampa Bay on Monday and Adrian Beltre (who spurned Reagins’ free-agent offers last winter) hit three homers in the Rangers’ ALDS clincher Tuesday.
