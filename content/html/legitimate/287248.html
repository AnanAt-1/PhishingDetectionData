Previews

Beyond Good & Evil

By Benjamin Turner | Sep 12, 2003
A girl, a pig and a camera make for a compelling new adventure.
Beyond Rayman

Do you know Michel Ancel? You will soon. He rose to fame on the non-existent shoulders of the limbless platform hero Rayman, becoming one of the most well-respected game creators in Europe. A few years back, though, he got tired of making Rayman games. He wanted to create something more meaningful, a game that would make a statement and maybe leave something with players who finished it. And so he embarked on "Project BG&E," which would eventually become known as Beyond Good & Evil. Now, with the game nearly finished, Ancel's vision looks like it's coming together beautifully.

Beyond Good & Evil (BG&E) is the story of Jade, a surprisingly tough, 20 year-old "action reporter" living on the fantastical world of Hyllis. An orphan, she runs a small orphanage for other abandoned children. The surplus of orphans is due to the ongoing war between Hyllis and the soul-sucking, extra-terrestrial invaders known as DomZ. The pseudo-military Alpha Legion is supposed to protect the common people from DomZ attack, but they're not around when the orphanage's shield goes down and the DomZ storm in. Only through a heroic effort by Jade and her porcine friend, Peyj, are all their lives saved. Jade passes out, and when she comes to feels a new resolve. She'll find the truth behind these suspicious invasions, and disseminate it to the cowering populace. She and Peyj set out on their journey, but neither could fathom the true depths of the terrifying conspiracy they would soon uncover ...

... I could say more, but I really don't want to spoil things for you.

Taste of Adventure

It's hard to sum up BG&E in a word or two, but "Adventure" probably comes the closest to doing it justice. From what I've played so far, it offers a compelling mix of both story content and traditional action-based gameplay. It should be noted that Ancel started the project with just a story in mind, and the gameplay was crafted to best tell that story -- not vice-versa. The result? A free-roaming virtual world that Jade can explore on foot or in a variety of vehicles. Ancel places a premium on player freedom, so the world is yours to explore as you see fit.

Those shields keep the DomZ out ... hopefully.
The on-foot sequences, which account for a majority of the game so far, bear a passing resemblance to the style of Zelda: Ocarina of Time. Jade can walk around, interact with the environment and talk to people (the dialogue seems well-written), take pictures with her camera, or pull out her staff to lay into enemies. The combat system is fairly simple button mashing, where the main challenge is knowing when to attack what and when to dodge out of the way. Luckily, combat is infrequent enough at this point that the somewhat simple mechanics don't overstay their welcome. The camera, meanwhile, is used to take pictures of the exotic fauna she encounters along the way -- certain zoologically-minded types will pay a lot for pictures of rare species.

Peyj and a holographic Spaniard are two of Jade's more endearing companions.
Jade is accompanied by Peyj, who looks like nothing more than a bipedal hog. Peyj is a very important part of the early going, helping Jade solve puzzles and fighting by her side during melees. Peyj actually has his own health meter for you to keep an eye on, and you can hand him food items, as necessary, to keep him healthy. When Peyj is not around later on, you'll also be able to enlist the services of Double H, a serious, devoted soldier who'd rather help Jade find the truth than follow immoral orders.
