How Much Will the City Have to Pay Out if the Viaduct Collapses Before 2016?

Dan Savage

Help me out here, Sloggers.

I don't know much about liability law—I'm no lawyer—but here's the tape of Richard Conlin, President of the Seattle City Council and lying sack of shit, at the council's Feb 28th meeting. Conlin puts on his best condescending liberal douche face and then shows some pictures of a double-decker freeway that collapsed in Oakland, California, during an earthquake in 1989. Forty two people were juiced to death when the Cypress Street Viaduct collapsed. Conlin then shows pictures of cracks that appeared in the Alaska Way Viaduct after the 2001 Nisqually earthquake. The viaduct, says Conlin, is a deathtrap, a clear and present danger, and something has to be done. If there's even a small earthquake—hell, if a tourist bumps the damn thing with a stroller—the viaduct is going to come crashing down and scores of people are gonna die.

"These pictures remind us that we're talking about people's lives and their livelihoods," the lying sack of shit lies on. "We are talking about safety and risk for people who travel on this corridor."

First, if Richard "LSOS" Conlin was actually concerned about saving lives, he wouldn't be backing the tunnel. Here's the relevant bit from Dom's excellent, must-read feature on the tunnel in this week's paper:

Conlin's grandstanding—his willingness to exploit tragedy to score a political point—wasn't just patronizing, it was dishonest. Building the tunnel means that the viaduct remains up longer. Demolition had been planned for next year—in 2012—but the tunnel plan delays demolition until 2016 (or later, if the project runs behind schedule, as projects like these often do).

"The city council has chosen the option that leaves the existing viaduct up the longest," says Mayor Mike McGinn.

Building the tunnel isn't about saving lives. I'm not sure what it's about—groupthink, cowardice, a refusal on the part of Seattle's electeds to protect Seattle's interests, the desire of our cowardly, do-nothing City Council to be seen doing something (even if it's the wrong thing because, hey, they'll all be long gone when the bill comes due)—but it is not about saving lives. If he was worried about saving lives, Conlin would be calling for the immediate closure of the Alaskan Way Viaduct. If this was about saving lives, the city would close the viaduct today, right now, before the next trembler or wayward tourist brought the whole thing down.

Now here's what I'm curious about: With the Richard "LSOS" Conlin and the mayor—and the previous mayor and the governor—all having acknowledged that the Alaskan Way Viaduct is a death trap, what happens if the thing falls down tomorrow? Or sometime between now and its currently scheduled closure in 2016? What kind of liability has the city incurred by acknowledging that the Alaskan Way Viaduct is a death trap—a threat to the lives and livelihoods of all who use it—and then letting drivers use it anyway for the next five years? Or longer? How much money will the city have to pay out in wrongful death suits? Millions? Tens of millions? Hundreds of millions?
