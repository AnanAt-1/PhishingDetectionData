    REVIEWS:

  • "The Acrobats" (1955)
    "On the whole, this depressing story is well told; its most violent and tasteless episodes give evidence of originality and a good eye and ear. . . . With this novel out of his system, Mr. Richler’s second one may be an entirely mature and rewarding book."

  • "The Apprenticeship of Duddy Kravitz" (1959)
    "Mordecai Richler is a young Canadian author who has been praised highly in his now country for his clear-eyed vision and his realistic style. The present novel will confirm that estimate with many readers."

  • "Stick Your Neck Out" (1963)
    "[Richler] has a ruthlessly antic imagination and he leaves few aspects of Canadian civilization unscarred. Some of his best darts will find targets in the United States."

  • "Cocksure" (1968)
    "I was not sure how to take 'Cocksure.' A satire about a Wasp from Ontario? British publishing? Hollywood movie-makers? Ambivalent protofascist English attitudes towards Jews? The male sex organ?"

  • "St. Urbain's Horseman" (1971)
    "That 'St. Urbain’s Horseman' is as effective as it is a tribute to Mr. Richler’s way with the word. For the truth is that he has triumphed in spite of his material."

  • "Jacob Two-Two Meets the Hooded Fang" (1975)
    "Mordecai Richler is a funny man, a good writer, and everyone should go out tomorrow morning and beat his local bookseller into submission if he hasn't got a nice plump display of books titled 'Jacob Two-Two Meets the Hooded Fang.'"

  • "Notes on an Endangered Species"
    "At times, [Richler] looks nervously like a bomber without a base, a novelist searching for a vantage point from which he might give his satire a more coherent direction and a larger shape. The effort is admirable; and in the meantime, the comedy speaks to a recognizable distress."

  • "Joshua Then and Now" (1980)
    " . . . all in all 'Joshua Then and Now' is a remarkable bittersweet accomplishment. Its slapstick and pathos blend artfully to make a wine of unique flavor."

  • "Home Sweet Home" (1984)
    "What is Mr. Richler's secret? How has he contrived to defeat the universal (in New York, anyway) belief that all news from above the 49th parallel is cataleptically boring?"

  • "The Best of Modern Humor" (1984)
    "Mordecai Richler, a funny writer but one wise enough to be a serious novelist, has collected 'The Best of Modern Humor.' His sole criterion for the material he chose, he says in his introduction, was that 'it had to make me laugh.' That is hard to argue with. But somebody's got to do it."

  • "Jacob Two-Two and the Dinosaur" (1987)
    "The range and bite of this novel's hilarity will come as no surprise to fans of Mordecai Richler's adult fiction and of his previous book for children . . . Yet what's astonishing is how much of the humor here strikes both adult and child as genuinely funny."

  • "Solomon Gursky Was Here (1990)
    "Regardless of what its author may actually have experienced, 'Solomon Gursky Was Here' reads as if it were great fun to write. Dense, intricately plotted, it takes exuberant, nose-thumbing joy in traditional storytelling with all its nervy cliffhangers and narrative hooks, its windfall legacies, stolen portraits, murders and revenges, its clues that drop on the story line with a satisfying thud."

  • "Oh Canada! Oh Quebec!" (1992)
    "Mr. Richler is often quite funny, even as he sinks his barbs deeply into the flesh of those responsible for 'the day's idiocies.' In 'Oh Canada! Oh Quebec! Requiem for a Divided Country,' he is, for the most part, simply angry."

  • "This Year in Jerusalem (1994)
    "In 1992 the Canadian novelist Mordecai Richler traveled to Israel. 'This Year in Jerusalem' is the report of what he saw there and the people he met. He lets them speak for themselves and it makes lively reporting. . . . 'This Year in Jerusalem' is history made personal and it rings true."

    FILM REVIEW:

  • Dreams Rampant in ‘Apprenticeship of Duddy Kravitz’ (1974)
    "Perhaps because of the close collaboration between Mr. Richler and [film director Ted] Kotcheff . . . the film looks very much like a novelist’s movie. Such an abundance of visual and narrative detail is essentially literary. This could mean disaster for a movie but not this time."
