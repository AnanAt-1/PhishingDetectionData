    Mets Can Follow Tigers’ Lead

    By Mike Flam

    The Detroit Tigers pulled the plug on Dontrelle Willis. Is it time for the Mets to do the same with Oliver Perez?

    The Tigers designated Willis for assignment Saturday. If he is not traded or claimed off waivers in 10 days, the Tigers will release him.

    Willis broke into the majors with a flourish, going 14-6 for the Florida Marlins in 2003 and being named the National League’s rookie of the year. After the 2007 season, he was dealt to the Tigers with Miguel Cabrera in a deal that sent the highly regarded prospect Cameron Maybin and five others to Florida.

    In parts of the next three seasons marred by physical problems, Willis went 2-8 with a 6.86 earned run average in 24 games.

    Perez has not been quite that bad for the Mets, and they are on the hook for considerably more than what’s left of Willis’s $12 million salary for this season. After allowing three runs in two innings of relief Saturday, his E.R.A. is up to 6.25. This after his 6.82 mark from last year.

    The Mets owe Perez another $12 million for next season before his contract expires, but should they follow the Tigers’ lead and get rid of their quirky left-hander before they are contractually obligated to?

    Some Are Partying Like It’s 2006

    By Justin Sablich

    The year was 2006. That summer, Fidel Castro relinquished his control (temporarily) over Cuba. “The Da Vinci Code” was captivating audiences around the globe. And Kevin Richardson broke a million hearts by leaving the Backstreet Boys.

    Eric Miller/Reuters 2006 was Barry Zito’s last season with the Oakland Athletics.
    Jeff Zelevansky/Reuters Dontrelle Willis’s last effective season was in 2006.

    In baseball, a heartthrob named Barry Zito was pitching in the final year of his contract with the Oakland Athletics amid trade rumors. It wasn’t the best season of his career, but it turned out to be better than any season he would have with his next team, the San Francisco Giants.

    That winter, the Giants signed Zito to a seven-year, $126 million contract, the highest of any pitcher at the time. During the three years since he signed that deal, Giants fans watched in frustration as Zito compiled a 31-43 record and a 4.56 earned run average.

    But just as some were ready to give up on Zito, he is finally showing life and the form that won him a Cy Young award in 2002, as Tyler Kepner recently detailed.

    Zito is not the only player flashing signs of his old self. In less dramatic fashion, Dontrelle Willis is turning out quality starts again, and if they continue, it will be the first season he has consistently done that since 2006.

    It’s a stretch to say that Willis looks like the same pitcher that burst onto the scene in 2003, going to the All-Star Game and winning the National League Rookie of the Year award. But the fact that he’s pitching to major league hitters at all is impressive enough after the way things have gone for him since arriving in Detroit.

    Big Talent, Big Appetite

    By Tyler Kepner

    JUPITER, Fla. – The best-known Mike Stanton in baseball is the one who pitched in more than 1,000 games and helped the Yankees win four World Series. That might be about to change.

    The Mike Stanton wearing No. 80 for the Florida Marlins this spring is an outfielder ranked as the No. 3 prospect in the majors. He is 20 years old and slugged 28 homers with 92 runs batted in between Class A and AA last season.

    One thing he cannot do is cook. One thing he can do very well is eat. Stanton, who is 6-foot-5, 240 pounds, is rooming with Marlins center fielder Cameron Maybin this spring, and he devours the lasagna and chicken dishes Maybin cooks.

    “I don’t think he can find a stove,” Maybin said Sunday, as the Marlins were losing to the Nationals, 9-7, at Roger Dean Stadium. “He can put it down, though. I’ve never seen anything like it.”

    Except at meal time, Maybin said, Stanton pretty much keeps to himself.

    “I’m not a big fan of roommates,” Maybin said. “But he’s like a big mouse. I never hear him around the house.”

    The Marlins have said they won’t rush Stanton, who is likely headed to Class AA again, but Maybin, who turns 23 in April, should hold down the No. 2 spot in the major league lineup between National League Rookie of the Year Chris Coghlan and N.L. batting champ Hanley Ramirez.

    Maybin had shoulder surgery in the off-season, and while he said the shoulder was not back to normal, he added that the pain was “definitely gone”. Slowed by a groin injury this spring, he recovered to bash two homers on Saturday in Viera against the Nationals.

    Until he stays healthy and makes an impact in the majors, Maybin will be best known as one of the six players Florida acquired from Detroit for Miguel Cabrera and Dontrelle Willis in Dec. 2007. But not to him.

    “That’s the last thing that ever crosses my mind, that trade,” he said. “I came over with five other guys. It’s really the last thing I ever think about.”

    For all his culinary skills, Maybin clearly believes his future is in the major leagues. You can tell that without even talking to him: he has a tattoo of the Major League Baseball logo on his left shoulder.

    The Watch List

    By Jim Luttrell

    The early season is not always about young players trying to prove they belong. Sometimes, it is about veterans searching for lost magic.

    Lance Berkman often has a bad month and still ends up with reliable numbers by the end of the season. So why should his .162 April, which ended with a 1-for-23 string, be cause for alarm? Well, perhaps because it came after he hit .171 last September. Berkman has five homers, so apparently he still has bat speed at 33. Maybe his mental health day on Wednesday or the turning of the calendar will straighten his course.

    Is Andruw Jones back? After two miserable and unexplainable seasons, Jones is batting .333 with three homers for Texas. He will get some regular at-bats with Josh Hamilton (ribs) going on the disabled list. But as his playing time increased, Jones, 32, hit .235 over six games, with five strikeouts in 17 at-bats.

    Dontrelle Willis looked as if he could rejoin the Tigers soon but took a step back in his rehabilitation from an anxiety disorder. On Friday, in his first start for Class AAA Toledo, he needed 102 pitches to get through five innings. He gave up three runs, seven hits and two walks and hit a batter. Three-fifths of the Tigers’ rotation has an earned run average of 6.00 or more, so perhaps they could do worse than Willis.

    Schreiber’s Notes: Three Lefties to Trade For

    By Jay Schreiber
    Dontrelle Willis wears his cap slightly crooked and has a captivating personality. But the past two seasons, he has performed below expectations. (Jeff Zelevansky/Reuters)

    The Red Sox signing Curt Schilling the other day is a signal that, for a relatively reasonable amount of money ($8 million plus incentives), the team didn’t see anybody out there on the free agent market that was very attractive. Boston could have explored trades, but instead they locked up Schilling before he could get away.

    There is no great free agent option out there. But there are three lefties that may or may not be up for grabs in a trade situation: Johan Santana of the Twins, Dontrelle Willis of the Marlins and Scott Kazmir of the Devil Rays — er, correction, the Rays.

    Those guys might be available, particularly Willis as he gets closer to free agency and seems to be stuck in a rut in terms of his pitching.

    Can The ‘Great’ Bonds Hit Two on Sunday?

    By Jack Curry

    Barry Bonds was warming up in left field before Saturday’s game when he stopped to listen to a special message on the center-field scoreboard. It was Wayne Gretzky, who was known as the Great One, speaking to Bonds, who is close to becoming the greatest home run hitter ever.

    Gretzky congratulated Bonds, reminded Bonds not to stop at 756 homers and said he would be rooting for Bonds as Bonds tried to “chase down history.” That chase will continue. Bonds failed to homer off Dontrelle Willis, but the Giants rallied for two runs in the ninth inning to edge the Florida Marlins, 4-3.

    Bonds went 0 for 3 with a walk off Willis, the animated left-hander with the corkscrew motion. So Bonds remained stuck at 754 homers, one short of tying Hank Aaron for the career record. Bonds has one more home game at AT&T Park on Sunday before the Giants travel to Los Angeles and San Diego for six games.

    For three of Bonds’s four at-bats, Willis challenged him. Willis pumped four fastballs to Bonds in the first inning before striking him out with a 96-mile-per-hour heater. With the eager fans chanting, “Bar-ree! Bar-ree!” Willis threw a perfectly placed fastball on the inside corner. Bonds could not extend his arms and waved through the pitch.

    Bonds popped out on the second pitch in the third, walked in the sixth and popped out on the first pitch with two on in the seventh. On both pop-ups, Bonds barely budged 15 feet out of the batter’s box.

    Unless Bonds hits two homers Sunday, he will leave for what could be an awkward trip. He does not want to set the record away from San Francisco, so he is likely to sit out at least two of those games.

    On July 19, Bonds hit two homers off the Chicago Cubs. Does anyone believe he can repeat that feat against the Marlins on Sunday, pass Aaron and complete this pursuit with a flourish? Bonds and the Giants’ fans want to believe it. Do you?
