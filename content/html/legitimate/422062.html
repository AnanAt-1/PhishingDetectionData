Pride and Prejudice: Week 7-Post B

After reading Pride and Prejudice I felt very fuzzy and warm after such a wonderful and optimistic outlook on how love turns out on the last 10 pages, however, after the fuzziness began to fade I really began to think about the story. Maybe it's just my bitterness, but is it possible that Pride and Prejudice could be a load of crap to a lot of people? Basically, what I am wondering is if love that conquers all and ends up so wonderfully is possible and normal for people? Has Jane Austen inspired young people to believe in the power of love, or is she setting us up for what we believe is a dream relationship, but ends up being another downfall? It is such a wonderful idea to fall in love like Elizabeth and Darcy, and we are left with such optimism, but what we don't ever find out about is how did it end up. Do Elizabeth and Darcy end up having kids? Is Elizabeth swamped with all the work of taking care of the kids and is Darcy a deadbeat father and husband? Is Elizabeth a nagging wife who never lets go of anything and drives Darcy up the wall? I think I sound a little cynical, but I'm just being honest about what I was starting to think about after finishing the book. It seems so wonderful, but after hearing about so many screwy and corrupt relationships, young adults like myself end up asking ourselves questions like this. Hopefully love like this is possible, and hopefully it can last a long time rather then burning out a few years into the relationship. But hey, if you don't find a love like this that makes your knees tremble, you always have Jane Austen and Danielle Steele novels!

Pride and Prejudice: Week 7-Post AHa

Having finished Pride and Prejudice over the long weekend, I have decided what I feel is the greatest cultural difference of the novel. I believe that what is most astonishing is the transformation of the role and independence of women. While I have found many other societal differences, this one is in my opinion the clearest. While one must put aside our protagonist, Elizabeth Bennet, who is very beyond her era if you look at her mother sisters and the other women that surround her it is obvious they are very dependent on the men in their lives. Without a husband, there is no chance at wealth and a good life. While love is sacrificed such as in the case of Elizabeth's good friend Charlotte Lucas, Charlotte is a wonderful example of how dependent the women of the late 19th were on men. She agreed to marry Collins simply out of need for security and a comfortable life. Now today if you walk down the streets of Manhattan or London, you will find many single successful women, who no longer need men in their lives to be happy and successful. A woman can now be single, successful, and even have a child and be a single mother. In the time of Pride and Prejudice, this wouldn't even be thinkable to a woman such as Mrs. Bennet. Despite that women still have a long way to go before they get completely equal treatment, and despite having already talked about this topic, I truly feel that the change in the position and lives of women is the greatest cultural difference between by modern world and the world of Austen's Pride and Prejudice.

Pride and Prejudice: Week 5-Post B

I have written about Elizabeth, but the character that particularly struck my attention this week was Jane Bennet. It was her eternal optimism in this section of the text that made me really think. On page (133) Jane says how she hopes Charlotte and Mr. Collins are happy together and expects it to be this way. She also downplays Mr. Bingley's abandonment of her as having many complicated causes that make no one at fault. Jane really reminded be of a character modern culture as well. She reminds me a lot of the character Charlotte York in the T.V. show Sex and the City. She sees to be the slightly annoying yet uplifting optimist, who always believes in the power of love. I kept reading and thinking, "didn't Charlotte say that in an episode!" I was wondering with Jane as I often wondered with Charlotte York, are they just flutes without a clue, or am I just a bitter cynic who thinks they're just living through their own denial to make the pain of real life endurable. Both characters Jane and Charlotte York really bring up a lot of ideas about the differences in people in my opinion. Are Jane and Charlotte simply weak and willing to believe in anything, or are they truly just happy people that believe nobody is as bad as they seem? Should we all try a little more optimism, or is it the people who are more cynical and doubtful that end up getting hurt less? I am happy however because both these women are very inspiring and bring up a lot of valid points. Should we believe so much in the good of people in love, or should we protect ourselves and say screw 'em?

Pride and Prejudice: Week 5-Post A

A new cultural difference in Pride and Prejudice I have found is the utter dependence of women upon men in the novel. In the novel, the minute Elizabeth turns down Mr. Collin's proposal, her friend Charlotte Lucas jumps and is three days later engaged to him. (123) We go on to learn how if these girls do not find a suitable husband, when their father dies, they will be left with nothing. Not only does this go for the Bennet girls but many others as well including their close friend Charlotte. I found this shocking, because today in my eyes it seems a girl is pretty unlimited in her aspirations. While many unfair things still need to be overcome, we have a woman running neck to neck with two male candidates for President, and we have more girls attending college than boys. We have certainly gotten past girls not having a chance at happiness or any sort of financial success without a husband. While this dependency is still seen in such places as parts of India, in my culture, it is relatively non-existent. The importance of finding a husband is obvious after seeing the quick moves made my Charlotte in the novel. It is sad how in the culture found in Pride and Prejudice love often has nothing to do with the choices made when it comes to marriage. Charlotte obviously doesn't even know Mr. Collins, but the fact that she will always have a residence is enough for her. The amount of unhappy couples must have been astonished considering people in our culture can marry at will with people they know and love, yet still such a large percent end up in divorce. Despite all else though, the reasons for marriage and the way it is done is a very large cultural difference from that which is in the book to what I live.

Pride and Prejudice: Week 4-Post B

Despite the fact that I wrote about this quote for my cultural difference this week, I want to reflect on it in my  free response because it effected me more than any other part of the section of the novel I read this week. The quote is on (page 101.)," Elizabeth was the least dear to her of all her children; and though the man and the match were quite good enough for her, the worth of each was eclipsed by Mr. Bingley and Netherfield. " This effected me because I thought is was very painful to read and I felt very sad for Elizabeth. How would you feel if your mother acknowledged that you were her least favorite child? The most comforting thing my parents would say to my brother and I were younger as I said in my Post A, that they loved us both the exact same. I began then to think, is this not the reason that we have so many problems in our world? I am not contradicting my statement that modern parents today usually always say they love their kids the same, I am saying that is it the parents like Mrs. Bennet that exist today that are causing kids to shoot up schools and start wars? Is this not the seed of the problems that effect us all and sadden our daily lives? So many kids who have major issues and hurt others commonly have disfunctional family lives and feel unloved. I often put myself in the shoes of the protagonist and couldn't help feeling very hurt by these comments. What it must feel like to have your mother, your confidante say such a thing would be too much to bare. I am quite distasteful of Mrs. Bennet right now, and will continue to root on Elizabeth and her unstoppable wit and perseverance!

Pride and Prejudice: Week 4-Post A

Another interesting cultural difference that I have found in Pride and Prejudice is seen on (pg. 101) when it says," Of having another daughter married to Mr. Collins, she thought with equal certainty, and with considerable, though not equal, pleasure. Elizabeth was the least dear to her of all her children; and though the man and the match were quite good enough for her, the worth of each was eclipsed by Mr. Bingley and Netherfield." this passage refers to Mrs. Bennet and it shocked me when it said that Elizabeth was the least dear to her. This shocked me as it is so culturally different than from my society. Today, where I live and in my society's morale, children are never ranked regardless of who they are marrying or their level of success. Parents always tell their kids I love all of you with all my heart and could never love one of you more than another. Because of this comforting message that is so often present in modern American family life, it was both hurtful and disappointing to read. I felt so bad, how would Elizabeth feel about this? She is her mother's least favorite because the man she is presumed to marry at this point is not as wealthy as Jane's presumed fiancee. Does Mrs. Bennet favor her other children more because Elizabeth is a bright, witty, and free spirited young woman? I knew that this was the most prominent cultural difference this week as it made me stop reading and realizing how uncommon that is in our society today. I'm glad my parents have never said such a horrible thing.

Pride and Prejudice: Week 3-Post B

So far in Jane Austen's novel Pride and Prejudice, I am having a difficult time understanding the feelings of Elizabeth. While I understand at this point that she is very interested in Mr. Wickham, her relationship with Mr. Darcy, is very troubling. She speaks so horribly of him, yet when she is with him they have this certain connection that to the reader is undeniable. This is seen on page 89, "When those dances were over she returned to Charlotte Lucas, and was in conversation with her, when she found herself suddenly addressed by Mr. Darcy, who took her so much by surprise in his application for her hand, that, without knowing what she did, she accepted him." This is an obvious sign to me that she isn't completely sure he is all that disagreeable, by her immediate acquiescence and lack of any hinderance. I am however very confused with Mr. Darcy's feelings towards her as well. Is he really truly falling in love with her, or is he just testing her out for his own selfish reasons? I am also very unsure about who to trust at this point as well. Mr. Wickham says to Elizabeth how cruel Mr. Darcy has been to him by taking away what Mr. Darcy's father promised him simply because Mr. Darcy's father was so fond of him. Later then Jane, Elizabeth's sister, tells her that what she has heard about Mr. Darcy from Mr. Wickham is untrue, and Mr. Darcy is not as disagreeable as he seems. Mr. Wickham is by no means a respectable young man. There are many confusing issues in the novel right now that I am hoping will soon be resolved. I don' t know if I can take all the mixed feelings anymore!
