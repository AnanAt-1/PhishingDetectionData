Nickelodeon Studios

View source
  • History
  • Purge
  • Talk (0)

Nickelodeon Studios was a hybrid television production studio/theme park attraction. It was ran by television network Nickelodeon, was located in Universal Studios Florida, and was apart of Universal Orlando Resort. It operated from June 7, 1990 and closed April 30, 2005.

History

Chapter 1 - Development of Universal Studios Florida

In the late-1980s, the Music Corporation of America, better known simply as MCA, had officially announced they had begun to build a theme park in Orlando Florida using their film and television division Universal, as an eastern counterpart to Universal Studios Hollywood.

MCAs venture into the theme park business has been thrown around since the early 1970s, with a proposal to build a Florida replica to their Hollywood studios, however those plans fell through due a recession. MCA re-entered the ring in 1980, opting to build a park with unique-thrill seeking attractions based off of IP, rather than a production facilities. This also went sour as MCA didnt feel confident in the park's development and construction finances, and couldnt find another company to bid for them.

The park would focus on stand-alone attractions based off of Universal IP, these attractions would center on the core idea of teaching guests about the art of making movies. Many of these attractions such as Jaws, Kongfrontation, and Earthquake were taken straight out of the Universal Studios Tram Tour in Hollywood, and fleshing them out to create unique experiences.

However, MCA had decided to incorporate the plans for production studio facilities into the park, to directly compete with DisneyMGM Studios. Unlike Universal Studios Hollywood, these facilities were integrated into the park, which created a much more confined space for production operations and due to all of Universal's major projects staying in Hollywood, there was no guarantee on how often the studios would be used.

Furthermore, the park's focus on thrill-seeking and informative attractions meant there was very little for families (especially ones with little children) to do at the park.

MCA's solution was an up and coming cable channel for kids, Nickelodeon.

Nickelodeons productions had been all over the place during its humble years. Which, among other reasons, made it troublesome to produce original programs and pitch other projects. When Nick hit it big with Double Dare, they now had huge brand recognition and money and could now invest new frontiers. One of the first things they did was to find a proper home for further Nickelodeon productions.

Nickelodeon would approach MCA for a deal to lease their own studio while construction for  Universal Studios Florida was underway in the fall of 1988.

Both companies soon realized how they can benefit greatly from each other, Nickelodeon would benefit from having their own studio space not only to film their shows, but also a way to help Nick advise their own production with each other through Matrix Marketing, and MCA saw how Nick can help the park attract families, on-air contestants and tourists, and make sure that at least some of the parks studio space is being utilized consistently.

Nickelodeon also saw the appeal of Florida being a right-to-work state, meaning that no production or studio staff were required to hire through labor unions.

By November, Nickelodeon made a 13-year agreement with MCA, granting them a 90,000 sq ft production facility, which housed two soundstages, which are 16,500 sq ft. with administrative and production offices housed in-between, where they would produce television shows 10 hours a day, everyday, except on Christmas. Nickelodeon ran the studio, while Universal ran the walking tour. Part of the contract was to advise Universal Studios Florida at least 1000 times a year.

Nickelodeon Studios was publicly announced in February of 1989, with construction starting shortly thereafter.

The exterior was designed by veteran production designer Byron Taylor, inspired by the Memphis style of design. Interior design was by Don St. Mars, a newer hire who had come from the Kearns and Associate advertising agency.

Construction saw several hiccups, not only due the park being rushed to make its spring 1990 opening, but also because Nickelodeon and MCA had to make several alterations to fit Nicks current and future needs. Nickelodeon also had a lot of ideas to implement bizarre and wacky elements around their studio, however MCA rejected many of these because they wanted a generic, realistic studio.

There were so many tweaks made to Nick Studios original configuration that you could write a book about it. The stages and production support space was set early on using the same Butler Buildings as the other Universal stages. Each stage was supposed to hold two shows the size of the original Double Dare, which had started out in a 50 x 90 studio in Philly. This was a huge error since the most recent version of Double Dare was considerably bigger. The cyclorama was scaled to the original Double Dare even though there was about 30 to the overhead steel available. A state of the art automated lighting system was installed oriented for the two set configuration as well. This posed a problem for the rigging of all future sets.[1]

Byron Taylor, production designer


On April 17th, 1989, filming of Super Sloppy Double Dare moved to one of Universal Studios Floridas backlot sound stages, while Nick Studios was still under construction. Think Fast, Make the Grade, Total Panic, and Eureeka's Castle would also move to Florida in the following year.

Chapter 2 - 1990

On February 1, 1990, Nickelodeon held a media preview for Nickelodeon Studios Orlando covered by Florida today

Prior to the studio's completion, Double Dare executive producer Andy Bamberger, Double Dare creator Geoffrey Darby, Channel President Geraldine Laybourne, and Vice-President Scott Davis, wrote their names on the cement floor of the video production building's second story. Bamberger wrote "I Love TV Production" above his name.

Just before opening, Nickelodeon ran a promotion for The Nick express. A nine-car train that Amtrak let them completely modify into one large game room. Decorated inside and out in orange, neon green and characters. The train had video games, Nick and teen celebrities, and as a bonus, played Super Sloppy Double Dare physical challenges. But only one winner would be lucky enough to be chosen to have a free ride to officially open Nick studios.

Opening Day

Universal Studios Florida would open on June 7th, 1990. Starting at 5:00/4:00 central, Nickelodeon hosted a 3-hour opening day celebration.

Hosted by several Nickelodeon personalities, such as Marc Summers and Kelly Brown, presents the viewers at home what it is like to spend a day at Universal Studios Florida. Featuring live performances from Kid and Play, Perfect Gentlemen, and The Cover Girls. And special guest appearances from Steven Spielberg and Bill Cosby.

There was also a subplot featuring  Awful Corp, featuring 3 old folk who despise Nickelodeon and prevent the new studios from opening.

The winner of the Nick Express sweepstakes was revealed to be Alberta Griffith who became the guest of honor for the studio's grand opening.

As part of the opening of Nickelodeon Studios, came the unveiling of "Nickelodeon's Declaration of Kids' Rights", an important document, below, inspired by the USA's Bill of Rights that proclaims to the world that kids have rights, too!

Opening day would also introduce Nickelodeon Studios Updates. These were short skits that played during commercials in which park guests are asked questions depending on the topic. These interstitials lasted until the mid-90s.

Tickets for the walking tour and guests on Nickelodeon tapings were purchased from the guest-relations office, right at the front gate of Universal Studios Florida. A short walk from the parks main entrance would place you in the Production Central, featuring all of the park's sound stages. Turning left on Nickelodeon Way, and past the kids simulator ride, The Funtastic World of Hanna-Barbera, is Nickelodeon Studios.

To enter the studio, guests would queue along either of the studio's two sound stages. After a briefing from their tour guide on whats to come, guests would enter the building and see a glimpse of the end of tour as they rode up an escalator into the windows walkways, officially dubbed the fish bowl.

TV sets were hung above every room of the tour, which would play videos that further guided guests throughout the studios.

In front, would be a top view of the sound stage below, behind guests would be production offices. The tour guide would describe the show seen on the stage below and how its made.

After that, guests were led into the production control rooms, which gave guests a close up look at how video and audio is edited and directed. These production suites were surrounded by sound proof glass, and the kids were encouraged to scream, NICKELODEON to prove the effectiveness of the glass.

After visitors are taken through the other soundstage, they are brought to a hallway featuring rooms for last minute preparations before going on stage. Originally called Production Alley, it featured a costuming and  make-up departments, a contestant waiting room known as the Green Room, and Kitchen where foods, slime, and other concoctions were made. Kids were encouraged to have a taste of either slime or gak, and wear some studio props.

At the end of the tour, guests made their way back to Auditions space where kids can participate in simple games to possibly win an opportunity to be on a Nick show. On average, the tour lasted just under an hour.

Following the parks opening, Nickelodeon would begin to add various props around the studio as guests wait for the tour or upcoming show tapings. Initially these were props from Double Dare, but soon they began to add various photo opportunities based on popular Nick shows as well as make-shift stands that would sell Nickelodeon-theme merchandise and food and drinks. Nickelodeon themed benches were also scattered around the studio. A Nickelodeon walkway starting from Plaza of The Stars was painted to guide guests to Nick Studios, and along the walkway, several Nickelodeon billboards were placed.

The Green Slime Geyser, the studio's main icon, was absent when the park opened. It was unveiled on October 27, 1990. A live broadcast aired, interrupting an episode of You Can't Do That on Television, and featured special guest appearances Russell Johnson of Gilligan's Island fame, and Gerry Laybourne, president of Nickelodeon.

Designed by theater and exhibitor designer Tom Hennes of ThincDesign[2], the 17-foot high fountain would "erupt" green colored water out of its spout every 10 minutes. Hennes gave the geyser a wacky design, instead of a natural-looking geyser, he gave it more of a plumbing machine look which gave it a unique feel that was special to Nickelodeon.

There was a pigment company in Orlando that developed the actual material. And, we have 2 kinds of slime. Actually, there were 3 kinds of slime in the guys. One of them was the stuff that came out of the spout and that's just water.  And it had a pigment suspended in it that's noncock thick and has no binders, so it'll just wash out of your clothes. Then there was, what we call the slime blending manifold, which was the crazy thing that spat slime out over the door. And that actually had a place where somebody could come out from inside the building (slime geyser) and pull a lever and gather a bucket of slime. And that slime was oatmeal slime. That was sitting in a 50 gallon pressurized tank, inside the geyser and it would come out of there. And then the third kind of slime was an oil slime, which was inside of the so-called Hydro Bubbler/Gas Exchanger, which belched and made big bubbles. And that was actually mineral oil. So, 3 slimes, 1 geyser. And that was so it wouldn't evaporate.

But, it was enormously fun because it was just this dumb machine and the dumber we made it, the better it got. And it had these kinds of quirky qualities and then, you know, late in the game, I had to come up with a backstory for it because they liked it so much, they wanted to put some signs around it like museum signs that would show how it worked. So I had to invent all the names for the different things and try to figure out what they actually did. It was a machine that was designed before its functionality was known.

One of the things they did really wonderfully was kind of create this whole idea around GAK and slime that stuff took on a life of its own. And, you know, it really was, it was like this thing. And so adding the geyser just gave it another level of mythology that came out of the ground.

And, you know, you could only get it in Central Florida. And, it was fun to participate in that myth. And they carry you know, they thought it all the way through. They improvise along the way, but they really developed it as a brand and as a fun idea.[3]

Tom Hennes, theater and exhibitor designer


Following the Geysers inclusion, the entrance for the parks Studio Tram Tour attraction moved from the breeze way of sound stage 23 and 21, to sound stage 19 of Nickelodeon Studios. Because of this, the entrance for Nickelodeons walk through tour was permanently fixed to stage 18.

Chapter 3 - 1991

In January, Vice President of Nickelodeon Scott Davis, a appointed the General Studio Manager of Nickelodeon Studios

Nickelodeon soon realized that filming a show everyday wasn't reasonable. Nick Studios didnt have enough productions going on during its initial months to fulfill filming 10 hours a day, everyday. And the shows they did have, had very brief film cycles. This didnt sit well with MCA.

We contractually had to have something going on the stages every day when the park and tour were open. Sometimes there was legitimate production going on. But when there wasnt, there was the necessity of creating the illusion there was. They called it camera blocking. They had all kinds of names for it. They had PAs down there moving things around, looking busy.[4]

Byron Taylor, production designer


Their solution was to replace the end of tour Auditions room, with a mock-game show titled Game Lab. It was presented just as the name implies, where Nickelodeon tested ideas for games and challenges for their game shows. Adults and children sat separately, and they would be selected to play various physical games, akin to shows like Double Dare. The main event would be a kid, selected from the audience, sitting in a small-inflatable pool, as they get slimed.

Game Lab worked to remedy two issues at once, to fulfill their contract with MCA, and grant kids fantasy of being part of a Nick game show and being slimed, especially because Nickelodeon wasnt always filming game shows.

Despite game shows being the cornerstone of Nickelodeon at the time and Nickelodeon Studios being built around that fact, the 90s would begin the channel's slow shift from game shows, to scripted sitcoms and variety shows, being the focus of the channel as this year saw the premiere of Welcome Freshman, Fifteen, and Clarissa Explains It All.

The short lived Nick at Nite sitcom Hi Honey, Im Home became the first prime-time network series produced in Orlando.

However, the game's new shows were given a much higher production value. Get the Picture featured 9 large screens and computer circuit set, Nick Arcade (which would premiere the following year) with its heavy emphasis on video games, and What Would You Do which took full advantage of Nick Studios being located in a theme park.

Further improvements were made to the studio. A snack truck was placed out front to serve guests. The tour queue was updated with TV displays and a sign that indicated the wait time for guests to enter the studio. Inside, background music was added throughout, and new tour videos were introduced. Cut-outs of Nickelodeon's brand new original animated shows, called Nicktoons, were added, and Production Alley was changed to place more emphasis on the Gak Kitchen.

We are finding we have to constantly review the presentation of the tour Were concerned with how people move through the tour and how audiences work as it relates to the flow and what is going on at the park.[5]

Scott Davis, Nickelodeon Studios general manager

Chapter 4 - 1992

In December of 1991, a Stimpy walk-around character to help prompt merchandise that was being sold around the studio. And in the following year, more Meet and Greet characters (most of which were from Nicktoons) were introduced.

On April 30th, 1992, the Nickelodeon held a live broadcast, dedicated to the burial of their time capsule. Hosted by Mike O'Malley of Get the Picture and Joey Lawrence of Blossom, Over 30 items that were considered important by kids were placed inside, and once sealed and buried it wouldnt be opened until April 30th 2042. During the event, some last minute items were placed inside.

This includes Joeys Whoa hat

A jar of Nickelodeon GAK which was put in under the insistence of the park's walk-around Dr. Emmett Brown character

A tape of the event itself and the camcorder used to record it, which was used by special guest and Florida native, Vicky Horn.

Two notable shows added to the production line-up was Nickelodeon Guts, an "action-sports" game show that put three kids in three colored teams to compete in athletic challenges. The show was so ambitious that Nickelodeon had rented space in another Universal soundstage to tape the show.

And Roundhouse a musical-sketch-comedy and one of the first shows for Nicks teenager block, SNICK.

The SNICK couch, seen in the block's many adverts and on-air promotion was added a prop to the Nick Studios tour, as well as the Nick At Nite Museum, a collection of vintage televisions and merchandise from the 50s and 60s.

Game Lab saw some major changes, a large projector screen was added which played footage of the Game Lab set and signs of past and present Nickelodeon shows were added to the walls over the years.

It was around this time where the famous Ren and Stimpy sign which led to the bathroom was added right out-front the studio.

Chapter 5 - 1993

1993 saw some more changes made to the tour. New wall decorations were added inside the studios, including the famous Ren and Stimpy space murals in the hallways of the production offices. New photo opportunities were added outside, notably one featuring the Nicktoons, and the iconic Partridge Family bus, a tie-in with Nick at Nite,

A proper queue canopy was installed replacing the old tents. TV sets and ceiling fans hung from above, and several video game kiosks were added where guests could play Sega, Nintendo, and later PlayStation games.

There was now a greater emphasis on the Gak Kitchen, and was completely remodeled to have a stand-up area, and newly painted walls.

Buses taking guests from the parking lot of the park were giving a new Nickelodeon look.

From April 16th to 18th, Nickelodeon Studios hosted the Kids World Council. 56 kids from around the world brainstormed ideas about preserving the world, and creating a action- plan that would be presented to Washington. This was televised on April 22nd, 1993 as Plan It for the Planet.

For todays kids, no issue ranks as high as the environment in shaping the world in which they live. This program will give national recognition to the millions of kids actively involved in preserving the environment every day. Nickelodeon has always been the voice for kids and it is our wish to empower kids. The Kids World Council is the ultimate extension of this idea.[6]

Geraldine Laybourne, President of Nickelodeon

New shows added this year were Wienerville, a variety puppet show hosted by its creator, Marc Wiener. With Marc interacting with his puppets, mostly through pre-recorded TVs, and the end two guests would be Weiner-ized and entered into Playland where they played silly games. And Legends of the Hidden Temple, hosted by Kirk Fogg, featured a large temple set as teams of six compete to retrieve historical artifacts in each temple.

Roundhouse moved to CBS Studio Center in Los Angeles for the filming of its second season this year.  The show's unionized cast, crew and musicians were unhappy that working in Orlando deprived them of residuals, and constantly flew back and forth to Orlando.

Nickelodeon (based in Orland) and Rebel Entertainment were always at odds in terms of the content and the music of the show. So to have the show in L.A., we had distance between us and Nick. And it didnt help (in terms of survival) that Roundhouse was Nickelodeons most expensive show to produce. Also being in L.A., we had a chance for people in the industry (i.e. agents, managers, other network execs, and press) to come and see live taping of the show. In Orlando you dont get that opportunity.[7]

Mark David, performer

Chapter 6 - 1994

One of the earliest productions that year was for a new program. Sketch-variety show All That started filming on January 17th, 1994, and went on to become one of Nicks defining shows of the 1990s once it officially premiered a year later on SNICK.

On February 14, 1994, an episode of Weinerville (the 68th episode of the show "It's Two, Two, Two Shows in One" which is also the shows final episode) became the 1000th episode filmed and produced at the studio. News media and community guests gathered that afternoon to watch the episode being taped.

Since its official opening in June 1990, Nickelodeon spent $110 million on production. In an average week, between 280 and 330 people were employed, including writers, production managers, artists, designers and technical crew. This was advertised on the network the same day.

Its a milestone the kids cable network also can measure in 39,000 gallons of green slime, 83,000 gallons of gooey GAK and millions of dollars.[8]


In March, the Nickelodeon Studios Chief for A Day Sweepstakes was held, in which kids would either send a postcard or look for specially marked boxes of Kelloggs Fruit Loops. The winner received airfare, a hotel, $1000, and limo. 5-year-old Rachel Brin won the contest and ran Nickelodeon for an entire day on April 16th, 1994.

This was all about fulfilling our promise of ONLY KIDS WIN.we did lots of competitions and wanted to create something cool around the idea that kids were in charge and we decided to use the Studio as a backdrop.[9]

Niels Schuurmans, creative director of talent and promos


Other new productions this year include African-American led sitcom My Brother and Me, U to U, a show focused on displaying viewer-submitted work and ideas, and Nick Jr. puppet shows Allegras Window and Gullah Gullah Island.

Marc Weiner would host the 1994 Kids' Choice Awards segments that were filmed at Universal Studios Florida, however these were filmed away from Nickelodeon Studios.

Nickelodeon also began to film other MTV Network productions at their studio. Mostly concerts for VH1.

The Big Help, a community outreach program launched this year, and broadcast on the channel on October 2, 1994 as The Big Help-A-Thon. Part of this marathon was Nickelodeons All Star Challenge, a combination of GUTS, What Would You Do? Double Dare, Wild and Crazy Kids, and Legends of the Hidden Temple, which was taped at Nickelodeon Studios.

The channels annual Halloween event, Nick or Treat was turned into a game show, and filmed at Nick Studios.

Studio updates include; Nick Jr branding being added to the entrance in-front the tour entrance of stage 18, while its mascot face is seen on the TV displays. The buildings exterior received a slight modification in a new paint job. Most notably the green and white patterned structural beams were repainted solid green. A Countdown Clock was added to the geyser, so guests could tell when the geyser was going to erupt.

Chapter 7 - The Mid-90s & the start of the end

1995

Nickelodeon as a network and a company began to majorly reform during this era.

Nickelodeon and MTV Networks parent company Viacom successfully acquired Paramount Pictures in the summer of last year, and at the time, Paramount had owned and operated several amusement parks. Three Nickelodeon theme parks titled Nickelodeon Splat City would open in Great America in Santa Clara California, Kings Island in Mason Ohio, and Kings Dominion in Doswell Virginia. To promote these parks, the Green Slime Transfer Truck seen in the parks live-show, Mega Mess-a-Mania, was added to Nickelodeon Studios in Florida. The truck would mainly be used for Nick In the Afternoon starring Stick Stickly.

Major productions include Global Guts, a revamped version of Guts featuring competitors from around the world to see who is the ultimate child athlete. The Mystery Files of Shelby Woo a children's mystery and was one of the few single-camera productions there, and another Nick or Treat was held, which was the final one filmed in Florida.

1996

By 1996 Universal Studios Florida started seeing far less productions than before. Universal shuttered the Tram Tour the previous year, and the parks focus slowly began to shift from The Art of Making Movies to being a theme park based off of movies.

As for Nickelodeon, its productions started to become decentralized. Although shows being filmed and produced outside of the studios wasnt new, for example shows shot on location, this year saw more shows leaving Florida.

All That moved out of Nickelodeon Studios following its second season. Showrunners Brain Robbins and Mike Tallion requested the show to be moved to California for the same reason as Roundhouse. Season 3 would be filmed at Paramount Pictures lot.

Its spin-off, Kenan and Kel started filming at Nick Studios this year. A show about two friends getting into hijinks and misadventures, it featured two breakout stars from All That, Kenan Thompsons and Kel Mitchell. However this show also moved out of Florida between its second and third season.

This year also saw productions that theoretically should have been done at Nickelodeon Studios, but werent. These include Harriet the Spy, the very first Nickelodeon feature film done in collaboration with Paramount Pictures and kicking off Nickelodeon's Movie division, was filmed in Toronto Canada. And Blue's Clues, an interactive educational children's television show which became one of Nick Jr. 's most iconic shows, was filmed in Tribeca, Manhattan, New York with initially little involvement with Nickelodeons production team.

A bigger blow to the studios longevity was Nickelodeons original animated programming, the Nicktoons, because none of them were produced in Florida. Although immediately successful, they were a small part of the network when it first launched in 1991 and all of their productions were separate. But following John K. and his production company Spumco firing from The Ren and Stimpy Show, Nickelodeon opened Games Animation Studios in Studio City, Los Angeles to produce the rest of the series.

By 1996 they were producing every Nicktoon not produced by Klasky Csupo, and their show Rugrats, which had been on indefinite hiatus, had surpassed Ren and Stimpy as the channels biggest show when reruns were added to weekly lineup. Now Nicktoons were eating up priorities for Nick which used to be put on game shows, which by extension, priorities on Nickelodeon Studios Florida.

This transition was further heightened when Geraldine Laybourne stepped down after 12 years of being president of Nickelodeon in February, with executive vice-president, Herb Scannell, taking over her role. Less than a month later, Nickelodeon Studios general manager Scott Davis, exited in 1997 and was replaced by Tim Quealy, former vice president of operations.

For the first time, the studio didnt have anything new to keep up. The studio tour began to stagnate with barely any new changes, the only significant additions being a proper gift shop being built after years of tiny, roll-around merchandise stands and the removal of Partridge Family bus, which was replaced by a Nick At Nite taxi. The studios didnt film any game shows this year outside of pilot episodes, Gullah Gullah Island had gone into hiatus when its lead puppeteer Philip D. Garcia, who played Binyah Binyah, became a victim of a car accident which left him in critical condition, and sadly died on January 3rd, 1997. The Mystery Files of Shelby Woo was a closed set, meaning it featured no studio audience. So the only show anyone could see was Kenan and Kel. Because of this, employee numbers halved compared to what they were 4 years prior.

Chapter 8 - The late 90s and the True Decline

1997

Nickelodeon opened two new studios in California, the live-action studio Nickelodeon on Sunset in 1997 in an old stage facility at 6230 Sunset Boulevard, and became the new home for live action Nickelodeon productions. With All That and Kenan and Kel moving over immediately.

Shelby Woo kept going, much to the chagrin of the production crew.

The weather stinks. Every single day I was dealing with thunderstorms Its an inhospitable place to shoot film and TV shows. And theres a tremendous talent pool to work with there.[10]

Alan Goodman, show creator


In August of 1997, when production switched from filming video tape to film, allegedly they could no longer pay the agreed welfare and pension for the local chapter of the International Alliance of Theatrical Stage Employees. All 60 crew members went on strike, so show creator Alan Goodman decided to move out of Florida.

They were good crews, but if you cant take advantage of some savings to work there, why bother? I cant speak for anyone else, but if I were a producer thinking of bringing my show down there and shooting nonunion, I would have to think twice We definitely wont be back in Orlando."[11]

Alan Goodman, show creator


After the success of its pilot the previous year, Figure It Out was shot and premiered on the network in 1997. A contest was held early that year where kids would enter recordings or photos of their talents, and choice contestants won a free trip to Nick Studios.

A spin on the old What's My Line? game show in which Nickelodeon personalities try to guess the strange habits or accomplishments of kids. Unlike all previous shows, there were no in-park contests for this show, instead they were all mail-in auditions.

The studio laid off 9 employees around this time.

Less than a year after he became general manager, Tim Quealy stepped down and Nickelodeon Guts creator Scott Fishman took his place.

The Universal Studios park over in Hollywood received its first Nickelodeon attraction in Totally Nickelodeon. A theater-game show where two groups of guests competed in games based on Nickelodeon shows. This along with the already existing Splat City and upcoming attractions, began to put the old Nick Studios Florida tour to shame.

1998

To start off the new year, a 1998 New Years Eve Party was held at Nickelodeon Studios, hosted by Stick Stickly of Nick In the Afternoon and by All That cast members, Danny Tamberelli, Lori Beth Denberg, and Amanda Bynes.

In 1998 Nickelodeon Studios only had two shows running, Figure it Out and the often forgotten Youre On! young contestants trying to convince passersby to complete a series of predetermined tasks while unknowingly being filmed. However, these segments were filmed in unknown locations, outside of Universal Studios Florida. Not only meaning that only a small percentage of the show was filmed at Nick Studios, but the guests had no live performance to watch.

Also, Since Nickelodeon also specializes in animation, and there was no specific room/workplace for animation, so Nickelodeon built a brand new 72,000-square-foot animation studio built from the ground-up, located on 231 West Olive Avenue in Burbank, California.

The lack of productions in Florida not only meant bad news for the studios, but also made it very difficult to maintain their contract to film something new every day. The only thing they could do was continue to outsource projects to other MTV Networks, promotion material, on-off episodes specials, and more pilot episodes.

Many pilots were shot at Nickelodeon Studios over the years, although many of them are not publicly documented, this is around the time where more the infamous ones were made, such as I Dont Think So, Morph Sports, Nick Camp, Tooned In, Who Knew, and most notably Me and My Friends which would later be redeveloped as The Backyardigans.

In the meantime, what I had done as general manager was I met with my counterpart at the time who was the head of the Universal Production Group because they owned all of the other sound stages, and they technically owned the building structure that we were in. We owned all the equipment inside. We had all the TV stuff that we put in. Universal owned the physical structure, the 4 walls. As part of our deal, we could not go out and sell the use of our sound stage to 3rd party productions.

Universal, at the time, really didn't have any sound stages that had video equipment in it. They were basically 4 walls that were used for film. So I went to my counterpart at Universal, sat down, and I said, look, let's do this. I have video equipment. You have stages, but you have no video equipment.

Why don't you sell our sound stages to 3rd party companies, and we'll split the revenue? So we did. So between 1998, 99, when our studio stuff started to go way, way down, and 2,005, which was sort of like the finish line that we needed to get to, we basically housed 3rd party productions. We did stuff for Animal Planet. We had some Golf Channel stuff in there.

We did a lot of stuff for a lot of people. And, basically, I would split the revenue with Universal. So we were running Nickelodeon Studios at a net loss. In other words, it really wasn't costing New York anything to keep the studios running because the revenue we would make from third parties was offsetting the cost of the studio.[12]

Scott Fishman, April 28th 2014

Nickelodeon Studios general manager, 1997-2005

There were some more changes to Nick Studios around this time, the previous year saw access to walk around stage 19, permanently closed off. When CityWalk opened on top of the park's original parking lot, the new Hard Rock Cafe now featured a gate at Nick Studios, which led in and out of Universal Studios Florida. The Slime Transfer Truck and Nick At Nite taxi were removed as well as the video game kiosks. In there place, Rugrats statues were added to the guest queue. A new photo opportunity for Figure it Out was added. Slime was painted on top of sound stages 18 and 19, and billboards were added across stage 18 which advertised current Nickelodeon productions and programming blocks. These would be updated over the years.

1999

March 1st, 1999 saw the launch of Nickelodeon Games and Sports For Kids, or GAS for short. A re-run channel for all of Nicks game shows from the past, with all original content for the channel being shot at Nickelodeon Studios.

1999 would see the last major updates made to the studio tour, advertisements for Nick Gas were implemented at production control, as well as murals placed in the production offices. Murals of The Wild Thornberrys were added around the escalator at the very start of the tour.

Notably, a portion of 1999 Kids Choice Awards event was filmed this year in Florida featuring performances by 3rd Storee, Britney Spears, TLC, and NSYNC.

Chapter 10 - The 2000s

2000

On January 5th, 2000 production began on Double Dare 2000, a brand new Double Dare show, taking some elements of all of the previous incarnations, albeit without Marc Summers as host. The show premiered 3 weeks later.

January 24, 2000 saw the return of Slime Time Live, a reboot of a short lived game show from the early 90s. Just like its predecessor, it was a live weekday interstitial show that led in and out of shows and commercials breaks. Hosted by Dave Aizer outside of the studios, unless there was bad weather, guests gathered up, played games, met celebrity guests, and got slimed or pied, and won prizes. The show retained the phone-in element from the original so kids from across America can play along. The show would have many specials throughout its history, such as a late night SNICK special, Nick Take Over Your School special, and most notably a special celebrating Nickelodeon Studios 10th anniversary, with Marc Summers making a guest appearance, and footage of the studios opening day celebration being played. This reboot proved far more successful than the original.

That summer saw production start two new sitcoms, Taina starring female lead actress Christina Vidal. And Noah Knows Best, a show about teens growing up in New York.

SNICK Underground concerts were filmed featuring A-Teens and Baha Men, and more pilots such as Slime Survivor and Roller Jam were produced this year.

2001

Despite the effort made by Nick and the staff at the Florida studio, their efforts to revive the studio didnt last. Taina moved to Nickelodeon on Sunset, Double Dare 2000 only lasted one season, and Noah Knows Best only aired 8 episodes for 3 months before being pulled. 5 episodes never aired in the United States. Slime Time Live was now the only Nickelodeon production left at Nickelodeon Studios.

On May 29th, Nickelodeon Studios auctioned off 2,000 items including props, set dressing and production equipment. Over 500 people showed up.

Were going to resell it on eBay as stuff from Nick shows, said Tiffany Smith, 14, of Fern Park, who was pushing a shopping card with boxes of china from Gullah Gullah Island and two brown fur coats.[13]

Nick Empties Toy Chest, Nancy Imperiale Wellons

During the summer of 2001, as Nickelodeon Studios rearranged work structure, and far fewer employees, especially full-time employees, were working there, the walking through studio tour was significantly shortened. All access to the upper floors leading to the sound stages and production control rooms were blocked off, and now guests were immediately led to the Gak Kitchen and then Game Lab.

2003

On April 11, 2003, Jimmy Neutrons Nicktoon Blast opened as a replacement for The Funtastic World of Hanna-Barbera. The refurbished simulator ride saw guests aboard an experimental rocket built by Jimmy himself as you fly through different Nicktoon worlds. After the ride, you can take a photo with a meet and greet character. Due to Nickelodeon taking over the old Hanna Barbera gift shop in the ride, one the gift shop built for Nick Studios years ago was shuttered.

GameFarm, one of the few original Nick Gas shows, was taped at Nickelodeon Studios this year where contestants would play online games against one another and after two rounds, whoever received the most points would move on to face The Gaminator in a final round.

2004 & 2005

Slime Time Live filmed and aired its final episode on July 2nd 2004. It was replaced by Nickelodeon Splat, which ran from March 7th, to August 17th. It featured teams divided into three colors (green, yellow and red), who compete in humorous tasks to earn prizes. The winning team is slimed at the end of the game. A webpage created for the game allowed viewers to interact with the program while it was airing, similar to Slime Time Live. It is the very last production to be filmed and produced at Nickelodeon Studios Florida.

From September of 2004, until the studios then underestimated closing, a crew of roughly 10 people were left to run the Game Lab and Gak Kitchen while the rest of the building and sound stages were left abandoned.

After Florida was hit by Hurricane Charley, the studio suffered some minor damage, notably the sign atop the studio. This, for some reason, prompted Nickelodeon to give the studio a completely new paint job after nearly 15 years. The iconic blue paint scheme and matching Memphis accents disappeared, replaced by a comparatively sober lime-green look, done to match the current look of Nickelodeons branding. This also included the removal of the slime atop of the soundstages, as Universal gave all of the sound stages new paint jobs. The removal of the signs across stage 18 advertising Nick shows and blocks, and the removal of all of the photo opportunities.

The Slime Geyser was also given a new paint job, but at this point it was completely shut off, permanently.

In 2005, when the deal was gonna end in December of 05, I went to New York at in the early part of the year, and I met with Seema Zargami, who's the president of Nickelodeon, and Albie Hecht.

And I said, you know, do you guys wanna keep it open for another year or 2? After that, I mean, you would really have to do a capital investment because you'd wanna upgrade your equipment, which I know Viacom didn't wanna do. Right? They didn't wanna invest in a place that was ultimately gonna close down. We could have probably gotten another year or 2 out of the equipment.

Do you wanna keep it open or do you wanna close it? And they said, okay. Let's close it. So, again, I made a deal with Universal that instead of waiting till December, we would vacate as of July 1st, and we would walk away. And we would sell them the equipment inside.

Because if I wasn't gonna sell the equipment, it would have taken us probably about 6 months and a lot of money, talking 100 of 1,000 of dollars, to get rid of all of that equipment between wholesalers and auctioneers that auctions that would have taken place, you know, getting rid of all the cabling that was done in the building, getting rid of all of that gear was at least a 6 month exit strategy. By selling the equipment back to Universal, MTV Networks basically got to walk away free and clear. They got some money for the equipment that we had, and Universal, to this day, owns, operates, and maintains that sound stage and the equipment that's in it.[12]

Scott Fishman, April 28th 2014

Nickelodeon Studios general manager, 1997-2005

Nickelodeon Studios officially closed down permanently on April 30th, 2005. Exactly 13 years after the time capsule was buried. The studios managed to film just over 2,000 episodes of shows, specials, pilots, and other live events.

Chapter 11 - Aftermath

Between the closure and summer 2006, much of the studio's exterior presence was removed. The iconic Nickelodeon sign, all the benches, the Ren and Stimpy bathroom sign which stayed there even years after the show was canceled and left the main Nickelodeon channel, and the iconic Slime Geyser.

The Nickelodeon time capsule was removed in August 2006 and reburied at the Nickelodeon Suites Resort. Upon the hotel's closure in 2016, it was reburied at the Nickelodeon Animation Studio, still set to open in 2042. The time capsule's original cover was sold by Heritage Auctions in 2022 for $19,200.

On November 9, 2006, Universal announced that Soundstage 18 would be redesigned to become a venue for the Blue Man Group. The new theater  opened on June 1, 2007, and was sponsored by Sharp Aquos until 2012. Their residency ended on February 1, 2021, leaving Soundstage 18 vacant once again.

Several break-ins of Nickelodeon Studios occurred over the years, showing all of the props and set pieces left behind, the most infamous of which is when adamthewoo entered the building for a second time in 2012. Showing never-before-seen murals innaccable to park guests on the tour.

Nickelodeon returned to film My Family's Got Guts in 2008 a reboot of the original with a Family Double Dare spin on it, using some of the former Nickelodeon Studios facilities including the green room and second-floor production suites. Training was in Soundstage 19, and the actual production was filmed nearby on Soundstages 23 and 24. It would be the last time Nickelodeon filmed a show at the park, and Florida in general.

Jimmy Neutrons Nicktoon Blast closed on August 19th, 2011, to be replaced by Despicable Me Minion Mayman, which is still in operation today.

Universal would eventually take over building 17, and over the years, by the 2010s leftovers from inside Nickelodeon Studios were removed. Especially in 2015 when a huge remodeling took place, removing all of the internal murals, signage and old equipment.

The only known remnant of Nickelodeon Studios left in the building, is the green slime emanating from the stalls in the public bathroom.

What Happened?

Nickelodeon Studios was built around a very different era of Nickelodeon. An era defined by game shows, and their agreement with MCA intended for easy to make shows that defined Nickelodeon at the time in the late 1980s. But when major changes were made in 1991, such as the introduction of scripted shows such as sitcoms and varieties, these shows needed writers, producers, actors, and other Hollywood talent, they were left in the dark by the state of Florida who didnt provide any help for producers in the area. And when Nickelodeon allowed Roundhouse to leave, it created a domino effect that allowed anyone to leave whenever. The increased emphasis on animation especially didnt help, once shows like Rugrats and SpongeBob became huge hits, Nickelodeon went into animation overdrive to compete with Disney and mainly Cartoon Network with their original cartoons series.

You're not gonna get Steven Spielberg and Francis Ford Coppola and, you know, Martin Scorsese to say, well, yeah, I'm just gonna pick up for the next 6 months, and I'm gonna move to Orlando and shoot this film when all the talent and casting is out in California, and the studios they know are in California, and the crews that they know are in California, and that they can live at home. I think Florida would have been better off with the soundstages it built if it would have started smaller by trying to attract some other TV property. Go get a soap opera. Get a sitcom. Get a game show.

At the same time, you had states starting to figure out that they gave tax incentives to these production companies that would come and shoot in their states that would make it economically more attractive to production companies looking to do stuff on location. Texas was out in front of us. Louisiana was out in front of us. Carolina was out in front of us. You know, Florida finally at the governmental level in Tallahassee finally said, oh, okay.

I think Florida bit off more than it could chew in 1989 to 1990 when they said, oh, we're gonna be Hollywood East because of a film.

They shot Parenthood here with Ron Howard. Right. Right. That was in 1988, 1989. Mhmm.

That was Universal's claim to fame for the longest time. We shot Parenthood here. That was one movie. One guy came, shot one movie, and then everybody went back home again. Mhmm.

There was never the understanding or the desire that maybe if we walk before we run, maybe if we build the talent base both in front of and behind the camera with smaller projects, let's go get a brand new soap opera that's looking to start up and give them the economic benefit that they wanna get that maybe they will work here and shoot here 8 or 9 or 10 months out of the year. Okay? And then we can start to build the base. So when Martin Scorsese is gonna look around ultimately and say, where am I gonna do my next film? Well, wow.

Great. Florida is less expensive, but look at all the stuff that those crews have done. Maybe they've actually got some talented people there. Maybe I should check out that state. They never did that.

They went right to the finish line and said, hey. We're gonna start making movies. But they never did anything to attract or build the talent base here, so it just stayed in California. It wasn't about the talent and the crews that were Nickelodeon Studios. I tell you, if you ever got the opportunity to talk to a Brian Robbins or any of the other people that worked for a long time in Nick Studios, they will tell you.

Scott Fishman, April 28th 2014

Nickelodeon Studios general manager, 1997-2005

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vpBGW_pl6Nk

But after all of these years, Nickelodeon Studios remains as one of the most sorely missed attractions at Universal Studios Florida, because Nickelodeon Studios not only entertained millions of kids, but informed them about the production of their favorite shows, was the center of community outreach with the Kids World Council and The Big Help, and got many kids slimed.
