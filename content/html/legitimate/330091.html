EARTHLINGS: the review

Posted in Gaby with tags documentary, Earthlings, entertainment, film, Moby, movies, Nation Earth, sentience, vegan on July 11, 2011 by Gabriele

Three primary life forces exist on this planet: nature, animals, and humankind. We are the Earthlings.
Make the connection.
Earthlings”

– Joaquin Phoenix (in “Earthlings”)

These words, in all their simplicity, speak volumes. Earthlings is a documentary, directed by Shaun Monson, that exploreshumanity’srelationship with other animals, highlighting our dependence on these animals and our mistreatment of them. The film focusses on the 5 ways in which we depend on animals, namely: as pets, as food, as clothing, as entertainment, and for research purposes.With stunning narration from Joaquin Phoenix and a soundtrack by Moby, Earthlings is not only possibly the most important documentary produced, but is also beautifully put together.

Earthlings is not easy to watch… not by any stretch of the imagination. And that is precisely why it is a film that everyone should watch. As humans we have the privilege of being able to make choices as to how we conduct ourselves during our time on Earth, and it is important that we make these choices with full understanding of the implications. Those who choose not to educate themselves regarding how their actions effect non-human animals are not less responsible for the effects and that is why Earthlings is an essential film for anyone to watch.

So many of us have grown up believing that as humans we have the right to exploit animals for whatever purpose and have never been exposed to the alternative; that we are all Earthlings and all deserve to live long and full lives that are free of suffering.

Whatever your views are, SEE THIS FILM. Do not turn away from the truth.

—

If you would like to see Earthlings visit their website where it can be viewed for free, or contact the ARA to find out about our upcoming screenings or organise your own.

SECRET SOCIETY OF VEGANS… The interview

Posted in Gaby, Interviews, Member Blogs with tags business, cake, clothing, England, hardcore, interview, London, Secret Society of Vegans, shopping, SSOV, vegan on March 13, 2011 by Gabriele

G: Firstly, you run the Vx store in Kings Cross? How did you start the store and what made you decide open it?

R: Yes, I’m the crazy person who, by accident, started a t-shirt company, then got a stall at the Camden Market, then opened a shop called Vx in Kings Cross. Why opening a shop? Because, I couldn’t find any places in the UK who would sell the SSOV gear. As simple as that.

G: Couldyou briefly explain what SSOV is?

R: The Secret Society Of Vegans (SSOV) is a brand. We produce t-shirts, tops, hats, bags etc… It was started in 2003 to raise funds for the Pogo Caf (London), still a project at that time. I kept producing t-shirts as a benefit for Pogo until I left the co-op in 2005. After leaving the SSOV designs to Pogo and seeing that they didn’t like it much, I started to start again the SSOV (circa 2006). The name (SSOV) is a joke, but there’s a lot of people who take us seriously.

G: Do you get a lot of new people coming by the store or have you mainly regulars?

R:London gets a lot of visitors/tourists. We are grateful to them. We also have regular locals who shop here everyday.

G: What’s it like to run a vegan business in London?

R: You meet people from all over the world travelling through London and visiting us. I am always interested in knowing in what’s happening in their home town or country.

G: What’s your favourite part of running Vx?

R: Probably meeting people. I do talk a lot. Too much sometimes!

G: And least favourite?

R: the volunteers rota.

G: Why did you decide to become a vegan?

R: I became vegan in 2001, I was a vegetarian before that since about 1991. Moving to London and having a vegan girlfriend, and having lots of vegan friends… And it’s England, it’s so easy to be vegan here! Everything is labelled at the supermarket. There are a lot of vegan options at restaurants.

G: Favourite vegan food?

R: Vegusto Cheese* from Switzerland (http://www.vegusto.com/), it’s smelly & strong like the real thing. Amazing! And I love the French pastries stuff we sell at Vx (Pains au Chocolat, Mille Feuilles) and the lovely sandwiches too (vegan Mozarella pesto tomato baguettes).

G: How do you feel about the stereotype of all vegans being hippies who wear grass skirts and eat only sprouts and nut roast?

R: That’s what I have been fighting against for years. The SSOV started as a reaction to the uncool image of vegans. I have a sign on the window that says “Vegan Junk Food”. It’s not really “junk food” but it’s a way of getting a reaction from people. We sell yummy comfort food. Vegan food = fun!

G: What do you see for the future of Vx/ SSOV/ veganism in London/ the world (I know, such a broad question- sorry!)

R: I have got a couple of projects but I can’t tell you now. We are trying here to build a community. To establish Vx as the place to be and we encourage people to get involved. We have already helped a bunch of people to go vegan. Just because we are here and they see us as a positive model. I’m really happy about that!

G:Any words of wisdom for aspiring vegans/ vegan shop owners/ entrepreneurs?

R: Keep it simple, start slowly. Keep it punkrock, in the DIY sense. Coming from the HC scene, I learnt a lot about doing stuff on my own terms and with a limited budget. And another tip, don’t start a business if you have got thin skin: some people are really nasty and they will let you know how much you suck.
—

*Vegusto cheese is sometimes available from the Checkers at Canal Walk in Cape Town.

—
For more on SSOV checkout their facebook page, twitter, and website.

Beach Cleanup

Posted in Campaigns with tags beach, environmentalism, marine life, recycling, Sea Shepherd Conservation Society on January 17, 2011 by Gabriele

The weather is perfect. You look perfect. Want to go to the beach with me? Well I’d say yes too with moves like that.
The ARA is holding a beach clean-up at Milnerton Beach on the 5th of February.


The objective is to cleanse the beach of garbage and other such nonsense. The point is to make the beach a better place for everyone. Everyone means the people who visit the beach, the pets that visit the beach, and the animals that live on the beach and in the sea. If you didn’t know already, garbage poses a major threat to marine life as it is not biodegradable and is often swallowed by the animals or simply infringing on their habitat, making it difficult for them to live. In worst casescenariosmarine life can die due to garbage such as plastic strangling them or blocking theirdigestivesystems . For more info check out this page that has a really simple break down of some of the bad stuff that rubbish does to sea creatures.

So if you’re keen, which you should be, to lend a helping hand to all the animals out there and also to the Cape Town community, come along to Milnerton Beach on the 5th. We’ll provide you with thenecessaryrubbish collecting gear and some yummy vegan cookies will also be present (rumour has it).

Almost Raw Vegan Cheesecake @_@

Posted in Vegan Recipes with tags cheesecake, recipe, vegan on January 12, 2011 by Gabriele

Cheesecake. The key part of that word is cheese. So it seems to defy all logic that a vegan cheesecake could be possible. Well logic was always the poor cousin to vegan innovation. What a surprise

Ingredients:

2 cups any combination of macadamia nuts, pecan nuts, and almonds
1 cup dates

3 cups cashews (soaked in a bowl of water for at leasst 1 hour)
3/4 cup lemon juice (fresh or bottled)
3/4 cup agave syrup OR maple syrup
3/4 cup coconut oil (liquid)
1/2 cup water
tsp vanilla essence
1/2 tsp salt

Optional:
Mixed frozen or fresh berries
Sugar

Method:

The cashews soaking away

1. Soak the cashew nuts in a bowl of water for at least 1 hour.
2. While the cashews are soaking, grind the 2 cups nut mix, and dates in a food processor.
3. Once the dates and nuts are a coarse meal press the mixture into the bottom of a 8-9 inch spring form pan.
4. Drain the water from the cashew nuts, and blend the now tender cashews with the lemon juice, salt, syrup/agave nectar, coconut oil, and water.
5. Blend the mixture until it reaches a very smooth consistency.

 

'Cheese mixture' and 'biscuit' base

6. Pour the mixture over the prepared base in the spring form pan
7. Place the cake in the freezer for 3 hours prior to serving so that it can set. After this the cake can be kept in the refrigerator.

Nom nom nom

8. To make a berry sauce simmer the berries with sugar until they are soft and a sticky jam like sauce has formed. Wait for this to cool before pouring over the set cake.

Finished cake! ^_^

Youth Apathy Versus Youth Energy

Posted in Gaby, Member Blogs with tags activism, Equal Education, Josh Budlender, South Africa, youth on October 21, 2010 by Gabriele

This really needs no introduction by me. Anything I say will just be an echo of the attached speech, delivered and written by a good friend and general upstanding citizen Josh Budlender. All I can add is that you really should read it. No matter who you are or where you are.

—

Apathy of the youth. This phrase has become the slogan of our times, the supposed central characteristic of our generation. But it carries with it a whole lot of demeaning and disempowering assumptions. It carries with it ideas of what we cant do and what we arent, rather than what we can do, and what we are. And, in its being defined, it becomes a mere topic we moan about, and yet do nothing to fix.

But Im not speaking today about the apathy of the youth. Im speaking about the emotion and concern of young people. Im speaking about youth protest, youth fire, youth zeal, and youth power.

About two months ago, I went to Khayelitsha with some friends to spend some time with the leadership committee of Equal Education. The students who make up the committee vary in age from around 16 to about 19. We went around speaking to people in the streets, handing out flyers, getting people to sign petitions, and generally educating and mobilising the public around issues of education. About a month ago, these students refused food for 24 hours in order to further their agenda of proper education.

This is one example of youth protest. And this example is useful, because it allows us to judge areas where activism is muted, without seeming unrealistic in our expectations of what youth protest can and should be.

The greatest cause of uninterestedness when it comes to political and social matters is, put simply, teenagerhood. Where we are now, we have the capacity and ability to act as adults. But we have the responsibilities of children. To me, childhood must be starting to end by, say around, 16. By 16, its time to start taking responsibility for your actions. Its time to start looking at the world and its time to start saying: what is my role as a member of society?

There is undeniably a phase of our lives where we cannot physically take on the responsibilities of adults. But how long does adolescence last? For how long can our ignorance and non-interest in the world around us be justified? For how long is it ok to concern ourselves only with childish self-indulgent behaviour, at the expense of interest in and interaction with the world around us? Until what age is it ok to ignore our societal commitment so that we can play computer games, watch blockbusters, obsess about music and over-invest in rugby or football or cricket or hockey? How long can we be children?

Did you know, that until about the 1950s, there was no such thing as a teenager? Children went straight into adulthood. And they were adults by the time they were 18. Teenagerhood was pretty much invented by business. Think about all of the companies and products today that are targeted at teenagers. From Playstations to Billabong hoodies to Smirnoff spins, perhaps the majority of consumer goods would be made redundant without this artificial, perpetually growing demographic. We are the superficial cash cows of corporate giants.

200 years ago, throughout the world, people younger than 19 were heads of families. Today, a few kilometres away, people our age are raising families. The situation is of course not ideal, but when a 13 year old has to mother her little sister, and succeeds, despite all the odds being stacked against her, how does that reflect on us? 16, 17, 18 year olds who are pre-occupied with the imaginary world of computer games? Or with the world of drunken parties and no responsibilities? Or with the world of T-Pains meaningless autotune crooning?

How is it then, that the teenagers of Equal Education continue to protest and act? I think the difference lies in that though the EE members are teens, they do not occupy themselves solely with music and sport and fashion. They also take an interest in their society. They have achieved a balance between the light-hearted side of being young, and the meaningful, powerful side of being a youth in South Africa, with all of the potential we hold. They have rejected teenagerhood, and accepted youthhood.

Its not that its bad to listen to music, or to party or to be wild. But shouldnt there also be some meaning? Should there be more in our lives than just repeated lyrics and cool dress? To me, a period that shows this well is America in the late 60s and 70s. This was an iconic period of youth protest and political awakening. But it was also the time of free love and amazing music and hallucinogenic drugs and hippies. There was a balance between fun and meaning, between frivolity and earnestness. And it was a period that will always be remembered as the time of the youth.

So lets change the way we see ourselves. Lets stop ignoring the problems of our society, just so that we can focus better on our efforts at being cool. Lets reject the role corporate giants have decided for us. Lets not accept apathy among ourselves. Lets be more like the leaders of Equal Education. Lets take our place as young roleplayers in todays society. Lets take our place as the fiery, indomitable, youth of today! Its time to reject non-action, and to embrace activism. We define who we are. Not corporate giants, not scientists, and not the pessimists who resign us to apathy. If we choose, we can become that iconic era of youth power. Let us wake up, and seize our moment! Masivuke!

To fur or not to fur

Posted in Thoko with tags beauty, fashion, fur on October 10, 2010 by Thoko Miya

Something that I have never understood as a teenage girl: that has an active interest in fashion and beauty, is how it is possible that people can condone the ill-treatment of animals for their own personal satisfaction, to fulfill their own vanity. How do people justify the exploitation of animals in the beauty industry?

 

I would expect that someone who wears fur understands the beauty of it and also fully comprehends that it once belonged to another living creature. With this point in mind, it eludes me that people can be as egotistical as to strip another being of its identity and of its life for their own satisfaction. Does the fact that an innocent animal has been tortured and in most cases brutally bludgeoned to death not distress them?

 

Many people argue that they wear fur because it is beautiful. I fully agree with this, fur is beautiful when admired on the back of an animal in the wild, a cat on a wall but not when it is hanging around a supermodels neck on the runway, or on a rich tycoon in Russia. Most animals have as much pride in their appearance as many humans have. Animals use their fur to keep warm, as an aid in the mating process and many other very relevant reasons. So then how is it that people excuse the murder of millions of animals for frivolities such as looking good?

 

It is would not be accepted in society to kill someone because they had nice hair, or clear skin. It would not be acceptable to strip someone because you liked what they were wearing. So then why then is it accepted to wear fur? How is it possible that someone can pardon or overlook the fact that an innocent and animal has been stripped of their own beautiful fur/skin so that you can wear it? Has this point been completely disregarded?

 

It is necessary that people realize that the suffering of millions of animals is infused into our everyday lives and that the most effective way of dealing with animal rights is o make appropriate lifestyle choices. This may seem trivial but, as clich as it may be combined, everybodys efforts can make a difference. People need to reconsider their moral and ethical values. Going to the zoo should not be a form of entertainment and it should not be the in thing to wear rabbit fur hats or fox skin jackets.

Animated Animal Movies

Posted in Lauren, Member Blogs with tags animation, anthropomorphism, Disney, film, Pixar on October 3, 2010 by lauren216

For those who have forgotten what happens at the end of Finding Nemo, a whole lot of fish get caught in a net, and there are an intense few minutes where you think maybe Nemo or Dory or the rest of the nameless fish are gonna die. Im almost 100% sure that every member of the audience roots for the fish to get out of the net and for Nemo to be reunited with his dad. But I dont think the drama of a big bad fishing boat ending the lives of a few hundred fish puts many people off eating these animals (i.e. being the reason why fish are caught). At the time when Finding Nemo came out, I wasnt a vegetarian yet, and I certainly ate fish for a long time after seeing the movie, regardless of my few minutes of solidarity with the animated sea creatures.

To me, this is very revealing of our skewed view of animals. Over the past few years, animated animal movies have been churned out endlessly Bee Movie, Over the Hedge, Bolt, Shark Tale and Valiant, for example. In so many of these movies, there is a situation in which the main character / animal is in trouble, and where the audience is supposed to feel sympathetic towards them. Sometimes the animals problems are caused by human characters, which might even make the audience feel guilty. If any of the movies didnt end happily, wed probably leave the cinema feeling a bit depressed. So, if we care so much about the well-being of animals in movies, and acknowledge that humans sometimes cause their problems, why dont we reflect this compassion in out everyday lives?

Of course, the animated creatures are not exactly realistic. They talk and act like humans, which could be the reason why we want them to live happily every after we feel they are more accessible because they act like us, and therefore deserve to live well. Perhaps the characters similarity to humans makes it difficult to apply these rights to animals in reality real-life animals arent always as perfectly cute, they dont make jokes and they have habits we dont always understand or tolerate.

It is this, our inability to connect the dots, that is so illogical to me. We claim to love animals and support them (although theyre fictitious), then go shopping and support the meat trade. Or we use animals for entertainment, or use their fur for clothing. Im not playing the blame game it can be difficult to change our mindsets about animals after growing up in an environment where millions accept that they are beneath humans. The bottom line is: I just encourage everyone to respect animals, especially the next time they see one of these movies. Consider why you support the characters (no matter how unrealistic or childish, and no matter how subconsciously you support them) if it is because youre rooting for animals and want them to have quality lives, try to translate that feeling into compassion for animals in general.
