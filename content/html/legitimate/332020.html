Court of Appeal dismisses EEA residence card appeal

Posted on March 10, 2025 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

Tipu v Secretary of State for the Home Department [2025] EWCA Civ 215 (05 March 2025) 

Bean, Andrews and Falk LJJ held in this appeal that the Upper Tribunal had been right to reject the residence claim of Mr Tipu, a Bangladeshi national, based on being a dependent extended family member of his cousin, an EU citizen, where there had been a three-year gap when his cousin was not meeting his essential needs. He was helping with the cost of college courses in that period, but the tribunal had been right to hold on the facts that those were not a material contribution to his living needs. The facts were that Mr Tanveer Hasan Tipu, the Bangladeshi appellant appealed against the Upper Tribunal’s decision upholding the SSHD’s refusal of his application for an EEA residence card made as an extended family member of his cousin Mr Salim Ahmed who had sent money from Portugal, where he was working and became a citizen, to support Mr Tipu while he was living in Bangladesh, including paying for school fees and other educational costs. Mr Tipu completed his secondary education and began university in Bangladesh, then came to the UK as a student in 2010. His evidence was that for his first year in the UK, Mr Ahmed still sent him money, about £250 every two to three months, for his education and other expenses such as travel, books, clothing and some food. He stayed with another relative who provided accommodation and some food. Mr Tipu’s evidence was that Mr Ahmed also paid for his studies at four colleges until 2014. 

After Mr Ahmed moved to the UK in 2011, Mr Tipu lived with him, and Mr Ahmed covered all of his living expenses. In 2016 Mr Tipu’s student visa expired and he then became an overstayer. The Upper Tribunal found that Mr Tipu had been dependent on Mr Ahmed per the definition in Jia v Migrationsverket [2007] QB 545, namely that Mr Ahmed had provided material support for his essential living needs, while he had attended school in Bangladesh until 2008, and from 2011 when he was a member of Mr Ahmed’s household in the UK. That was common ground, as well as the fact that an extended family member could move from being a dependant of a sponsor to being a member of the sponsor’s household or vice versa without losing his status as an extended family member. But the Upper Tribunal found that there was a three-year gap between 2008 and 2011 when he was not dependent on Mr Ahmed for his essential living needs, and thus his application failed. Mr Tipu submitted that the Upper Tribunal had erred in law in holding that first he had not been dependent on Mr Ahmed from 2008 to 2011 and, in particular, proceeding on the basis that funding for education during that period was not a material contribution to his essential living needs; and second, he did not qualify as an extended family member because of the gap in support between 2008 and 2011. Mr Ahmed was a dual Bangladeshi and Portuguese citizen.

Supreme Court: Withdrawal of deprivation of citizenship order has retrospective effect

Posted on March 10, 2025 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

N3 v Secretary of State for the Home Department [2025] UKSC 6 (26 February 2025)

In this case, the Supreme Court held that if a deprivation order is withdrawn, it is to be treated as having no effect for the purpose of determining an individual’s citizenship status in the period from the date of the making of the order until it is withdrawn. The Court unanimously allowed ZA’s appeal in full, and N3’s appeal in part. They were both deprived of their British citizenship in 2017 on the basis that they had participated in Islamic terrorist organisations and that they thus posed a threat to the UK’s national security. The key issue before the Supreme Court was whether the SSHD’s withdrawal of an order depriving a person of citizenship because she accepts that the order has made the person stateless means that the original order is of no effect and the person affected has always retained their British citizenship? N3 was born in Bangladesh and is a British Citizen. E3 is a British citizen who was born in the UK and both of E3’s parents were Bangladeshi citizens at the time of his birth. In 2017, the SSHD made orders depriving N3 and E3 of their British citizenship on grounds that they had participated in Islamic terrorist organisations and that they posed a threat to national security. The SSHD considered both N3 and E3 to be dual British-Bangladeshi nationals and that depriving them of British citizenship would not make them stateless. On 10 June 2019, E3’s daughter, ZA, was born in Bangladesh. If E3 had been a British citizen at the time of her birth, ZA would also have had British citizenship by descent.

Both E3 and N3 appealed to the Special Immigration Appeals Commission (“SIAC”) against the decisions to make the deprivation orders on a number of grounds including that, at the date of the decisions, they no longer held Bangladeshi citizenship, and the order would render them stateless. E3 and N3 argued that, under Bangladeshi law, they had lost their Bangladeshi citizenship at the age of 21. On 18 March 2021, SIAC handed down its judgment in separate cases concerning other alleged dual British-Bangladeshi nationals in a similar situation to E3 and N3. SIAC held that these individuals had lost their Bangladeshi citizenship at age 21 and depriving them of their British citizenship would therefore render them stateless. The SSHD corresponded with E3 and N3 stating that the deprivation orders had been withdrawn and that their British citizenship had been reinstated. Solicitors acting for E3 and N3 replied to the SSHD disagreeing that their citizenship needed to be “reinstated”; instead, the solicitors argued that the deprivation orders had always been unlawful and never existed, with the result that E3’s and N3’s citizenship had always remained intact. This would also mean that ZA would have had British citizenship at birth through E3. The SSHD replied refusing to accept that E3 and N3 had been British citizens during the period in which the deprivation orders were in force and had not been withdrawn.

Court of Appeal laments remitting human rights case back to FTT for a third time 

Posted on March 10, 2025 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

AA (Morocco) v Secretary of State for the Home Department [2025] EWCA Civ 144 (18 February 2025)

In considering whether the Upper Tribunal had erred in law in its decision to remit to the First Tier Tribunal a Moroccan national’s appeal against the SSHD’s refusal of his claim for asylum and humanitarian protection and his human rights claim under articles 3 and 8 of the ECHR (read together with paragraph 276ADE(1)(vi) of the Immigration Rules), the Court of Appeal held that it would uphold the order of the UT to remit the whole of the case to a different constitution of the FTT with no findings preserved, but for the reasons expressed by the court and not those given by the UT. The Court of Appeal had to decide whether the UT had erred in law in its decision to remit to the FTT. The appellant “AA” was born in Italy in 1999. He moved to Morocco with his family when he was aged seven years and he had Moroccan nationality. In 2015, he received threats because he posted an image on social media which was critical of the Moroccan monarchy and Government. So he left Morocco and arrived in the UK clandestinely in 2016 and he was aged 16 at that time. In 2019, he made a claim for asylum and humanitarian protection on the basis that he had a well-founded fear of persecution in Morocco because of his political opinion and/or as a member of a particular social group, namely persons living with mental ill-health. Alternatively, he also claimed that his removal to Morocco would breach his human rights and/or he would not be able to reintegrate by reason of his mental health issues. 

AA’s claims were refused. In November 2020, the FTT dismissed his appeal on asylum and humanitarian protection grounds. However, it allowed his appeal on human rights grounds under paragraph 276ADE(1)(vi) of the the Immigration Rules. Aggrieved, both parties appealed to the UT. In May 2021, UT Judge Stephen Smith set aside the FTT decision without preserving any findings of fact, and the appeals were remitted in their entirety to the FTT for redetermination by a different judge. Subsequently, in June 2022, FTT Judge Davey dismissed the asylum/humanitarian protection appeal and allowed the human rights appeal under articles 3 and 8 of the ECHR, based on the appellant’s serious mental health problems and the risk of societal discrimination and/or adverse treatment by the Moroccan state. The SSHD appealed against Judge Davey’s decision allowing the human rights appeal, arguing that the errors identified by UT Judge Smith when he had allowed the SSHD’s previous appeal from the FTT had not been duly addressed in the redetermination, nor had the principles in AM (Zimbabwe) v SSHD [2020] UKSC 17 been applied. AA also sought to raise two grounds of appeal against Judge Davey’s dismissal of his asylum/humanitarian protection appeal. Later in October 2023 the SSHD conceded AA’s appeal, but he resisted the SSHD’s appeal. On 4 December 2023, the UT allowed the appeals of both the appellant and the SSHD and remitted the matter to the FTT to be redetermined again, with no findings of fact preserved.

High Court interprets section 4L of the BNA 1981

Posted on March 10, 2025 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

R (APD) v Secretary of State for the Home Department [2025] EWHC 246 (Admin) (07 February 2025)

The High Court (Aidan Eardley KC) dismissed these proceedings but held that the words “would have become” in section 4L(1) of the British Nationality Act 1981 simply directed the decision-maker to consider what the position in the past would have been had the historic wrong or other relevant matter not occurred, and did not carry any implication that that position could not occur again in the future. The words “able to become a British citizen” in turn referred to the point at which a person satisfied the criteria for registration or naturalisation under some other provision of the 1981 Act. Thus, a person “would have been able to become, a British citizen” for the purposes of section 4L if, at some point before they made their section 4L application, they would have satisfied the criteria for registration but for a reason falling within section 4L(1). On that approach, section 4L was not limited to the situation of a person who had been definitively excluded from citizenship, in such a way that the matter could never be reopened even if a person’s circumstances changed. It followed that the SSHD had misdirected herself in her decision. However, that misdirection did not require that the decision be quashed since the SSHD had, in the alternative, also addressed the “but for” question by reference to the correct position by which the ability to apply again in the future was no obstacle to registration via section 4L. The claimant “APD” and her husband were both Pakistani nationals.

The husband, who lived and worked in the UK, applied under the Immigration (European Economic Area) Regulations 2016 for permanent residence as a family member of a European Economic Area national, namely his son from a previous marriage who was a British citizen. The SSHD refused that application on the basis that, applying case law of the Court of Justice of the European Union on the right of free movement, the husband did not qualify for a derivative right of residence under the 2016 Regulations. The husband challenged that decision and, following a further judgment of the European court which made it clear that the 2016 Regulations were incompatible with European Union law, he was finally granted permanent residence. He subsequently applied for and was granted naturalisation under section 6(1) of the British Nationality Act 1981. Thereafter, the claimant was granted leave to re-enter the UK as the spouse of a British citizen. She subsequently applied to be registered as a British citizen, pursuant to section 4L of the 1981 Act, as someone who “would have been, or would have been able to become” a British citizen but for, inter alia, historical legislative unfairness or an act or omission of a public authority.

High Court upholds damages of £98,757 for unlawful detention

Posted on March 10, 2025 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

Secretary of State for the Home Department v Almas [2025] EWHC 212 (KB) 04 February 2025

In another substantial damages award involving unlawful detention, Mr Justice Ritchie dismissed the SSHD’s appeal and held that Recorder McNeill’s decision on unlawful detention due to breaches of policy were logical and supported by the evidence. The policy breaches were not trivial or minor. The SSHD’s appeal was in relation to Recorder McNeill’s decision that Nadra Tabasam Almas, a Pakistani who had entered the UK on a student visa which had expired and went on to become an asylum seeker, was entitled to damages of £98,757 for unlawful detention and breaches of Home Office detention policy in circumstances where there had been no consideration of alternatives to detention and no adequate explanation or assessment of her risk of absconding. Moreover, her rights under article 8 of the ECHR had also  been breached because of a disproportionate delay in determining her asylum claim, during which she was subject to reporting conditions and was thus restricted from travelling, working or claiming state benefits. Almas was born in Pakistan and had entered the UK on a student visa which had expired and a notice of temporary admission had been issued, subject to many restrictive conditions preventing her from working or running a business and requiring her not to travel, to live at one address and to report monthly. She had made an asylum claim, which had been refused in March 2015. In May 2015 she submitted a fresh claim. It had not been determined by 2017, and she sent further submissions to the Home Office, which kept the claim on file rather than determining or returning it. 

In March 2018, Almas rejected an offer of voluntary removal by the SSHD. When the respondent attended the reporting centre on 9 April 2018, she was handcuffed, detained and informed that she was going to be flown back to Pakistan. When the Home Office became aware of Almas’s further submissions which had not yet been considered, she was released on 23 April 2018. She remained subject to the restrictive conditions. In February 2021, she was informed that she would be granted refugee status. No appeal was raised as to the findings of fact of the Recorder. The decisions in Henderson v Foxworth [2014] UKSC 41, per Lord Reed at [67]; Grizzly Business v Stena Drilling [2017] EWCA Civ 94, per Longmore LJ at [39-40] and Deutsche Bank AG v Sebastian Holdings [2023] EWCA Civ 191, by Lord Justice Males at [48]-[55], clarify that any challenges to findings of fact in the court below have to pass a high threshold test. The trial judge had the benefit of hearing and seeing the witnesses which the appellate court did not. The appellant needs to show the appeal judge was wrong in the sense that there was no sufficient evidence upon which the decision could have been reached or that no reasonable judge could ever have reached that decision, or that the judge took into account an irrelevant matter or failed to take into account a material and relevant matter. Ritchie J first considered lawfulness of detention and then addressed damages, limitation and breaches of article 8.

Court of Appeal: Tribunal correct to say refusal must advise on right of appeal

Posted on March 10, 2025 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

R (Chowdhury) v The First-tier Tribunal (Immigration And Asylum Chamber) & Anor [2024] EWCA Civ 1380 (08 November 2024)

The Court of Appeal held in this case that the Upper Tribunal had not erred in finding that a decision notice given by the SSHD in respect of a refusal to issue a person with a residence card as an extended family member of an European Uinion national did not comply with the requirements of regulation 5(3) of the Immigration (Notices) Regulations 2003 as the notice did not advise the person that he had a right of appeal. The court found that the 14-day period within which an appeal must be brought (“not later than 14 days after they are sent the notice of decision”) does not begin to run until a notice of decision is sent which complies with regulation 5(3)(a) by informing the person that he has a right of appeal. A person who knows, or learns, that he has a right of appeal may choose to lodge a notice of appeal, thereby, in effect, waiving any non-compliance on the part of the SSHD. The SSHD appealed against the UT’s decision (Judge Blundell, JR-2023-LON-000104) that the respondent Bangladeshi national—Mr Chowdhury—had a right to appeal against a refusal to issue him with a residence card. Mr Chowdhury entered the UK as a student in 2008. He had been granted leave to remain until 2016 and had appealed against the SSHD’s refusal to issue him with a residence card as an extended family member of an EU national under the Immigration (European Economic Area) Regulations 2006 which were then in force.

Notice of that decision, dated 30 November 2016, was sent to Mr Chowdhury. The notice did not comply with the regulation 5(3)(a) of the Immigration (Notices) Regulations 2003 as it said that he had no right of appeal. In Sala v SSHD [2016] UKUT 411, the UT earlier held that there was no right of appeal against such a refusal. However, in November 2017, in Khan v SSHD [2017] EWCA Civ 1755 that decision was reversed. In November 2022, Mr Chowdhury appealed against the decision that he had no right of appeal. The FTT held that his appeal was out of time as, applying rule 19(2) of the Tribunal Procedure (First-tier Tribunal) (Immigration and Asylum Chamber) Rules 2014, it had been lodged more than 14 days after the decision notice was sent to him. However, Judge Blundell subsequently allowed Mr Chowdhury’s judicial review of the FTT decision. The UT held that the time limit for bringing an appeal had not begun as he had not been sent a notice in compliance with the 2003 Regulations. Aggrieved by the decision, the SSHD submitted that the time for lodging an appeal began from the date when a notice of decision was sent, even if the notice did not comply with the regulation 5 requirements. The Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal and first addressed the 2003 Regulations, the time within which an appeal had to be brought, and then addressed the instant case and the SSHD’s decision (which did not substantially comply with the requirements of regulation 5(3)(a) as it did not advise as to a right of appeal).

Supreme Court: ZH (Tanzania) concession on scope of section 55 is not good law

Posted on March 10, 2025 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

CAO v Secretary of State for the Home Department (Northern Ireland) [2024] UKSC 32 (23 October 2024)

The Supreme Court held in this case that the FTT had committed no error of law in its decision. It applied article 8 properly, as it was required to do, and had regard to the best interests of the respondent Nigerian national’s daughter as a primary consideration. Lord Sales and Dame Siobhan Keegan gave the judgment, with which Lady Rose, Lord Lloyd-Jones and Lord Hamblen agreed. The Supreme Court considered the interaction of article 8 of the ECHR and section 55 of the Borders, Citizenship and Immigration Act 2009 and their effect on decision-making by the SSHD and the FTT. The SSHD appealed against a decision of the Court of Appeal (Northern Ireland, McCloskey J, CAO v SSHD [2023] NICA 14) remitting an immigration appeal to the FTT on the basis of her non-compliance with the statutory guidance laid down by section 55(3) of the 2009 Act, i.e. Every Child Matters: Change for Children: Statutory guidance to the UK Border Agency on making arrangements to safeguard and promote the welfare of children (November 2009). The respondent “CAO” was a Nigerian citizen who entered the UK with her son and daughter, then aged 16 and 12. She claimed asylum, on the basis she had been subjected to violence by her husband and that, if returned to Nigeria, she was at risk of further violence and her daughter was at risk of being subjected to female genital mutilation (“FGM”). The SSHD refused her asylum claim, finding that her claims lacked plausibility. 

Section 55(3) of the Act obliges any person exercising immigration functions, including the SSHD, to have regard to the guidance on the making of arrangements to safeguard and promote the welfare of children. In her decision letter, the SSHD stated that she had taken into account the need to safeguard and promote the welfare of CAO’s children, but she did not expressly refer to section 55(3). CAO’s appeals to the FTT and UT were both dismissed and the section 55(3) issue was not argued before the FTT, which substantially endorsed the SSHD’s conclusion. The UT upheld the first decision. The Court of Appeal found that the SSHD decision-maker’s failure to expressly reference section 55(3) led to the inference that she had breached that duty, giving rise to an unlawful interference with the daughter’s rights pursuant to article 8 which had not been remedied by the FTT or the UT. McCloskey J thus set aside the UT’s decision and remitted the matter to the FTT for reconsideration. Lord Sales and Dame Siobhan Keegan allowed the SSHD’s appeal and held that there was substantive compliance with the guidance and therefore there was no breach of the section 55(3) duty by the SSHD when she issued the decision letter. The SSHD was not required by the guidance to interview the respondent’s daughter and the FTT properly directed itself and sought to apply article 8, treating the best interests of the child as a primary consideration.

Court of Appeal: Bouchereau is irrelevant to test of ‘imperative grounds of public security’

Posted on March 10, 2025 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

Secretary of State for the Home Department v George [2024] EWCA Civ 1192 (14 October 2024)

The Court of Appeal held in this deportation case that although the historic case of R v Bouchereau (Pierre Roger) (30/77) [1978] QB 732 had provided that certain conduct could in itself threaten public policy so as to require the perpetrator’s removal from a Member State, that principle was irrelevant to a case of deportation of an EEA national on “imperative grounds of public security” within the meaning of the Immigration (European Economic Area) Regulations 2016, regulation 27(4) which applied to Mr George who had lived in the United Kingdom for a continuous period of at least 10 years before the date of the relevant decision (an EEA decision taken on the grounds of public policy, public security or public health). There was no suggestion of any discrepancy between the Regulations and the law of the European Union, so it was unnecessary to refer directly to the underlying Directive (2004/38/EC). A Belgian citizen, Mr George was born on 27 March 1996 and he arrived in the UK with his family in 2004, when he was eight. The Home Office accepted that he had lived in the UK since 2004. In 2017, when he was 21, Mr George was convicted of manslaughter for playing his part in a horrifying gang murder. He was sentenced to 12 years’ imprisonment and on 8 November 2018, the Home Office decided to deport him on “imperative grounds of public security”. The Regulations transposed the Directive and the SSHD’s decision noted that the risk of reoffending was low, but stated that that was not decisive. 

The decision said there was a risk of serious harm to the public from any similar instance of offending. The First-tier Tribunal allowed Mr George’s appeal. In the Upper Tribunal, the Home Office had permission to argue, relying on Bouchereau that gang violence was conduct that in itself threatened public policy so as to require the perpetrator’s removal. However, the Home Office abandoned that ground before the UT. Although the UT found that the FTT’s decision had been flawed, it re-made the decision in Mr George’s favour, and determined that there was not a serious threat that he would again become involved in violence. The Home Office argued that the case fell within the Bouchereau principle. But Elisabeth Laing LJ, with whom Underhill and Asplin LJJ, dismissed the appeal. The short issue was whether, as the SSHD submitted in the Court of Appeal, the UT erred in law by not considering that issue. Reliance was placed in R v SSHD ex p Robinson [1997] EWCA Civ 3090. The court dismissed the appeal. Mr Abdul Hafidah (“AH”) was the victim and Mr George, who was on his bike confronted AH. The sentencing judge was sure that Mr George acted as a scout, “and in doing so he was performing a valuable service”. He did not have a knife, and AH did, and he held his bike in front of him to protect himself and had enabled AH’s chasers to catch up with AH. He did not take part in the final attack. He had been acquitted of murder. The sentencing judge recorded that he had written a letter “expressing evidently sincere regret; he even conceded that he was guilty of the offence for which he was convicted, which is the start of rehabilitation”.

Home Office ordered to pay over £203,995.24 in damages to Nigerian man

Posted on September 30, 2024 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

Adegboyega v Secretary of State for the Home Department [2024] EWHC 2365 (KB) (15 September 2024)

In Mr Adegboyega’s case, the SSHD was ordered to pay over £203,995.24 in damages. A Nigerian national, Mr Adegboyega was unlawfully detained at Brook House Immigration Removal Centre for 88 days in 2017 despite having the right to remain in the UK as the spouse of an EEA national. The damages included compensation for the arbitrary and outrageous exercise of executive power, breaches of article 3 of the ECHR due to the poor conditions at the centre, and the post-traumatic stress disorder the claimant suffered as a result of his detention. A Panorama undercover investigation later raised concerns about the conditions at Brook House and the treatment of detainees and an inquiry report published in 2023 confirmed that there had been systemic failings. HHJ Richard Roberts considered various claims for damages against the SSHD following Mr Adegboyega’s unlawful detention at Brook House IRC. Mr Adegboyega sought damages for (i) his unlawful detention at Brook House for 88 days between 28 April 2017 and 24 July 2017, (ii) trespass to the person involving an incident in his cell on 5 June 2017, (iii) violation of his rights under article 3  during the period of unlawful detention, (iv) psychiatric injury suffered as a result of his unlawful detention, (v) breach of his rights under Directive 2004/38/EC and the  Immigration (European Economic Area) Regulations 2006, and (vi) violation of his rights under article 8 of the ECHR. 

HHJ Richard Roberts assessed damages and addressed unlawful detention (basic award, aggravated damages, exemplary damages and trespass to the person) breach of article 3, Psychiatric injury, damages for cognitive behavioural therapy and breaches of the 2006 Regulations. As to staffing and culture, the report findings in relation to contracted service provider staff behaviour and culture were shocking and unacceptable. As to the weight to be attached to Brook House Inquiry report it was hard to see how it could be contended that the court should not place weight on the findings made by the Brook House Inquiry. Yet, when considering specific incidents, such as Mr Adegboyega’s claim for trespass to the person on 5 June 2017, and the question of whether he had suffered psychiatric injury, the court had to reach its own findings based upon the evidence. The court stated that the damages claim for psychiatric injury was decided solely on the lay and expert evidence it heard in this case. It was ironic that the SSHD argued that the case could have proceeded without the Brook House Inquiry report when the Defendant obtained the adjournment of the trial because the findings of the Inquiry were directly relevant to Mr Adegboyega’s claim. His counsel Mr Zainul Jafferji submitted that the court should place weight upon the findings in the Brook House Inquiry report because they had been made following an exhaustive four-year inquiry. The submission was accepted by the court.

Family life is unitary in nature: KF (Syria) overturned by Tribunal 

Posted on August 26, 2024 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

Al Hassan & Ors (Article 8: entry clearance: KF (Syria)) [2024] UKUT 234 (IAC) (04 July 2024)

The Upper Tribunal has held that the jurisdiction of the European Convention on Human Rights is primarily territorial, but as observed in SSHD v Abbas [2017] EWCA Civ 1393, family life is unitary in nature with the consequence that the interference with the family life of one is an interference with the rights of all those within the ambit of the family whose rights are engaged. Upper Tribunal Judge Rintoul also held that properly interpreted, KF and others (entry clearance, relatives of refugees) Syria [2019] UKUT 413 is not authority for the proposition that it is only a UK based sponsor whose rights are engaged. The Upper Tribunal said that while the rights of the person or persons in the UK may well be a starting point, and that there must be an intensive fact-sensitive exercise to decide whether there would be disproportionate interference, it is not correct law to provide focus exclusively on the sponsor’s rights and to do so risks a failure properly to focus on the family unit as a whole and the rights of all of those concerned, contrary to Abbas. The 10 appellants appealed decisions made on 11 August 2021 to refuse their applications for entry clearance as the family members of Ms Faten Al-Helwani, a Syrian national recognised as a refugee in the UK who was their sponsor. First-tier Tribunal Judge Komorowski dismissed their appeals. However, a panel consisting of Upper Tribunal Judge Macleman and Upper Tribunal Judge Rintoul set aside Judge Komorowski’s decision and Judge Rintoul remade the decision on 30 April 2024. 

Judge Komorowski proceeded on the basis that sufficiently close ties exist between each of the appellants and the sponsor, her husband and children such that their exclusion was a significant interference in terms of article 8 of the ECHR. He concluded, however, that the interference was not disproportionate and Judge Komorowski identified three issues relevant to proportionality: first of all the benefit there would be to the sponsor’s and her husband’s mental health if the family were admitted; second, the risk of refoulement to Jordan and the discrimination they face there; and, third the separation of the family had been occasioned by the Syrian civil war. The judge found that the appellants did not meet the requirements of the immigration rules. After having considered the medical evidence, it was not intuitively obvious, nor was there clinical opinion that admitting the “in-laws” would make a difference, in contrast with KF and others (entry clearance, relatives of refugees) Syria [2019] UKUT 413and the appellants were at a real risk of serious harm including a risk to their lives, given the serious risk of refoulement and serious level of discrimination in Syria and that their best interests lay in being admitted; but this had only a limited and indirect bearing on the seriousness of the interference with the family life represented by refusal of entry clearance, “these matters having more of an effect on the qualify of the individual lives rather than the quality of their lives with each other as a family”. He said that the appellants’ private lives fell outside the scope of article 8 and their plight had only a limited bearing on the private and family lives of the family in the UK.

Transitioning to eVisas on 31 December 2024

Posted on August 26, 2024 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

By the end of 2024, physical visa documents in the form of a Biometric Residence Permit (BRP) or a visa/sticker/stamp in a passport will be obsolete and millions of people in the UK will need to transition to eVisas. Physical visa documents will be made replaced by an online eVisa recording the holder’s visa status linked to the holder’s passport. Rather than having any kind of physical document, an eVisa holder will have access to an online account where their immigration status can be viewed and shared. Everyone with a BRP can apply for a UKVI account for free and people need to register for a UKVI account and provide their details including the BRP number. The UKVI account must be applied for before 31 December 2024. The transition to eVisas is just one facet of a wider Home Office strategy for a wholly digital immigration system. Other elements in the digital immigration system conceived by the Home Office include strategies such as the use of eGates and the abolishment of paper immigration applications and their replacement with online applications. It is apparent that the Home Office Digital, Data and Technology (DDaT) Strategy is central to the governments transformation efforts. It sets the direction for how the Home Office will deliver better services and organise its technology and data estate more efficiently.

Digital delivery was at the heart of the EU Settlement Scheme and other areas of the digital system are reflected in the implementation of a points-based immigration system, the creation of an advanced data analytics and risking system, and the rollout of Digital Services at the Border, which the Home Office thinks will make the UK border crossing more secure. The end of BRPs and Biometric Residence Cards will come on 31 December 2024 which is the cutover date to the new system. The Home Office has 3,800 staff in Home Office DDaT. Every year, their systems support over 3 million visa applications, check on 100 million border crossings, 5 million passport applications and 140 million police checks on people, vehicles and property. Their motto is that “We must now become digital by design in everything that we do and change the way we operate. This means embracing automation and investing in our cyber capabilities while also becoming more efficient, user-centric and adaptable to changing conditions.” The six principles for change are to converge technologies where possible, to create shared technology products, to be product-centric over programme-centric, to become data-driven to improve decisions, deliver effectively at scale, and to embrace innovation. An eVisa is an online record of a person’s immigration status and the conditions of the type of permission a person has to enter or stay in the UK. A person with access to an eVisa can prove their immigration status online instead of using a BRP— for e.g. an eVisa can be used an to prove a person’s immigration status to a landlord or employer.

Unanimously dismissing the Home Secretary’s appeal, the Supreme Court held in the case of “QX” that the right to a fair hearing guaranteed by article 6(1) of the ECHR is applicable to the imposition review, to impose the temporary exclusion order (“TEO”) and to maintain it in force. Resultantly, the Home Secretary must provide QX with article 6(1) compliant disclosure of the evidence relied on in support of the Syria allegation in both the imposition review and the obligations review. The appeal arose out of QX’s application for review of the Home Secretary’s decisions relating to the imposition of a TEO under the Counter- Terrorism and Security Act 2015. The purpose of a TEO is to protect the public in the UK from the risk of terrorism posed by the person who is subject to the order. QX lived in Syria between 2014 and 2018. He is a British citizen. On 26 November 2018, the Home Secretary applied to the High Court for permission to impose a TEO on QX, alleging that that he had travelled to Syria and was, or had been, aligned with an al-Qaeda aligned group (“the Syria allegation”). He returned to the UK on 9 January 2019, in accordance with the terms of a permit issued by the Home Secretary.

On his return, QX was served with the TEO and notice of the related obligations imposed on him by the Home Secretary. A dispute then arose between the parties as to whether QX is entitled, by reason of the right to a fair hearing guaranteed by article 6(1) of the European Convention on Human Rights (“ECHR”) and implemented in domestic law by the Human Rights Act 1998, to disclosure of the evidence relied on by the government in support of the Syria allegation. That was the issue the Supreme Court was asked to decide in this appeal.

Court of Appeal allows SSHD’s appeal on showing dependency

Posted on August 12, 2024 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

Secretary of State for the Home Department v Rexhaj [2024] EWCA Civ 784 (11 July 2024)

The Court of Appeal has held that an Albanian national’s prior grant of leave to enter the UK pursuant to entry clearance under Appendix EU (Family Permit) (“Appendix EUFP”) did not exempt her from demonstrating dependency when subsequently applying for leave to remain under the Immigration Rules, Appendix EU (“Appendix EU”). The court held that dependency was only assumed where an applicant had previously been granted leave to enter or remain before 1 July 2021 under Appendix EU, not Appendix EUFP. Underhill, Popplewell and Lewis LJJ allowed the SSHD’s appeal noting that it was agreed that the phrase “Under this Appendix” meant “under Appendix EU”. The SSHD appealed against the Upper Tribunal’s decision in Rexhaj (dependent parents, assumed dependency) Albania [2023] UKUT 161 (IAC) that the respondent Albanian national, Have Rexhaj, should have been granted leave to remain as a dependent parent under Appendix EU of the Immigration Rules. Appendix EU sets out the requirements for leave to remain in the UK. An applicant had to show dependency on their relevant sponsor, but dependency was assumed where the date of the application was before 1 July 2021 and the applicant had previously been granted leave to enter or remain “under this Appendix” as a dependent parent. Appendix EUFP sets out the basis on which a person would be granted entry clearance. The conditions of eligibility were that the applicant was a family member of an EEA citizen. There was no need to show dependency on the EEA citizen where the date of the application was before 1 July 2021. 

On 21 June 2021, the respondent applied for entry clearance under Appendix EUFP as the dependent parent of her son and his Romanian wife, each of whom held indefinite leave to remain under the EU Settlement Scheme, “EUSS”. Since she had applied before 1 July 2021 there was no requirement to demonstrate dependency when making that application. The application was granted in November 2021. In December 2021 she arrived in the UK. In January 2022, she then applied from within the UK for leave to remain as a dependent parent under Appendix EU. The application was refused on the basis that she had failed to show dependency, which the Home Office believed was a requirement of the EUSS. The key issue was whether Mrs Have Rexhaj’s eligibility for leave to remain was subject to a dependency requirement. The UT allowed her appeal, finding that as she had previously been granted leave to enter “under this Appendix” (i.e. Appendix EU, and not Appendix EUFP), there was no requirement to show dependency. However, disgruntled, the SSHD submitted that Appendix EU specifically incorporated a requirement that the respondent be dependent on her sponsor. On the other hand, Have Rexhaj, the respondent, submitted that the exception under Appendix EU applied to her as she had previously been granted leave to enter “under this Appendix” as a dependent parent, namely by her grant of leave pursuant to entry clearance.

Granting the application for judicial review. Sarah Clarke KC concluded that the SCA’s decision was irrational in that it had failed to take into account all relevant considerations and to follow and rationally apply the SSHD’s own guidance in terms of all the relevant factors to be considered. The decision was therefore unlawful and so it was quashed and remitted for reconsideration. The SCA’s decision had listed the NRM referral and welfare form, SM’s witness statement and asylum and interview record and SM’s solicitor’s email as being information that had been considered.

Court of Appeal: Albanian siblings of EU nationals wrongly refused status under EUSS

Posted on August 6, 2024 by Asad Ali Khan, BA, MSc, MA, LL.B (Hons), LL.M

Vasa v The Secretary of State for the Home Department [2024] EWCA Civ 777 (10 July 2024)

The Court of Appeal held in this case that the SSHD had erred in refusing to grant pre-settled or settled status under the EU Settlement Scheme (EUSS) to non-EU nationals who were siblings of EU nationals who had exercised free movement rights in the UK before its withdrawal from the EU. The non-EU siblings had come to the UK border prior to the UK’s withdrawal from the EU and immigration officers had given their passports stamps saying that they were admitted to the UK under the Immigration (European Economic Area) Regulations 2016. While those stamps were not relevant documents for extended family members under the EUSS, the SSHD’s refusal to accept them for the purposes of that scheme breached their rights under the EU-UK Withdrawal Agreement. The SSHD had appealed against a decision overturning his refusal to grant an Albanian national Mr Leonard Vasa pre-settled or settled status under the EU Settlement Scheme as a relative of an EEA national. Moreover, another Albanian national Mr Gentian Hasanaj appealed against a decision upholding the SSHD’s refusal of status in his case. While the UK had been a Member State of the EU, nationals of other Member States had rights of entry and residence under Directive 2004/38/EC. Article 2 granted similar rights to defined family members. Article 3(2) dealt with family members outside the article 2 definition. It did not oblige Member States to grant such extended family members entry and residence rights, but only to facilitate entry and residence; any rights of entry and residence extended family members had therefore derived from national law, not EU law. 

The 2016 Regulations implemented the Citizens’ Directive in the UK. Notably, regulation 7(3) provided that extended family members issued with an EEA family permit, residence card or registration certificate had to be treated as family members if they satisfied all the relevant conditions. The 2016 Regulations were revoked on 31 December 2020 at the end of the transition period of the UK’s withdrawal from the EU and the EU-UK Withdrawal Agreement sought to protect those who had exercised free movement rights prior to the UK’s withdrawal. Article 10(2) provided that extended family members “whose residence was facilitated by the host State in accordance with its national legislation before the end of the transition period in accordance with [art.3(2) of the Directive] shall retain their right of residence” and article 18 provided that the UK could provide for a new residence status. Article 18(1)(l)(iv) provided that documents required for that status could include, for extended family members, “a document issued by the relevant authority in the host State in accordance with [art.3(2)]”. The EU Settlement Scheme, set out as Appendix EU of the Immigration Rules, provided the new residence status. Mr Vasa and Mr Hasanaj had both come to the UK border before the end of the transition period and they each had a sibling who was an EU national living in the UK and UK immigration officers stamped their passports with the words “Admitted to the United Kingdom under the Immigration (EEA) Regulations 2016” and they were allowed to travel into the UK.

FDA, R (On the Application Of) v Minister for the Cabinet Office & Anor [2024] EWHC 1729 (Admin) (05 July 2024)

Rwanda removals were the Conservative party’s vanity project which cost the UK taxpayer £700 million. Chamberlain J examined the legal effect of rule 39 indication and his Lordship then proceeded to address the duties of civil servants, the effect of section 5 in domestic law and the interpretation of the code.
