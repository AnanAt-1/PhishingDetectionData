What to do in Taiwan as a tourist?

Taiwan is a captivating island, rich in varied landscapes and fascinating culture. From historic temples and vibrant night markets to spectacular natural sites such as Taroko Gorge and Sun Moon Lake, every corner of the island offers unique experiences. Whether you're into hiking, local cuisine or cultural discovery, Taiwan is sure to seduce you with its harmonious blend of modernity and ancient traditions.

What to do in Taiwan?

Taiwan, a dynamic island in East Asia, offers a diversity of landscapes and unique cultural experiences. With its futuristic skyscrapers, ancient temples, impressive national parks and lively night markets, it's an ideal destination for lovers of nature and culture.

Tourism in Taiwan: Top places to visit

  • Taipei 101: The world's tallest former skyscraper, offering incredible panoramic views.
  • Shilin night market: Taste the delights of Taiwanese street food.
  • Taroko Gorge: Magnificent national park with spectacular scenery and hiking trails.
  • Sun Moon Lake: A picturesque lake surrounded by mountains, ideal for boating and cycling.
  • Tainan: Former capital, famous for its historic temples and culinary culture.
  • Kenting National Park: Popular seaside resort with beaches and tropical nature.
  • Jiufen: Charming mountain village, source of inspiration for the film Chihiro's Journey.
  • Green Island: Popular destination for diving and hot springs.

Tourist attractions in Taiwan

Taiwan is a fascinating island that combines modernity and tradition, breathtaking natural landscapes and urban bustle. For visitors, this destination is packed with iconic places and unique activities to discover the island's cultural and natural wealth.

 

Taipei 101: A symbol of modernity

Taipei 101, at 508 metres high, is one of the world's most iconic skyscrapers. Located in the heart of Taipei, it was the world's tallest building for many years. A visit to its observation deck, located on the 89th floor, offers a breathtaking view of the entire city.

Don't miss its architecture, inspired by traditional Chinese culture, particularly its design, which is reminiscent of a bamboo, a symbol of prosperity. The shopping centre at its base is packed with luxury boutiques and fine restaurants, perfect for an upmarket shopping experience.

 

The National Palace: A treasure of Chinese history

Taipei's National Palace is a cultural sanctuary housing one of the most comprehensive collections of Chinese art in the world. More than 700,000 artefacts, ranging from paintings to ceramics, are on display in this museum. Each object tells the story of China for thousands of years and illustrates its rich heritage. Among the must-see pieces is the famous ‘Jade Cabbage’.

The museum also offers captivating temporary exhibitions, giving visitors an in-depth perspective on various aspects of Asian culture.

 

Beitou hot springs: a sanctuary of well-being

Located north of Taipei, Beitou is a must-see destination for relaxation enthusiasts. Renowned for its natural hot springs, the district offers both public and private baths for relaxing in serene surroundings. The Beitou Hot Springs Museum offers an insight into the history of this tradition.

Nature lovers will also appreciate the Beitou Thermal Park, where you can admire the fumaroles escaping from the rivers, creating a mysterious and soothing atmosphere.

 

Taroko National Park: A natural wonder

This national park, also known as the Taroko Gorge, is famous for its breathtaking scenery. With its impressive cliffs, winding paths and spectacular waterfalls, Taroko is a paradise for nature lovers and hikers. Among the points of interest, the Nine Bend Tunnel and the Tianxiang Marble Bridge offer sublime views.

For total immersion, visitors can spend the night in lodges located in the heart of the park and wake up to the sound of rivers and exotic birds.

 

Sun Moon Lake: A mystical gem

Sun Moon Lake, located in Nantou County, is one of Taiwan's most romantic spots. This picturesque lake owes its name to its unique shape, reminiscent of the sun on one side and the moon on the other. Here you can take a boat trip, explore the surrounding cycle paths or visit sacred temples such as the Wenwu Temple. The area is ideal for nature lovers and photography enthusiasts.

During your visit, be sure to sample the Oolong tea grown in the surrounding plantations, a local speciality that will delight tea lovers.

 

Night markets: A unique experience

Night markets are an integral part of Taiwanese culture. Their lively atmosphere and mouth-watering food stalls attract visitors from all over the world. Here are some of the most famous:

 

Shilin Market in Taipei

This market is one of the largest and most popular in Taiwan. Here you'll find an incredible variety of local dishes such as oyster omelettes, grilled chicken kebabs and fish balls. Shoppers can also pick up clothes and souvenirs.

The atmosphere is particularly lively, with street performers and traditional games to entertain visitors.

 

Luodong market

Located in Yilan county, this market is notable for its culinary speciality: roast duck. The stalls offer a host of local delicacies, accompanied by a friendly, authentic atmosphere.

Visitors can also discover local handicrafts, perfect for taking home a unique souvenir of their trip.

 

Keelung Market

This famous night market is located in the port town of Keelung. It is renowned for its fresh seafood and tasty dishes such as sticky rice dumplings and oyster pancakes. The unique maritime atmosphere makes it an unforgettable experience.

The proximity of the harbour adds an authentic touch, and local fishermen often sell their catch of the day directly to visitors.

 

Traditional festivals and events

Taiwan's cultural richness is also expressed through its festivals, which celebrate age-old traditions with a unique fervour.

 

The Lantern Festival: an explosion of light

Celebrated at the end of the Lunar New Year, the Lantern Festival is one of Taiwan's most spectacular events. At Pingxi, visitors can take part in the release of lanterns into the sky, symbolising wishes for the coming year. The scene of thousands of illuminated lanterns floating in the night is absolutely magical.

Other cities also organise parades, with lanterns in the shape of dragons, tigers or other traditional symbols.

 

Lunar New Year: A major cultural celebration

Chinese New Year is a time for family reunions and sumptuous feasts. Streets are decorated with red lanterns, temples host prayers for prosperity, and the spectacular lion and dragon dances leave a lasting impression on visitors. Traditional dishes, such as dumplings symbolising wealth, are also a highlight.

For tourists, this is a unique opportunity to experience Taiwanese hospitality and take part in local celebrations.

 

The Ghost Festival: Tribute to ancestors

Also known as the Zhongyuan Festival, the Ghost Festival is celebrated to honour the spirits of the ancestors. Food offerings are prepared, lanterns are floated on rivers and traditional opera performances entertain wandering souls. This celebration offers a fascinating insight into Taiwanese beliefs and traditions.

The festival also includes impressive temple ceremonies, where visitors can observe ancient rituals.

 

Local gastronomy: a culinary adventure

Taiwanese cuisine is a unique blend of Chinese, Japanese and local influences. Here are a few emblematic dishes to try during your visit:

 

Beef with noodle soup

This classic dish is a must for all food lovers. With its tender meat, rich broth and perfect noodles, it warms the heart and the stomach. Each region of Taiwan has its own version, offering a wide variety of flavours.

 

Stinky tofu

Despite its powerful smell, stinky tofu is a favourite with locals and adventurous tourists alike. It is often fried and served with a spicy sauce. Try it in the night markets for an authentic experience.

 

Bubble tea

Originating in Taiwan, bubble tea is a drink made from tea, milk and tapioca pearls. You'll find specialist shops in almost every street, offering a wide variety of flavours. It is one of Taiwan's most popular cultural exports.

 

Xiao long bao

These steamed buns filled with meat and broth are a must-try. Restaurants such as Din Tai Fung are especially good places to try this speciality.

 

Exotic fruit

Thanks to its tropical climate, Taiwan offers a wide variety of exotic fruits such as durian, dragon fruit and mango. Try mango-based desserts, such as ice creams or smoothies, for a refreshing experience.

 

Street snacks

As well as the major dishes, don't miss the street snacks such as iron eggs, Taiwanese sausages or pineapple cakes. These quick and tasty treats are an integral part of the dining experience.

Taiwan is a destination of choice for travellers seeking cultural, culinary and natural discoveries. Whether you're attracted by futuristic skyscrapers, age-old traditions or delicious street food, this island has it all. Get ready for an unforgettable experience and let yourself be seduced by Taiwan's unique charm. With its diverse landscapes, bustling markets and colourful festivities, Taiwan guarantees lasting memories for all who venture here.

When is the best time to visit Taiwan?

Taiwan's climate varies according to the season. To get the most out of the island, the months from October to April are ideal, as they offer a mild, dry climate, perfect for exploring the tourist sites and enjoying the local festivals. Hikers will particularly appreciate spring (March to May), when the mountains are covered in flowers. However, if you want to take part in events such as the Lunar New Year or the Lantern Festival, plan your visit for winter.

What are the most practical means of transport in Taiwan?

Taiwan has an excellent transport network. The high-speed train (THSR) links the major cities quickly, while regular trains and buses cover the more rural areas. In Taipei and Kaohsiung, subways are convenient and economical. Scooters are popular for exploring local areas, but it is advisable to have an international driving licence to hire them. For the adventurous, cycling is an excellent option for exploring Sun Moon Lake and other scenic spots.

Are there any specific recommendations for respecting Taiwanese culture?

During your visit, respect local traditions. For example, when entering a temple, wear modest clothes and avoid taking photos without permission. When sampling local dishes, it's a good idea to try everything that's served. In addition, showing respect for the locals, especially the vendors and guides, will make your stay even more enjoyable. Finally, taking part in local ceremonies or buying handicrafts directly supports the local communities.
