      N.W.T. premier race in full swing

      Members who will soon make up the N.W.T.'s new legislative assembly are jockeying for positions in cabinet and the premier's seat, in a campaign often done behind closed doors.

      Process criticized for being done behind closed doors

      CBC News · Posted: Oct 12, 2011 7:32 PM EDT | Last Updated: October 13, 2011

      Social Sharing

      Members who will soon make up the Northwest Territories' new legislative assembly are jockeying for positions in cabinet and the premier's seat.

      Most of the campaigning is done in private between MLAs and premier hopefuls. The courtship process is being criticized for leaving the public out.

      Jane Groenewegen, who will serve her fifth term as the MLA in Hay River South, says the public's interest is not being served.

      There are alliances, deals and promises of awards — like the stuff of reality TV shows such as Survivor — but it's the reality of politics in the Northwest Territories.

      "Actually, there are a lot of similarities," she said. "People want those positions so badly, it does make you wonder to what lengths they'll go to get them."

      Groenewegen said the voting pits members against each other, instead of having them work together for the public good.

      "You hate to think it could degenerate to the point of who can convince the most people that they're going to support them," she said.

      Groenewegen added that when members vote out of self-interest, the best people for the job might not succeed.

      MLA-elect David Ramsay of Kam Lake agreed that the political dance is in full swing.

      "Someone will walk into somebody's office and say, 'Listen, I'll support you if you support me' … there are alliances," he said.

      Premier, cabinet chosen by secret ballot

      In most provinces, the leader of the winning party gets to be premier. But in the Northwest Territories, independent MLAs choose the premier.

      They hold a secret ballot vote within a few weeks of an election. Cabinet is selected in the same way.

      "If you look at voter turnout in this past election, it was pathetic," Ramsay said. "We need to find a way as a government to make sure people feel connected to this process."

      Frame Lake MLA-elect Wendy Bisaro said the issue was discussed at a caucus retreat that was held during the last legislative assembly.

      "Without telling tales out of school, it was generally felt [that] no, this was not the right time to make changes," said Bisaro. "But I think the noise gets louder every election … we have to make some changes."

      For the first time, Bisaro said she plans to canvass her constituents online about who she should support for the territory's top job.

      MLAs can consult their constituents, but laws would have to be changed to give the public a direct vote.  If that happens, the Northwest Territories would become the first government in Canada to let the people directly elect their premier.

      At least four members are vying for the premier's job, including Norman Yakeleya, Bob McLeod, Kevin Menicoche and Michael Miltenberger.

      After the dust settles from the secret ballots, Groenewegen said members may have to deal with the betrayals and surprise winners so that they don't take those grudges into the new assembly.
