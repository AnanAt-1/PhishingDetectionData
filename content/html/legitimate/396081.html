Selling Tech basketball, part 3: Texas Tech plans to draw crowds back to United Spirit Arena

The athletic department is slashing season ticket prices and opening the best seats in the gym for public purchase.

Courtney Linehan

A-J SPORTS EDITOR

Note: This is the final in a three-part series about attendance at Texas Tech men's basketball games. The first story, which ran Sunday, can be read here.

The second story, which ran Monday, can be read here.

Jordan Kvanli rarely missed a men's basketball game during his first semester at Texas Tech in spring 2004, when Tech made it to the NCAA tournament's second round that season and guard Andre Emmett won the NCAA slam dunk contest and became an NBA draft pick. Kvanli continued attending games the next year, when Tech made it to the Sweet Sixteen, and even as former coach Bob Knight worked toward his NCAA-record 880th career win.

Kvanli, who graduated from Tech this spring, says has hasn't been back to United Spirit Arena in two years.

"A few years ago I knew every single player on the team," Kvanli said. "Now I couldn't tell you one."

Texas Tech has a plan in place to bring fans like Kvanli back to its men's basketball games. The athletic department announced Monday that it is slashing season ticket prices and opening the best seats in the gym for public purchase, hoping to fill areas close to the court that were once reserved for Private Seat Licensees.

Tech is also devoting new resources to selling group packages and hopes an exclusive bar and grill area will create a need-to-be-there environment.

But is it enough?

Overall attendance rose in 2009-10 from the previous season, and student attendance nearly doubled. But those numbers are still significantly lower than they were four years ago.

"It seems like it became the general culture that people weren't going,"

Kvanli said. "I don't really care about the team anymore, and if nobody else is going, it diminishes the appeal anyway."

That's tough to hear for athletes like Nick Okorie, who was a shooting guard for Tech during the 2008-09 and 2009-10 seasons. Okorie watched fan reaction greatly affect games at New Mexico and Wichita State, where they cheered so loudly the Tech team could not hear coaches' instructions during timeouts. It affected players' concentration during the two-point loss in Wichita, Kan., and 15-point loss in Albuquerque, N.M.

"Fans give anybody a home-court advantage," Okorie said. "When you play at Wichita State or New Mexico the fans become a big part, because you can't hear play calls. It throws off players' timing and judgment."

Trying to fill out the student section last year, Tech promised students who attended a certain number of nonconference games the best seats when teams like Texas and Texas A&M came to town. Approximately 70 more students came to early games compared to comparable dates a year earlier.

An average of 517 students attended each of Tech's first six games, meaning nearly 2,900 lower-bowl seats reserved for them remained empty. Hoping to rally their peers, team managers went door-to-door in the dorms the night before Tech hosted 12th-ranked Washington on Dec. 3, 2009.

Whether because of their efforts or the high-profile opponent, more than 2,900 students were there to storm the court after the Red Raiders won 99-92 in overtime, a victory that propelled them into the Associated Press Top 25.

After seeing that turnout, the managers distributed 1,000 flyers before all but one Big 12 Conference home game.

There's no way to measure the effect of their efforts, but student attendance at conference games was 2,089 last season, up from 932 a year earlier and 1,760 when Tech hosted the same teams in 2007-08.

Coach Pat Knight said it showed that legwork could bring bodies to the gym.

"Our managers took it upon themselves to do something and they made a difference," Knight said. "So much of everything is done on the computer, e-mail, texting, but this has to be done in person."

With about 20 percent of United Spirit Arena stands reserved for students, Tech's marketing department and ticket office are focused on filling the other 80 percent when the 2010-11 season begins in four months.

Tech saw cheap tickets turn into big revenue when it offered significant price reductions for games against Stanford and Washington. It hopes the new $275 season ticket price and availability of more sideline seats will return similar results.

The marketing office is also about to debut a new call center with the primary purpose of selling group and season tickets.

"Our goal is to turn all those people who came to single games last year into season ticket sales this year," said Scott Orsheln, director of marketing and promotions.

Knight knows his team's success will make a major difference. Attendance dipped while Tech's record faltered late last season; approximately 5,000 people went to the each of the final Big 12 home games against Kansas State and Baylor.

But find a way to double that, let them all sit in the lower-level sections, and Tech might get the boost teams at New Mexico and Wichita State enjoy.

"I've always said all you've got to do is fill that lower bowl," Knight said. "The way the arena's built, that's 10,000 seats and it gives you a home-court advantage."
