Bahamas 2025 – Day -1

leave a comment »

As we start our annual spring journey to the Bahamas to find Bonefish, I started to experiment with an articulated fly to imitate a mantis shrimp. This is my first pass after many hours of thinking.

.

Some notes and my thinking of importance:

  1. A mantis shrimp comes in many colours, but I have decided to focus on tan and add cream later.
    • Segmented strips are good. I am relying upon a dark underbody.
      .
  2. IMHO Important: General shape > The body is thicker at its tail than at its head.
    • I am using a 9″ section of a brush (for both segment) and I believe to make a better imitation, I need to make my own brush, with a taper. With the fly above, I have done some trimming, and decided to see how it behaves in the salt before I trim more.
      .
  3. IMHO Important: Minimum length of 50mm (2″)
    • There are records in Dick Brown’s Bonefish Pattern book of Bonefish caught on on 2-3″ imitations. Given the length, I have chosen to use a
      • Senyo Articlated Shank (in this example, 25mm) and will consider 35mm.
      • Mustad 34007 Size 4 hook, but will use Size 2 here-after.
        • The Mustad 34007 hook is old stock and there are many better, but as long as you sharpen it, it is still a good hook.
          .
  4. IMHO Important: Legs are a notable feature in the front (half).
    • I am using micro chenille.
      .
  5. IMHO Important: Claws are retracted and a small feature, until they strike. So keep it small.
    • I am using a small hen feature, after experiments with hair failed.
      .
  6. Antenna are short and stout, which I have deviated for this sample.
    • I have used boar here, and like it, but probably will tie it in shorter next time.
      .
  7. Eyes are usually muted, here I am using a green eye (bead) with a black point.
    • In some species, the eyes are oblong, but I have decided to keep the eyes low key and next time, I may forgo the black center (brown?).
      .
  8. Tail is a loop silicon segment, thus providing some shape, some movement and little resistance.
    .

New Zealand 2024 ‘6 – Leaders and Tippet

leave a comment »

.

For fear of not having, I bought and brought too many leader options, and could have simplified for dry fly and nymphs to.

  • RIO 3x 15′ or SA 3x 14′ Leader
    • As you are hunting for fish, you are not casting frequently, and damage should be infrequent. For a week or more, six leaders will be more than enough, as long you are maintaining-rebuilding them as you go. I believe we consumed 3.
  • Tippet spools of ~0.024+ to rebuild the butt , example
    • Maxima Clear – 40lb at 0.024″(which is stiffer than RIO, SA)
    • SA – 50lb at 0.026″
  • plus Tippet Sppols for 3x, 4x, 5x
    • If you are really concerned about large cicadas,
      .
  • To make a longer leader, with a blood knot add 0.024+” material to the butt.
  • If you need finer, cut off 1 to 2.5′ off the tip and add the finer material
    .
    These comments are supported with the measure line profiles below and what did work. Please note, I manipulated the tippet mesaurements, so you can see RIO and SA profiles, but bother had a 0.007″ tippets (supporting a 3x leader).
    .
    These recommendations were updated March 27, 2027 after I found and test a a long SA leader.
    .

.

You can also consider:

  • Adding 3-6′ to an Airflo 10′ Polyleader
  • Fluorcarbon for the nymphing and even small tippet rings. I respool Seaguar Tatsu 6#.
  • And if you are streamer fishing, again something simple:
    • 20lb Maxima UltraGreen double uni-knot to
    • 10lb Seaquar Blue Fluorocarbon
      .

Guides were fussy on lrngth (longest we used was 18′) and the tippet size – focusing on 3x or 4x. Later in summer, consider 5x, and length, but never on profile. id est, no need for a complex George Harvey Leader. If you want a complex leader for a complicate drift, go for it, but as wind was a constant companion, what was usually need was a strong butt.
.

The purpose of the leader is simply present to the fish up, and keep that fly line well behind the trout. Given the wind and purpose, a long and strong butt is a good thing.
.

.
The advantage of buying is that you are not fussing about with blood knots in the field, something I appreciate as my eyesight is fading with age.
.

If you make want to make your own, which is “cheaper”, my formula is simple. Please note the Maxima diameters listed are those that I measured on my spools and not the OEMs.

  • Perfection loop at the top to a 6 to 9’ of Maxima UltraGreen 30lb (.022 diameter) butt.
    .
    Using blood knots into a fast transition 2 1/2′ (30 inches) to the tippet
    .
  • 9” of Maxima 20lb – 16,0
  • 9” of Maxima 12lb – 13.5
  • 12 to 18” of RIO 1x at 0.10
    .
    Yes, I am skipping 25 and 15lb
    .
    And then blood knot the tippet
    .
  • 3+ of tippet, like RIO’s 3x.
    .
    If somethigns does not go really wrong, you are just changing tippet on the RIO 1X.
    .

You need to know how to make:

  • Perfection loop
  • Blood Knot
  • Uni and Double Uni Knot
    .
  • There is nothing wrong with a triple surgeon’s knot if you are in poor light to change the tippet
    .

.

🙂

.

New Zealand 2024 ‘5 – New and Improved?

leave a comment »

As I reflect on our recent experiences, I have look back at our previous trips, including New Zealand in 2000 (24 years ago), and ponder the question: “Has there been any real new and improve updates in fly fishing (equipment) in the past 3 decades?”.

Trout Reels – Compared to my old Hardy’s and SA System 2 reels, reels available today are a notable and worthy improvement from 1995 with light large arbour reels with a good drag. My old reels are fine for the smaller trout in the Adirondacks (upper New York state), but not for New Zealand.

As noted earlier in this series, I lost my first 2 large trout (~6lb) with the old Hardy’s and happliy changed to Galvin Torque. These are great reels, and the alternative for a great reel would be a Daniellson.

I recognize for spey fishing, many are devoted to the old Hardy’s and frown on modern reels, but the final tippet when you swing a fly is much stronger and can cushion without breaking the change up.
.

.

Trout Rods – Not really. Yes, I like the the new Scotts that are frequently held up as the right rod for New Zealand, but these rods are not better than our 30 year old Scott STS. The only improvement is the recognition, by a few, a grip should not “be” fine and can include a fighting built.

.

I will contend for saltwater fishing, the crop of rods that emerged a decade ago (starting with us with the Scott Meridian), was a vast improvement over the previous generations of broomsticks.
.
Similarily these nwer graphite materials have made Spey rods lighter and possibly easier to design, I would contend many of the old Spey rods are great to cast and fish as most of today’s offerring. The gain in Spey rods, is the expansion of offerings and OEMs.
.

.

Trout Fly Lines – not really, My old SA lines, like the Ultra are just as good as …. all of the coatnigs, et cetera provide no substantial benefit for trout fishing. Also there has been no real gains for leaders and tippet.
.
For saltwater, I will acknowledge the current offeering is an improvement, and the available of flurocarbon is great thing; but like what remains the same is that leader-tippet quality can be inconsistent.
.
Spey lines of today are a vast improvement (for me), and thankful for the small group of entrepreneurs who habe created some fine lines, including Gene (Boss), Simon (NextCast), Tim (Bridge) and James (Gaelforce).
.
.

Hooks for Trout – Definitely an improvement, and worthy an update. The Europen influence with competition barbless hooks is possibily the biggest gain in trout fishing in the past 3 decade. My old standards like TMC 3761, 5262, 5263, Mustad 3399 – fine for smaller trout – will be replaced by hooks with larger gaps and improve bends.
.
I provide the following comparison below, and look first at the smaller hooks below (<8mm body) from Hanak againt the older TMC and Mustad.
.

.
I am definitely build future small nymphs (<8mm) on the Hanak H150, and for now consider the Kamasan B175 for larger, as I investigate the Hends 254 and Hanak 200 & 230.
.
Please note, size reference such as #12 is ambiguous and inconsistent across models and manufacturers. What is important to me, is body length, followed by abaility to hook and hold a fly – which is a function of eye, gap, how the point is created and shape of the bend.
.
Oh yes, my body length measurements (above) is very subjective and inconsitent, and thus, only provides an relative estimate. (For example the 3399 #10 is measured to be larger than the Kamasan B175 #10, which is probably not true.)
.

.

Waders and Gore Rain Jacket – No improvement in the waders, and I will note my old green Simms G3 Jacket in Green with the neoprene wrist cuffs was better made than the dark G3s today, where Simms has been cutting corners-costs. As posted earlier, I look forward to Grundens entering the market.
.
And I lament the lost of good old neoprene waders too, though you might find a pair linked to hunting or can get a custom set made from a scuba shop.
.
At best, what we have today is as good as what was available 30 years ago.
.

,

Boots – Absolutely a gain, if you accept that felt boots are better for the enviroment and you are wading with studs. The other advantage – in winter – rubber points do not pick up snow as you walk in and out from the river.
.

.

Packs – The availability of waterproof packs is the only gain in 30 years, an advantage for my wife, who at 5’1″, often gets here pack into the water. Yet, many of the classic vests and bags of 30 years ago are as good today, as anything yes on the market.
.
It is interesting to note that Fishpond continues to sell this old design, validating the gains today are limited.
.

.
Now I am waiting for a larger waterproof sling that I can wear on my left shoulder.
.

.

Shirts and Colour – Yes (IMHO) and no. I prefer the simplicity of the hoodie of today to wear and wash versus the traditional front button shirt with front pockets and back vents; BUT, the lack of a light natural green and ground-tan to help blend into background is a disappointment (and was available 20+ years ago) from Orvis, LL Bean, et cetera. There are some light tans and blues available.
.

.

Fly Boxes – What is available today is absolute improvement of the old Wheatly Boxes of old. A shout-out to:

  • Tacky Original (with Silicon) Box
  • Umpqua Silicon Essential Box
  • and the classic Myran Boxes
    .

.

Supporting women anglers – Marginal Gain. Simply the industry needs to do a better job supporting women anglers for outer wear – waders, boots, et cetera. A shout-out to Miss Mayfly working to fill in this void.
.

.

🙂

.

New Zealand 2024 ‘4 – Spey and Streamers

leave a comment »

New Zealand is known for sight fishing to large trout, for it has large trout, clear waters and fish density is low. And sight fishing – like bonefishing – is something we like to do. To help you start, you need a guide to help you / teach you in seeing that trout, but the guides have no experience / confidence in streamers; and thus you need to strike out on your own if you wish to fish streamers or cast with a spey rod.
,

So I only cast 4 times to a sighted trout, after we cast to the fish already with a dry fly or dry & dropper. The results:
.

  1. Weighted Brown Tube Bunny – brown trout move and follow fly, but did not commit.
  2. Weighted Brown Tube Bunny – brown chase fly, thought it took it (guide was excited), did a strip strike, but there was no weight. Possible my error in a premature strip strike. Upon reflection, we agreed to to “trust the tug”.
  3. Tube Bunny (change up – colour forgotten (not noted)) – Fish ran out of pool.
  4. Sex Dungeon – large brown aggressively charge, but did not commit.
    .

I can think of one other lie, it would have been better to start with a streamer, than trying to make a difficult cast (over-handing bannk, grass and tress).
.

With an experience at the lake emptying into a river we watch from a swing bridge slam a spinner, so I am confident a swung fly or streamer will work.
.

One other observation – of the three fish who chase the fly – they all move substantial distances to the fly, more than they would have for a small nymph. So you do not need to present the fly at their nose.
.

Lessons and advice I will apply in the future:
.

  1. Be prepared to go it yourself.
  2. Your standard 9′ 6wt will suffice in all, but the larget rivers, so a spey rod is a luxury and should be only considered the third rod.
  3. Trust the tug.
  4. Weigted flies.
    .

Some fish images from – none of them caught on a streamer.
.

.

.

.

Judy’s brown earlier in the day.

.

As you can see, a spey rod is too much rod, but a 6wt is just fine.

.

As you get further down the Maturua, then a trout spey if fine (from the day prevoius to those above).
