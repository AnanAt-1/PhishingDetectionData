Lack of English Players in the Premier League Continues

  • Geoff
  • September 19, 2011
  • 12:00 am
  • No Comments

This past weekend showed up another failed statistic for English football – a lack of English players playing in the “best league in the world”.

While Oliver Holt beats his drum to get more managers of colour into the game through the Rooney Rule, you have to ask why nobody seems to care that only 37% of players lining up in the starting XI for English teams in the English Premier League were actually English this past weekend? This is a number similar to week #1 of the English Premier League – where, of the 246 players to have played Premier League football through August 16, 2011 – 95 (38.6%) of these were English or eligible for England.

That awful 38.6% rises to a measly 45.5% if you include English-born players (17) who now represent other nations (eg- Phil Bardsley, Rory Delap).

This week, however, Arsenal failed to even start a single English player. This was closely followed by Wigan Athletic who only had two English players represented, one of which was Victor Moses – and he’s close to declaring his allegiance to Nigeria.

[divider]

Below, we take a look at English born or capped players who started at the weekend, came on as a substitute, or were on the bench for their Premier League club. A player who is substituted on is not double counted in the bench statistics. Those numbers essentially represent English players who did not feature.

Example: Shola Ameobi for Newcastle was counted as an English player, due to his England U21 caps and no international switch at Senior level, but his brother Sammy Ameobi has opted to play for Nigeria so is therefore not counted.

Example: Junior Hoilett was born in Canada and is also eligible for Jamaica and England (through residency), but has not been capped nor declared, so is therefore not counted.

If these numbers were whittled down even further to take into account age (i.e.- U21 eligibility), England retirees, goalkeeping staff, and the potential for international involvement – they would be even more damning.
