Henry Brown and Mary Clayton
Henry Brown was born May 1, 1800 in County Kerry, Ireland and died 29 Jun 1890 in Iron Mountain, Dickinson County, Michigan. He married  Mary Clayton in 1831. She was born in 1815 in Wales and died 15 Sep 1898 in Iron Mountain, Dickinson County, Michigan. They are both buried at Catholic Cemetery, Quinnesec, Dickinson County, Michigan.

In 1840, I am told, Henry and Mary and their two children fled Ireland because they had hidden a Catholic priest who was wanted by the British authorities. The Browns landed at Tracadie, New Brunswick, Canada. Their goal was Ashfield Township in Ontario and one can only speculate on the difficulty of their long and ardous journey from New Brunswick to Ontario to Ashfield Township and finally locating near the town of Goderich.

In the 1851 Ontario, Canada, Huron County, Ashfield Township agricultural census,* Henry and his family are reported to hold 100 acres. Ten of these acres are under cultivation. Ninety acres are said to be under wool or wild. Henry had four acres in wheat which produced twenty bushels, one acre in potatoes which produced thirty bushels. The land produced six bundles or tons of hay, the sheep produced twenty-two pounds of wool which made forty eight yards of flannel (Annie Brown Stack had a spinning wheel in her home and must have well known how to use it). Henry had five oxen, nine milk cows, one calf, eleven sheep, six pigs and no horses. The family made one hundred pounds of butter in 1851 and salted away four barrels of pork. Their home is listed as being a one story log shanty. In 1851 there were eleven people in the Brown family. Four more children, including Annie Brown Stack, came later. It would be interesting to know the dimensions of the log house.

During the lumber and mining boom in the Upper Peninsula, Henry and Mary moved their family to Negaunee, Michigan (1867). Henry farmed, built log cabins, was a blacksmith and was able to make plows and door hardware for the cabins. He also did mine timbering. In the book, "Spring of Gladness" by Mary Brown McGonigal (Henry's granddaughter), there are several proud and fond references to the skills of Henry Brown. He passed these skills onto his children. Mary mentions on p. 120 that in the spring of 1883, her father, Michael, asked his parents to send him some hardwood tree slips. Henry and Mary packed and sent them from Northern Michigan to Idaho. Michael planted them on the two banks of his new ditch running east and west along the north boundary of his acreage. When Mary Brown McGonigal wrote her book in 1975, it was still a beautiful grove of trees.

My Grandmother, Annie Brown Stack, too, spoke lovingly of her parents and truly appreciated their hard work and accomplishments.

In 1886, Mary and Henry moved to Iron Mountain, Michigan to be near three of their daughters and many grandchildren.

Henry died June 29, 1890 at 90 years of age. He is buried in the Catholic cemetery in Quinnesec, Michigan, Block 1, Lot #1.

*In 1991, Casey Shields Matranga of Manhattan Beach, Ca. great granddaughter of Elizabeth Brown McKenna, found and sent to us the 1851 census information

Mary Clayton Brown was born in Wales in 1815. We do not know the first name of her father, but according to Annie Brown Stack, he was the captain of a whaling ship. Her mother's maiden name was Hoare (pronounced hair). While she was still very young, both Mary's parents were lost at sea. Mary was sent to live with her mother's sister in Ireland, attended a convent school and was adept at languages. Mary and Henry Brown were married when she was sixteen and he was thirty-one years old. They had sixteen children, twelve of whom grew to adulthood: John W (1837), Henry (1838), Michael (1839), Mary (1842), Elizabeth (1844), Nora Peter (1845), Julia (1847), Joseph (1849) Annie (1850), Margaret (1851) and Hannah (1853).

Annie Brown Stack often spoke lovingly of her parents. She had many skills which must have been learned from them. I would imagine that with such a large family and living on a farm, there was more then enough work for all. Margaret Stack Dundon told her daughter, Margaret, that Mary was a wonderful storyteller and young and old enjoyed being around her.

John Brown, Mary and Henry's oldest son, and his wife, Mary McKenna Brown, had a farm in Idaho on land joining Michael Brown's. In 1888, Mary died of pneumonia and in 1890. Nora, the youngest of their five children, died of the same illness. John was heartbroken and depressed over these losses and according to the book "Spring of Gladness" by Mary McGonigal Brown, his health failed and he died March 3, 1891. The four surviving children were Minnie age 19, Frank 18, Sarah 14, and Katharine 8. Mary Clayton Brown insisted they come to her even though she was alone. Henry had died in 1890. Having been orphaned herself while very young, she must have felt that she could best take care of them. Frank opted to stay and work the farm but the three girls went to their grandmothers and remained with her until her death. They eventually married and had families of their own.

Mary died September 15, 1898. The following appeared in the Iron Mountain Press on Thursday September 22, 1898:

"Mrs. Henry Brown died at her home on West D St. last Thursday morning at the advanced age of 83 years. The deceased had been a resident of this city for 12 years, and was a most estimable lady. She was the mother of sixteen children, eleven of whom are living as follows: Mrs. Catherine Corrigan, Kinlough, Canada; Henry Brown, Nevada; Michael and Joseph Brown, Bellevue, Idaho; Peter Brown, Provost, Utah; Mrs. Ellen Sullivan, Goodrich, Ontario; Mesdames Eliza and Mary McKenna, Quinnesec; Mrs. Annie Stack, Escanaba; Mrs. Margaret Atkinson, Iron Mountain; and Mrs. Hannah Atkinson, Green Bay The funeral was held at St. Mary's last Sunday.

Mary is buried next to her husband, Henry in the Quinnesec, Michigan Cemetery in Lot 1, Block 1, of the first addition.

Dad and I and your Aunts, Mary and Ruthie, visited the Browns' grave in 1985. Mary Brown's gravestone had been broken in half. We drove to Iron Mountain for epoxy glue and Dad repaired it.

Some notes about the Clayton, Hoare, and DesBrisay families

I have tried hard to find a record of Mary's parents being lost at sea. Lloyd's of London referred me to the Guildhall Library in London where their early records are kept. The library wrote "whaling was an isolated and dangerous activity and numerous vessels disappeared without trace." Lloyd's did not begin to keep lists of such missing vessels until 1873. Dad and I spent an afternoon at the Library of Congress going through "Lloyd's Lists" of the right period and found both owners and "Masters" by the name of Clayton and Hoare. Mary must have come from a truly "seafaring family."

While in Pembroke, Wales in 1991, I checked the South Wales telephone book for Claytons and Hoares. There were fifty-two Claytons and forty-one Hoares listed in South Wales. Pembroke is a an old harbor town and nearby a major British navy presence has existed for hundreds of years. I cannot help but wonder if this might be where the Claytons lived.

In the 1911 Encyclopedia Britannica, there is reference to an 1883 "History of the Hoare or Hore Family" by Edward Hoare. It is on microfilm at the Library of Congress and recently I made a copy of it.

The Hoares are an ancient family and came to England with William the Conqueror from Normandy. The word Hoare means a boundary marker or it can mean the color white as in hoarfrost. There was a knight by this name whose suit of armor was white and was known as "the White Knight." In the 12th century, members of this substantial and prolific family settled in Pembrokeshire, South Wales or Dyfed which is its ancient name. There were so many English people in the area that it was sometimes called "Little England." The Hoares accumulated an impressive amount of land and held various public offices, sheriff, representatives in Parliament, etc. Some of this name participated in the conquest of Ireland, including the "White Knight" and then settled in Wexford and Cork. In later years many were officers in the British occupying army, ministers in the Church of Ireland, bankers, lawyers and so on. For generations they were part of the privileged British occupying Ireland and Wales.

Our 1991 trip to the British Isles and Ireland reminded us of the many civilizations that have existed there. The Celts and their amazing piles of stones; the Romans (in England 500 years), and their 5000 miles of roads, their walls and baths; the Vikings (there 200 years) with the many cities they established and named still thriving, plus the red headed individuals who may be reminders of the genes these people left behind; the Normans and the castles they built to protect themselves from other marauders and the locals; the wretch Cromwell and his armies; and the way each of these groups seem to have smashed as much as they could the building efforts of those who came before.

It took the Irish a long time to get rid of the British and those citizens who did not wish to become Roman Catholic and truly Irish. In many towns, Church of Ireland (Anglican) buildings still remain symbolic targets and are seen one after another closed and chained or worse. The dear little church in Waterford, formerly St. Patrick's, in which Theophilus DesBrisay received his priests orders was sold to the Presbyterian Church and has been bombed twice. Many of.the palatial homes built by wealthy British belong to others, the public buildings belong to Ireland. The Georgian doors of Dublin have been painted brilliant colors and are identified with the Irish but the ancient St. Patrick's and Christ Church Cathedrals are some of the very visible reminders of the 700 year occupation.

Four generations of DesBrisays lived in Ireland. For three generations, they were officers in the British army as were others of your Huguenot ancestors. At different times they were stationed at Cork Hill (now called Dublin Castle) in Dublin. Cork Hill is an installation that must have been then like our Pentagon. The castle stands damaged but is now being repaired and is in use. Another way of life, another civilization "bit the dust" and you had forebears on both sides, the privileged and the downtrodden.

These pictures and and the two stories above are from a book written by Jean Patrick Stack Jones a granddaughter of John King Stack and Jane Anne Brown. which she had found at the Orange County Genealogy Library. Ca.
