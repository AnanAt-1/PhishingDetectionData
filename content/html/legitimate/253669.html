Random House Sues Over Rights to Publishing E-Books

By David D. Kirkpatrick

  • Feb. 28, 2001
See the article in its original context from
February 28, 2001, Section C, Page 5Buy Reprints
View on timesmachine
TimesMachine is an exclusive benefit for home delivery and digital subscribers.

Opening a new battle over the ground rules of the nascent business of electronic books, the Random House division of the Bertelsmann media empire has sued a start-up, RosettaBooks, over who owns the rights to sell digital versions of previously published books.

The lawsuit escalates a struggle with authors and agents over how to compensate writers for digital sales of their books and at the same time seeks to squelch a scramble among established publishers and their upstart rivals bidding for the electronic rights to popular printed books.

Until recent years, few book contracts specified who owned rights to electronic books. Authors and agents have argued that digital rights belong to authors unless explicitly specified, seeking new advances on royalties from digital sales. Although consumer demand for books read on computer screens is not yet known, established publishers and newer competitors have been trying to gather up those rights, hoping that a lucrative market will develop. Random House, the largest English-language publisher, now contends that except in special circumstances, the print publisher, not the author, automatically owns the electronic rights to a book.

RosettaBooks, a start-up publisher based in New York and founded by the agent Arthur Klebanoff, focuses mainly on publishing digital versions of books previously published in print by other houses. Among other titles, RosettaBooks is selling electronic versions of ''Confessions of Nat Turner'' and ''Sophie's Choice'' by William Styron as well as ''Slaughterhouse-Five,'' ''Breakfast of Champions,'' ''The Sirens of Titan,'' ''Cat's Cradle'' and ''Player Piano'' by Kurt Vonnegut. Mr. Styron's and Mr. Vonnegut's novels were both previously published by Random House. Mr. Klebanoff said that he negotiated with the authors' representatives to acquire the electronic rights.

Mr. Styron's agent and Mr. Vonnegut's lawyer each declined to comment. Random House has not named the authors in its suit.

In the suit, filed in United States District Court in the Southern District of New York, Random House argues that publishers own the rights to sell electronic editions of printed books just as film companies have the right to sell videocassette tapes of movies, citing an appeals court ruling that ''the physical form in which the motion picture is fixed -- film, tape, discs and so forth -- is irrelevant.''
