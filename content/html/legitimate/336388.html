Alabama native Mac McAnally records his first live album in the Shoals (video)

  • Updated: Sep. 16, 2011, 9:30 a.m.
  • |Published: Sep. 16, 2011, 8:30 a.m.
By
  • Mary Colurso | mcolurso@al.com
Mac McAnally, born in Red Bay, says he's proud to be part of the Year of Alabama Music. "I think it's great for the state to stand up and acknowledge what a great musical heritage they have," he says. His personal heroes include Hank Williams, Hank Williams Jr., Emmylou Harris and Nat King Cole. "I listen to Nat King Cole almost every morning," he says. (Stan Kellam)

BIRMINGHAM, Alabama -- When Mac McAnally apologizes, he holds nothing back.

Forget the heartfelt speeches, sorrowful looks or rivers of tears. During a Monday recording session in Nashville with Lee Ann Womack, McAnally made a much more significant gesture.

"Working in the studio, whenever I made a mistake, I’d offer to give one of my CMAs back," he says.

There's a chuckle in his voice, but McAnally — a guitarist, songwriter, singer and producer — really does have a storehouse of CMA Awards to draw upon. He's earned three back-to-back trophies from the Country Music Association as musician of the year, in 2008, 2009 and 2010.

McAnally received another nomination this year, and he's in prestigious company, vying with mandolinist Sam Bush, Dobro player Jerry Douglas, steel guitarist Paul Franklin and guitarist Dann Huff.

Overall, 2011 marks his fifth CMA nomination in that category, but McAnally, a modest fellow, will be the first to downplay his streak.

"I don’t understand it," he says. "I don’t feel worthy in that context. I think I’ve definitely had more than a great run."

Mac McAnally performed at the 2011 WhistleStop Festival in Huntsville. (The Huntsville Times/John Perry)

The Alabama native, born in Red Bay, has forged a music career that many would envy. It includes his solo work as a folk singer-songwriter — McAnally has 11 records to his credit — and high-profile stints as a key member of Jimmy Buffett's Coral Reefer Band.

McAnally also collaborates with country stars such as Kenny Chesney, who covered McAnally's "Down the Road" in 2008 and featured him on the recording as a duet partner.

A network of friends and colleagues relies on McAnally for his songwriting talents, and he's highly respected as a producer, teaming with artists that range from Ricky Skaggs to Marty Stuart to Sawyer Brown.

"I’ve always been somebody who’s done the other guy’s stuff first," says McAnally, 54. "I’m grateful to the folks who allow me to do what I love and call it a job."

His own music does take center stage occasionally, and will do so again on Oct. 11, when McAnally releases his next album, "Mac McAnally in Muscle Shoals."

Its 27 tracks were recorded live during the 2010 W.C. Handy Music Festival in Florence, as McAnally performed a concert in the Norton Auditorium at the University of North Alabama.

He hand-picked a band for the occasion, including the rhythm section of the Coral Reefers, bassist Jim Mayer and drummer Roger Guth. Keyboardist Steve Nathan, a top session player in Nashville, signed on for the evening. So did guitarists Jack Pearson and Duncan Cameron, veterans of the Allman Brothers Band, Sawyer Brown and the Amazing Rhythm Aces.

"The W.C. Handy folks said, ‘Do a show of everything you do’," McAnally says. "They wanted me to play all the types of music I’ve done in my career. I’ve had a pop deal, a couple of country deals, and I’m basically a folk singer. It was sort of an ambitious thing to throw at a crowd. Hopefully, my God-given limitations as a singer are the glue that holds it all together."

Storytelling introductions are paired with tunes such as "Blame it on New Orleans," "Opinion on Love," "Back Where I Come From" and "All These Years." The set also includes "Down the Road," "It’s My Job," "On Account of You" and "Little Blue Pill."

Want Mac McAnally to autograph his new CD for you? That's automatic on pre-sales of the disc ($15), available via his website.

Although this marks McAnally’s first live disc as a solo artist, and his only attempt at a career retrospective, he wasn’t inclined to fret.

"At that time, I was doing so many different things, I didn’t have time to get too nervous about it," he says. "The hall felt great and the crowd was wonderful. The band played one rehearsal. I got lucky."

Perhaps it helped that McAnally feels completely at home in the Shoals, where he spends part of every year at his house and studio in Sheffield. In fact, McAnally says, he finds himself craving a dose of the Shoals when a concert tour or Nashville commitments keep him away too long.

"The standard line is, ‘I miss the place like a girlfriend,’" he says. "Every time I have control over a project, I bring it down there. I’m real happy to be a part of what’s going on in Muscle Shoals."

What make the Shoals so special? McAnally credits the atmosphere, the people, the rich music history, the variety of music talent and the winding Tennessee River. (No wonder, he says, the Yuchi American Indian tribe called it "the Singing River.")

When McAnally was asked to participate in a documentary that's in production, "Sweet Home Alabama: The Music of Muscle Shoals," he was happy to sit down for an interview.

"They wanted to talk to me about my experiences and the next generation," McAnally says. "I'm about a half-century beyond the heyday. But I'm a big fan of that time, because they looked at every type of music the same. One day it would be Cher; one day it would be Aretha Franklin; one day it would be Willie Nelson. Whenever I'm doing anything, I check it and recheck it against that mindset."

Excerpts from his interview are featured in a promotional clip for the film, shown at this year's Sidewalk Moving Picture Festival in Birmingham.

McAnally, who grew up bashful on a farm in Belmont, Miss., says he’s learned to be a decent speaker in public. But basking in the limelight doesn’t come naturally to him.

"Take my guitar away, and I won’t say a word," he admits. "It’s my security blanket."
