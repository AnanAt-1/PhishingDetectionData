Hidden Costs of Search Engine Optimization

 Author: Michael Martinez

Search engine optimization is a technical process, not marketing. Few people make that distinction and they do that only rarely. SEO can only be a business practice for consultants and agencies; otherwise, it is a technical process that you integrate into your business practices.

The Web spammers, affiliate farmers, and other passive income specialists out there are not practicing search engine optimization although they may be using it. To practice search engine optimization you must make it your primary function. You’ll only be paid for practicing SEO if you are selling your services or hiring yourself out for an SEO position.

Don’t make the mistake of dismissing these points as “semantics”. People fall back on the “semantics” put-down when they don’t understand what is being discussed (and that is proven by the fact that they dismiss an argument as “semantics” because their use of the word differs from the intention behind its actual meaning).

There are secret costs to search engine optimization and if you’re specializing in providing SEO service either as an in-house SEO technician or a consultant/agency, you should understand those secret costs well enough to be able to explain them. You don’t need to justify these costs to anyone. The decision to incur them is a simple (or complex) business decision.

Search engine optimization can only support the business decision — it cannot be the business decision. The business decision has to be to acquire traffic through Web search; the business decision cannot be to use search engine optimization (except as a means to enhance the traffic acquired through Web search).

You could say that the numerous misunderstandings about what SEO is and who is actually doing SEO is the first of many hidden costs. Unless you’re a journalist writing a snappy article that makes cheap shots at the expense of the SEO industry, failing to understand that search engine optimization is a process holds you back, diminishes your chances for success, and increases the costs you incur because you make bad decisions.

So that is the first hidden cost of search engine optimization, but it can well be argued it is a hidden cost of many processes that are misused, misunderstood, unnecessarily feared or avoided, and otherwise treated wrongly by a poorly informed business community. Here are a few other hidden costs.

The Cost of Measurement

It is enough to acknowledge that not everyone agrees on how search engine optimization progress and success should be measured. We don’t need to dwell on which measurements and tools we think are the best or the worst. When SEO productivity is measured the measurements generally fail to provide an accurate picture.

It is the industry’s lack of standards that ensures none of us will ever know which measurements provide the most accurate and complete picture of SEO progress and accomplishment. In my opinion, none of the popular SEO metrics and tools provide anything particularly useful but your opinion may differ. Neither of us can be proven right because there is no objective means of showing that a measurement or tool does what it is intended to do.

If we were to agree to standards of measurement we would at least be able to gauge all measurements of progress and success by a common set of rules. That would be something. And those standards would enable statisticians, economists, mathematicians, and other people with the appropriate training to start carving out the science required to show what the return on investment from SEO measurements should be.

Meanwhile we’re just shooting in the dark and the more we measure the more expensive these measurements become. The time you invest in creating and documenting measurements for SEO progress is expensive. Many a manager has mandated that progress reports and project charts be constructed to ensure that employees are not wasting their time — and, ironically, it is this sort of wheel-spinning nonsense that raises the cost of search engine optimization considerably.

You can set goals and track work done. You cannot reliably predict success for search engine optimization because there is no reliable, scientifically credible method for measuring success in search engine optimization. We can point to the improvement in search referral traffic, conversions, and sales and say that SEO is having an effect — but predictions based on past performance are just black magic formulae that have no relevance to what is actually happening in the Searchable Web Ecosystem.

Change is the only constant we have to work with. The cost of measuring progress and success without taking change into consideration is immense and immensely wasteful.

The Cost of Waiting

Most of us would easily agree that search engine optimization — being a technical process — is not simply a “set it and forget it” activity; yes, there are “set it and forget it strategies” but their optimal performance drops off after some unmeasurable point (and you would be right to ask how I can say such a thing if even I cannot measure progress and success).

Entropy and Change ensure that everything we do will eventually become meaningless and unhelpful in the Searchable Web Ecosystem. We just don’t have any way of predicting how long that counter-process of decay in value will take.

Still, many SEO tasks require an element of patience. You publish your content and wait. You get your links and wait. You wait for Change and Improvement in the Search Results.

Knowing when to wait and when to take action is the key to improving your chances of being successful (in an unmeasurable way). That is, quite often when people are screaming “all hands on deck — I want to see movement! DO SOMETHING!” all that should really be done is to wait. My refusal to take action when search results get choppy has infuriated people on more than one occasion but when you have seen just how powerful a force Change is in the Searchable Web Ecosystem as often as I have, you learn to trust in Patience more than in Movement.

By the very same token, however, there are times when you SHOULD take action and do something — and that is usually when Change has wrought devastating and disruptive effects on carefully laid out “SEO strategies”. Ironically, these are the times when clients and non-SEO managers are most likely to want to hold fast and “wait”. They are hoping that the forces of Change will bring back the Success-that-Was, not understanding (or perhaps refusing to accept) that Change was driven by the need to undermine and rid the system of the Success-that-Was. It’s not coming back no matter how long you wait.

Choosing to wait at the wrong time, and waiting for the wrong reasons, denies a business a serious chunk of revenue. You have to rebuild and rebuild quickly, especially if your competitors have lost ground, too. What should have happened across the Web starting on February 25, 2011 was every downgraded Website should have changed, changed radically, and changed forever. Instead nearly everyone waited and now almost a year later they are still waiting.

The Success-that-Was is not coming back. Stop waiting. It has cost you way too much money.

The Cost of Entropy

I not-so-obliquely referred to this above. Too many decision makers — having no idea of what they are doing — assume that after they have “built the infrastructure” and “learned how to do SEO” they can get along without that expensive SEO agency or that argumentative in-house SEO. A lot of companies have severed ties with the only people who were in a position to defend them against disaster only to realize too late they had shot themselves in the head.

Sure, complacency sets in with agencies and in-house SEOs. After you have won the hard-fought victories and improved search referral traffic you need a chance to relax or deal with other problems. But the odds are better than even that a major search algorithmic event six months down the road will put the Web traffic back in jeopardy. It does depend to a certain extent on how much a company depends on any one search engine. The more dependent they are on a single source of traffic, the more likely disaster strikes once or twice a year.

Such ill-advised business policies cannot be mended by any SEO agency or employee. But these kinds of storms can be foreseen and you can prepare the people you’re supposed to be protecting against the eventual Change that will degrade their performance.

Your links will stop passing value.

Your copy will no longer be as relevant or loved as the new articles being published.

Someone will come out with a lower price than yours.

Consumers will lose interest in the queries you have been depending on for years.

I have dealt with all these situations both personally and professionally. You cannot hold back Change and Decay. The value you create today will slowly erode. That doesn’t mean you need to be constantly “building links”. It means you need to look six months down the road and know what has to happen THEN in order to ensure that there is new value in place for the consumers of six months in the future.

You have to plan to retire old content. You have to allow for the fact that old links will disappear for all sorts of reasons beyond your control.

Dealing with Change and Entropy is more expensive when you allow them to surprise you, but you MUST incorporate strategies for managing search system Change and Entropy into your business plans. These are long-term costs that never go away. That is the only way to mitigate the risks of participating in the ever-volatile search space.

The Cost of Inexpert Wisdom

I not-so-obliquely referred to this above as well. Once people think they have figured out what they need to do they become their own experts. I have participated in many meetings where newly acquired clients shared their SEO wisdom with me. They repeat an endless litany of what was usually mediocre formulaic advice when it was first proposed, but by the time I’m hearing it for the 100th time it’s just bad, outdated advice.

“All we need is links” has been the swan song of many a company that asked me why their search rankings tanked. Your search rankings tanked because you were misled to believe that search engine optimization is all about links and you lacked the expertise (and experience) to understand that is entirely bullshit.

If SEO were really that simple and simple-minded there would never have been a need for an SEO Theory Website. No one would have published any books about search engine optimization.

The client (or the boss) becomes the SEO expert when he sees that “you did THIS and I saw THAT happen — it’s just like MAGIC!” When we have to confront the ridiculous expectations of these pseudo-experts we are inevitably placed on the defensive.

I frankly admit that about half my strategies in professional search engine optimization have been devised solely to outwit the nitwits who thought they understood the SEO process well enough to participate in an expert-level discussion with me. Seriously, if you know it THAT well, you don’t need to talk to me and you’re wasting everyone’s time.

But inexpert wisdom is another great time waster. It leads to many fruitless hours creating stupid SEO projects that have no chance of success. It forces under-empowered employees and consultants to go through the motions of rallying the troops to “get more links”, “write more copy”, “insert more keywords in the title tags”, or whatever formulaic solution the decision-maker has convinced him- or herself is required to change the Changeful System.

In the past I would occasionally allow people to overrule good sense and real SEO wisdom and mandate incredibly wasteful activities for my team, knowing that all we had to do was WAIT or that I would have to patch together a Hail Mary Pass anyway. I have said it before and I’ll say it again: There are no atheists running Websites that are in trouble. They either blame God or demand a miracle from him; either way, they all talk to God.

Inexpert wisdom is hard to distinguish from barely expert wisdom. It’s also darned near impossible to distinguish from utter stupidity because, frankly, even the most expert experts can be wrong, are often wrong, and have learned to learn from being wrong. That may be the most important distinction between a real expert and a faux expert: the real expert expects to find new lessons in whatever Change happens.

The Cost of Not Spamming

If you’re only doing enough to create a Website that is “Not Spam”, then you have to add the cost of future fixes to this year’s budget because you will lose time and revenue when the search engines decide that your “Not Spam” content isn’t the content they are looking for.

In other words, can you say “GOOGLE PANDA UPDATE”? I mean, seriously. Once all the inexpert experts had search engine optimization figured out, they put on their formulaic propeller hats and started churning out templated Websites that were filled with the same homogenous non-speak that seemed to work for them the first time around.

Some people became expert in generating Websites filled with uninformative pseudo-information that populated Websites with boilerplate copy in which they injected their keywords. It all seemed so very clever.

And it was doomed from the start. It should not have required any level of genius to see that Google would eventually react to all the MUSH that was being published in pursuit of search traffic. It was never a matter of IF but of WHEN.

And as it turns out no one had a backup plan for the WHEN. No one had a fallback position, except to produce more Websites according to the same tried-and-true formulas.

Google Panda was a huge slap in the face but unfortunately it was not really a wake up call. Just as soon as the inexpert experts find something else that works they’ll jump right back on that treadmill and run it into the ground.

Google will continue working its Panda magic but eventually that will become less effective (because Change and Entropy work against Google just as much as they work against you and me). And then inexpert experts will settle into creating the need for the next great search algorithmic event.

It’s an endless process and an expensive one because no one looks six months down the road, much less two years. Or ten. By my calculations, based solely on past performance, the next Panda-like update may occur sometime around the year 2020, maybe 2021. Panda was much larger and affected more sites proportionately than the so-called “Florida” update. The last algorithmic upheaval of this magnitude, which forced Web-wide changes in practices, was Altavista’s Black Monday update of October 1999.

You have some time to figure out what Change has wrought upon our Web. But in the coming years, the more we focus on creating real value, the less we’ll need to prepare for the next Black Monday or Panda Algorithm. And the better prepared we’ll be to weather such storms when they do arrive.

Because it’s not a matter of IF but WHEN. Failing to plan for those WHEN events is extremely expensive.
