Hello all!

I blacken brass all the time with the following: make a super-saturated solution of copper carbonate (CuCO3Cu(OH)2) and regular, over-the-counter ammonia (what you use to clean with). The longer you leave the brass part in the solution, the darker it will get! I have to manufacture custom camera bushings and eyepiece inserts all the time - that need to be black to prevent light reflections. This is the same chemical staining procedure used by Zeiss and Leitz(Leica). It produces a finish that replicates black anodizing on aluminum. Try it and post back how well it works!

Hello,
If your are blackening cartridge brass (that you intend to reload) you should stay away from ammonia. It causes "stess corrosion cracking". Unfortunately, I don't remember the chemical expanation, but I think it reacts with the zinc in the alloy. The upshot is that the brass will end up with microscopic cracks and will not have the strength to properly seal the chamber during firing.

I remember a common crack initiation site was on the corners of primers. This area was stressed from the manufacturing process and was more susceptible to cracking with exposure to ammonia. Just the fumes will do it.

Of course, black brass is commonly used to make "dummy" cartridges for training purposes. In this case, the stess corrosion cracking problem shouldn't be an issue. Drill a hole into the side of the brass case to ensure it stays a "dummy".

Best wishes,

John

Hello all!

Just a few follow-up comments regarding my copper carbonate solution. To make a "super-saturated" solution of anything, just keep adding solute until the solvent won't take any more. EXAMPLE from down south - here in good'ole NC - I wanna make a super-saturated glass of sweetened iced tea (I know you Northerners won't know what I'm talking about, but I still luv you)......I start adding sugar and stirring......the sugar dissolves so I add some more. At a certain point, the tea (or solvent) won't hold any more sugar (or solute) and no degree or stirring makes any difference - the tea, at this point, has become super-saturated and VERY DELICIOUS!

Also, regarding steel bluing comment above. As I previously mentioned, I am a fine-mechanic and repair microscopes and related instrumentation. Very rarely I will have to machine internal components to duplicate damaged, blued steel parts......the Japanese used to put many blued nuts and screws in fine-focus mechanisms - steel for strength and blued for stability and a certain degree of corrosion protection. The trick my old German master taught me was heat up some heavy oil just under its smoking point and drop the parts in like fondue. This blackens very nicely. We had a Deckel FP1 that we used Vactra on and I had an old metal lid off a shoe polish can and heated the oil on a hotplate for the best (and safest) control over the heat. Most oils have a high heat of combustion and because we were using such piddly amounts of material, I've never been very concerned about it catching fire.
